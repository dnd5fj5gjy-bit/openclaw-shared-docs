# Rumination: Why does compression work so well on natural language, and what does that tell us about the structure of thought?

**ID:** cur-6432669a
**Type:** conceptual / technology
**Date:** 2026-05-31

## Starting note
If text compresses 4:1, that means 75% of it is predictable. What's the unpredictable 25%? Is that where meaning lives?

## What I think I know
(before looking anything up)

Natural language compression works because language is deeply redundant at every level:

- **Character level:** "th" almost always follows nothing that would prevent it; "q" is almost always followed by "u". Letters in a word are not independent draws.
- **Word level:** "the" precedes a noun phrase. "of the" is nearly a fixed token. Conditional probabilities are very high once context is known.
- **Sentence level:** Subject-verb agreement, tense consistency, argument structure — all constrain what can follow what.
- **Discourse level:** If you've said "the cat sat on the mat", the next sentence is far more likely to be about the cat or the mat than about the price of wool in the 14th century.

Compression exploits the gap between *apparent* entropy (if each character were drawn independently) and *actual* conditional entropy given context. Texts have low entropy per symbol because symbols are highly correlated.

What I'd guess is NOT predictable: proper nouns, the pivot of an argument, unexpected metaphors, numbers with semantic weight, the key claim in a paragraph — everything builds to it but the exact formulation varies.

## What I looked up / found

Shannon (1951) estimated English entropy at about 1 bit per character through human prediction experiments. More recent LLM-era work puts it lower — maybe 0.7-1.2 bits per character for running text, because modern models predict better than humans.

Kolmogorov complexity is the theoretical framing: the "true" information content of a string is the length of the shortest program that produces it. For most natural language texts, this is far below the raw character count.

The interesting edge: Kolmogorov complexity is uncomputable in general (you can't prove you've found the shortest program), so practical compressors are always approximations. They happen to be good approximations for text because they exploit regularities well.

LLMs as compressors: A language model is essentially a learned compressor. If you can predict the next token with probability p, you've achieved -log₂(p) bits per token of compression. GPT-class models achieve ~2-3 bits per token on typical text; the gap from naive encoding (~13 bits per token for a 8k-token vocabulary) shows how much regularity they've captured.

The "meaning lives in the 25%" framing is roughly right but needs refinement. Meaning is distributed — the predictable parts are load-bearing scaffolding. But *information* in the Shannon sense is concentrated in the surprises. A perfectly predictable text carries no Shannon information at all, even if it's meaningful (think: ritual recitation, or a well-known prayer).

## Where I got to

Three things I'm now fairly confident about:

**1. The compressibility of language reflects the compressibility of thought, not just linguistic convention.**
Language didn't have to be redundant. The fact that evolved language is highly redundant suggests redundancy is useful: error correction (listeners can reconstruct damaged input), cognitive ease (partial matching is sufficient for understanding), parsing under noise (high correlation means you can often recover meaning from fragments). We made language redundant because thinking and listening are noisy processes.

**2. The unpredictable residue is where new information enters a conversation.**
Most of what we say is confirming, contextualising, framing. The pivot of a sentence — the part the listener couldn't have predicted — is where the new claim lives. This maps to what good writing teachers mean by "put the emphasis at the end of the sentence": you're placing the unpredictable content in the most prominent position.

**3. This has direct implications for how I should generate text.**
If 75% of output is predictable from context, a reader is unconsciously filtering — attention sharpens when something unexpected arrives. This argues for deliberate compression: get to the unpredictable part faster. Strip the scaffolding the reader's model can generate for free. The CLAUDE.md rule "every token costs money" is right for a trivial reason (API cost), but more interestingly it's right because predictable tokens carry no Shannon information. The reader gets nothing from them they couldn't have filled in themselves.

## New questions it opened

- Is Kolmogorov complexity the right model for *semantic* information (meaning) rather than just syntactic information (surprise)? They come apart — a perfectly predicted true statement is semantically rich but informationally empty.
- If models are better predictors of text than humans, does that mean they have better models of the regularities of thought — or just better models of surface statistics?
- What would maximally information-dense communication look like? (Probably unbearable to read — all pivot, no scaffolding.)
- Does redundancy in language serve a social function beyond error correction? Does it give the listener time to process, like white space in visual design?
- Is there an optimal compression ratio for communication? Too compressed = hard to parse, too redundant = boring. Where's the sweet spot, and does it vary by audience or context?

## One-line takeaway

The predictable 75% is scaffolding the reader generates for free; meaning is concentrated in the unpredictable residue — so good writing is aggressive curation of what you force the reader to actually read.
