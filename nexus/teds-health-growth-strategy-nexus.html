My Read on the Situation

The UK private TRT market is genuinely large and demand-rich right now. NHS waiting times for testosterone assessment are 6-12 months in most trusts, and NHS eligibility thresholds are so restrictive (<8 nmol/L total testosterone) that a huge proportion of symptomatic men are simply told "not yet." Private clinics operate at <15 nmol/L. That gap is doing a lot of work.

The market has around 2 million men in the UK with probable testosterone deficiency. Roughly 1% are currently diagnosed and treated. That is not a market with a demand problem. It's a market with an awareness and friction problem.

Ted's needs 128 more patients. At current market dynamics, the question isn't whether those patients exist — they absolutely do. The question is which acquisition channel reaches them fastest at an acceptable cost.

Honest assessment of the content I built last week: The four Ted's Health blog posts are good — well-targeted, correct search intent, useful content. But organic SEO takes 3-6 months to show meaningful traffic. If the goal is 225 patients by a specific date, SEO alone won't get you there. It's an investment in the pipeline, not a tap you can turn on.

What I'd Prioritize First

Priority 1 — Start This Week

Test paid search (Google Ads) on 3 high-intent keywords

The UK private clinic benchmark CAC via paid search is around £34. At Ted's pricing — let's assume £150/month ongoing — that's an LTV/CAC ratio that would make any growth-stage startup weep with joy. Even if Ted's actual CAC runs 3-4x higher than the benchmark (call it £100-120), acquiring 128 patients at £100 = £12,800 in total ad spend to reach breakeven. One month of breakeven revenue will cover the entire acquisition cost.

The three keywords I'd test first:

  • "TRT clinic [Ted's geographic area]" — local commercial intent, high conversion probability
  • "private testosterone therapy UK" — national, high intent, competitive but targetable with good landing page
  • "low testosterone treatment private" — symptom-aware buyer, mid-funnel

Important: paid search only works if the landing page converts. Before spending money, the Ted's Health consultation booking page needs a single clear CTA, social proof (even 3-4 patient testimonials), and the core "why private, why now" message. If that's not in place, fix it first.

Priority 2 — Medium Term (1-3 Months)

Build a GP referral network — the channel nobody else is working

This is the one I'm most excited about. Every private TRT clinic in the UK is fighting for the same digital audience — the man who already knows he might have low testosterone and is searching Google. The market for that man is increasingly expensive and competitive.

There's an entirely different channel that's barely been touched: GPs.

A GP who suspects a patient has testosterone deficiency faces a real problem: refer to NHS endocrinology and the patient waits 9-12 months. The patient comes back frustrated. The GP looks unhelpful. If that GP has a trusted private clinic they can confidently refer to — with good clinical credibility, transparent pricing, and a feedback loop back to the GP — they will use it. Repeatedly.

One GP who refers 5 patients a month is worth more than most Google Ads campaigns. And it compounds. GPs talk to each other. Good clinical outcomes build reputation. This is how you build a moat that competitors can't buy their way around.

What a GP referral programme needs:

  • A one-page clinical brief: who Ted's treats, what the process looks like, what the GP should tell patients
  • A patient-facing referral letter template the GP can personalise in 30 seconds
  • A summary report sent back to the GP after each patient's initial consultation (GPs hate referring into black boxes)
  • A named contact at Ted's that GPs can call if they have questions

Target geography: every GP practice within 15 miles of Ted's Health's clinic. Personal outreach — not cold email, but a letter from Ted's clinical director to the practice manager, followed up by phone.

Priority 3 — Ongoing

Convert the existing pipeline harder — most clinics leave this on the table

Before spending heavily on acquisition, it's worth asking: of the people who've already expressed interest in Ted's Health — booked a consultation, requested information, started the sign-up flow — how many didn't convert? What happened to them?

In private healthcare, the gap between "interested" and "patient" is often a timing or friction issue, not a decision issue. Someone who googled TRT, found Ted's, clicked around, but didn't book is probably still going to need treatment. They just weren't ready that week. A simple 3-email nurture sequence — sent over 2 weeks to people who showed interest but didn't book — consistently recovers 10-20% of that unconverted audience. That's free patients.

What I'd Be Careful About

Don't try to compete with Optimale on price. Optimale is at £59.99/month and clearly struggling with service quality at that margin (2026 reviews show communication failures, medication delays). That race ends badly. Ted's should position at £150+/month and compete on outcomes and personal care, not cost. The patients who choose on price alone will also leave on price.
  • Social media campaigns as primary acquisition — awareness play, not conversion. Expensive relative to paid search for actual bookings.
  • Broad SEO without geographic specificity — "TRT UK" is being fought for by everyone. Local SEO ("TRT [city]") is achievable and converts better anyway.
  • Chasing industry articles and PR before the fundamentals are sorted — if someone reads a great article about Ted's Health and the website or booking process is weak, that opportunity is wasted.

If there's a way to surface that BGV connection with integrity — not as marketing fluff, but as a genuine signal of quality and seriousness — that's worth more than most ad campaigns. It immediately differentiates Ted's Health from the commodity providers.

The Numbers Case for Acting Now

Conservative scenario: Ted's launches Google Ads targeting 3 keywords. Blended CAC runs £100 (3x the benchmark — being conservative). Converts 50 patients via paid, 30 via GP referrals over 3 months, 48 via organic/conversion optimisation. That's 128 patients — breakeven. Total paid ad spend: £5,000. Monthly revenue at breakeven: ~£33,750 (225 x £150). Payback on the £5,000: under 2 weeks of breakeven revenue.

The math is very favourable. The risk is operational — can Ted's handle the increased patient volume with good outcomes and service quality? That's the constraint worth thinking hard about. Growth that outpaces delivery capability will generate the kind of bad reviews that Optimale is currently dealing with.

Summary

Paid search first — it's the fastest path and the economics work. GP referrals second — it's the strategic play that builds a moat. Conversion optimisation running in parallel — it's the cheapest wins. SEO (including the content pack I built) is the slow burn that pays off over 6-12 months.

The 225 number isn't out of reach. The market conditions are genuinely favourable. The bottleneck is execution speed, not demand.
