1. The three structural gaps that matter

Gap 1 — Symptom-specific landing pages (Arc: 9, Ted's: 0)

Arc has built one dedicated landing page per low-T symptom. Each ranks for its own long-tail term and is a clean ad destination.

/trt-symptoms/brain-fog, /trt-symptoms/muscle-mass, /trt-symptoms/energy-levels, /trt-symptoms/sex-drive, /trt-symptoms/erectile-issues, /trt-symptoms/anxiety, /trt-symptoms/sleep-quality, /trt-symptoms/weight-gain, /trt-symptoms/low-mood

Why it matters. Search behaviour for low-T is symptom-led, not condition-led. Most men type "why am I always tired" or "can't get hard at 40" long before they type "low testosterone treatment". Arc captures that intent layer; Ted's funnels everyone through one generic /testosterone-replacement-therapy page. Each symptom page also halves Arc's paid CPCs — high quality score, perfectly matched landing copy.

What Ted's has instead. One page for erectile dysfunction (/erectile-dysfunction). That's it. The blog covers some symptoms editorially, but blog posts are not built to convert, don't show in shopping-style intent searches, and aren't ad destinations.

Gap 2 — Individual clinician profile pages (Arc: 4, Ted's: 0)

Arc has a /team hub and one URL per clinician: Dr Chris Airey, Dr Lauren Airey, Julia, Ursh. Each is indexed; each is a trust signal in its own right.

Why it matters. Doctor-led trust is the entire moat in private TRT. Patients Google the doctor's name before they book. If Ted's clinicians don't have their own pages, the "is this person legit?" search hits LinkedIn, third-party review sites, or nothing — Ted's loses the framing. Arc's Dr Chris Airey page is also a wedge for ex-Optimale / ex-Manual brand equity, which the May 27 brief flagged as their core differentiator.

What Ted's has instead. /sir-steve-redgrave — a celebrity ambassador page. Celebrity trust is real but it's a different vector. It doesn't answer "is the person prescribing me testosterone qualified?". You need both.

Gap 3 — Transparent pricing + dedicated conversion page

Arc has a top-level /pricing page and a /get-started page. Ted's has /trt-costs, /erectile-dysfunction-treatment-cost, /mens-health-supplements-cost — cost-by-product, but no single "here's what it costs" hub, and the conversion CTA is /how-it-works (informational), not /get-started (transactional).

Why it matters. The "how much is TRT" search is one of the top three commercial queries in this category. A single pricing page wins that query; three fragmented cost pages dilute it. /get-started is also the URL ads platforms will route conversion campaigns to — cleaner attribution, cleaner remarketing audience.

2. Ted's moats Arc can't easily copy

Moat 1 — Long-form blog (Ted's: 10 posts, Arc: ~0)

Ted's has ten substantive blog posts on TRT, blood tests, NHS vs private, normal testosterone by age, male menopause, diet, etc. Arc's /blog page exists but is empty of unique content (Arc's "articles" are short copy on /articles/* not a publishing engine).

Implication. Ted's has organic-search compounding that Arc does not. This is a 6-12 month moat if Ted's keeps publishing weekly. Do not stop the blog while building out the symptom landing pages — they're complementary, not substitutes.

Moat 2 — BGV portfolio integration

Ted's has dedicated pages for supplements (/supplements), GP partnerships (/partner-with-us), tools (/mens-heath-tools), patient stories (/patient-success-stories), and the Sir Steve Redgrave ambassador integration. Arc has none of these portfolio levers.

Implication. Ted's is a clinic and a brand inside a larger ecosystem. Arc is just a clinic. Lean into this when Arc closes the symptom-page gap — their next move is hard, Ted's next move (publishing Steve Redgrave's TRT journey, for example) is trivial and reuses owned assets.

Moat 3 — GP referral pathway

Ted's has /partner-with-us and /referrals/test. Arc has nothing equivalent. The GP-trust moat thesis from the May 11 brief and May 22 decision journal is still load-bearing — Arc has not entered that channel.

4. What we did NOT build (and why)

Three items Arc has that Ted's deliberately should not chase:

  • Genetic testing positioning (/genetic-testing) — thin clinical evidence base, regulatory risk for a UK clinic. Skip unless evidence improves.
  • Three-tier blood test product pages (Arc: initial / enhanced / ultimate) — possibly worth doing if Ted's has tiered tests, but only if the pricing actually differs in a way patients can self-select on. Otherwise pure decision fatigue.
  • Urban TRT / brand alias page (/urban-trt) — Arc carries this from a previous identity. Ted's doesn't have a legacy brand to redirect, so this is N/A.

5. Methodology & caveats

Method: sitemap-driven crawl of both sites (31 vs 38 pages indexed), single-page parse, regex topic classification, URL-slug diff. Built with workspace/tools/content_gap.py — reusable for any future BGV competitive analysis (Modern Savage vs AG1, Be Military Fit vs Barry's, etc.).

Caveats: (1) crawl is sitemap-only — pages Arc or Ted's didn't list in their sitemap are missed; (2) classifier is keyword-based, so a few "both cover" calls may be coarse — the URL-slug analysis above is the authoritative gap signal; (3) content quality not assessed — presence-of-page vs depth-of-page is a separate audit.
