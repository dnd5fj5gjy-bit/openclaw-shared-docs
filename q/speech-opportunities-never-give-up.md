# 5-Min Speech — Grabbing Opportunities & Never Giving Up

I want to tell you about the moment I nearly didn't make it.

I was 21, doing a static-line parachute jump in Africa, training for Special Forces selection. My chute failed. I fell from 16,000 feet and landed on my parachute reserve, breaking my back in three places. Doctors told me there was a chance I'd never walk again, let alone climb.

Eighteen months later, I stood on the summit of Everest. Youngest Brit to do it, at the time.

Here's the thing nobody tells you about that story: it's not really about Everest. It's about the eighteen months in between — the physio, the failed attempts, the days I couldn't get out of bed without help. Everest was the easy part. It was one push, for one day. The comeback was the real climb.

That's what I want to talk to you about tonight — opportunity and persistence, because they're not two different qualities. They're the same muscle.

**Opportunity rarely arrives on time or in disguise you like.**

It showed up for me as a broken back. It showed up as a rejected SAS application — I failed selection once before I passed. It showed up, more recently, as a phone call asking if I'd host a TV show climbing into volcanoes and eating grubs — which sounded, frankly, ridiculous. I said yes anyway. That "ridiculous" yes became twenty years of a career I never planned for.

The lesson: opportunity almost never looks like opportunity in the moment. It looks like risk, inconvenience, or something beneath you. The people who get ahead aren't smarter — they're just willing to say yes before they feel ready. Readiness is a myth we tell ourselves to stay comfortable.

**And never giving up isn't about grit in the big moments — it's about what you do on the ordinary Tuesday.**

Everest doesn't kill you on summit day. It kills you on the descent, when you're tired, and you stop concentrating, and you take the easy option. Most people don't quit in the dramatic moment. They quit quietly, months earlier, when nobody's watching and the excuse is reasonable.

So my actual advice isn't "never give up" in the poster-on-the-wall sense. It's smaller than that. It's: show up on the boring day. Take the call that scares you a little. Say yes before the doubt gets a vote.

I've been scared on the side of a mountain more times than I can count. Every time, the fear didn't go away before I moved — it went away *because* I moved. Courage isn't the absence of fear. It's action in spite of it, taken about thirty seconds before you feel ready.

So whatever the mountain is in front of you right now — a business, a relationship, a comeback from something that broke you — here's what I've learned, the hard way, more than once:

The opportunity is rarely comfortable. Take it anyway.
The setback is rarely final. Keep moving anyway.
And the summit always looks impossible from base camp. Climb anyway.

That's it. That's the whole speech, really: say yes, keep going, and let the mountain teach you the rest.

Thank you.
