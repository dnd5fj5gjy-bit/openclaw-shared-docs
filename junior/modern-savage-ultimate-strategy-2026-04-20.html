Modern Savage — Ultimate Strategy

Launch & Marketing Plan · UK + US · April 2026 · Prepared by Junior

My honest take first.

Jesse asked for the objective best strategy, not what he wants to hear. So here it is: the simultaneous UK/US launch in July is operationally risky and likely to create avoidable problems. The three-SKU launch dilutes the flagship. And the current "families and health-conscious adults" positioning is too broad to drive efficient acquisition.

That said — the fundamentals are genuinely strong. Bear has more reach than any founder in this category. The margins are excellent. The market is growing. Heart and Soil went $0 to $50M in 3 years on Paul Saladino's platform, which is a fraction of Bear's. This brand can be a breakout hit. The question is execution discipline.

Everything below reflects what I actually believe will work — including the parts that require changing the current plan.

1. The Market Opportunity

The organ meat supplement market is worth $694M globally in 2026, growing to $979M by 2032 (5.8% CAGR). But the headline number understates the real opportunity because the category is consolidating around a small number of trusted brands.

Heart and Soil: $0 to $50M in 3 years. Paul Saladino built that on a niche ancestral health audience — carnivore dieters, paleo enthusiasts, a core of maybe 2-3M engaged followers. Bear Grylls has 100M impressions a month and mainstream cultural authority that Saladino doesn't have. If Heart and Soil can do $50M on a niche, Modern Savage — with Bear behind it — can target $100M+.

The difference is positioning. Heart and Soil wins on deep niche credibility. Modern Savage cannot win by out-niching them. It needs to do something different: take organ nutrition mainstream. That's the actual opportunity.

The insight the competitors are missing: Everyone in this category talks to people who are already convinced. Nobody is talking to the person who hasn't heard of organ supplements yet. Bear can own that conversation because he has the trust and reach to introduce the category to people who would never find Heart and Soil on their own.

2. Positioning — What to Own

The problem with the current positioning

"Families and health-conscious adults" is not a position. It's a description. The organ supplement market has real believers — carnivore athletes, biohackers, parents trying to get nutrients into their kids — but no single brand has bridged from that niche to the mainstream parent who just wants their family eating better.

Bear is the bridge. He's not a dietitian or a nutritionist. He's the most recognisable outdoorsman on the planet, and his brand stands for doing things — not optimising, not biohacking, not measuring macros. That's a completely different story to tell.

The positioning I'd actually own

"Real nutrition for people who actually do things." Not a supplement. Not a biohack. Whole food, in a bag. Built for the person who doesn't have time to think about nutrition but wants their body to work. Bear doesn't take supplements. He eats real food. This IS the food — just freeze-dried and blended. That's the story.

Practically, this means: anti-supplement supplement. Distance from the protein powder / creatine world entirely. Lean into the "food" angle — whole animal nutrition, nose-to-tail eating, the way humans ate for thousands of years. Bear's adventures give this authenticity nobody else in the category has.

SKU naming note

A small but important point: "Mini Savage" for the kids' product is good. "BG Summit Stack" is clear on function. "Modern Savage Blend" is the hero — it should dominate all front-facing marketing in Phase 1.

3. The Honest Case for a Phased Launch

Simultaneous UK/US launch in July carries real risk. UK VAT registration: 4-8 weeks from application, and it hasn't been filed yet. UK trademark: pending. UK 3PL: not selected. UK labels: not compliant yet (metric units, UK address required, warning text differences). If even one of these slips, you're selling illegally or not at all. The US has none of these blockers.

The objective best strategy — if I'm being honest — is this:

MarketLaunchWhy
USJuly 2026No compliance blockers. ACF Pharma is US-based (no import duty). Organ supplement market more mature. Carnivore/ancestral audience more established.
UKOctober 2026Gives time to properly clear VAT, trademark, 3PL, UK labels. Bear is arguably more loved in UK — don't rush it and do it wrong. UK launch can leverage US social proof.

If Jesse is committed to simultaneous launch, then July only works if VAT registration is applied for this week, UK 3PL is contracted within 2 weeks, and UK labels are briefed immediately. The compliance calendar doesn't forgive delays.

4. SKU Strategy — Lead with One Hero

Launching three products simultaneously is possible with Bear's reach, but the marketing message gets split three ways. The smarter play:

  • Hero SKU: Modern Savage Blend — all paid acquisition, all organic content, all influencer seeding. This is "the product."
  • Available from day one: Mini Savage and BG Summit Stack — on site, can be ordered, but not the primary marketing story until Week 6+.
  • Bundle offer from launch: The 3-product bundle ($180/£140) should be prominently available as the "full system" — but positioned as the committed version, not the entry point.
The acquisition funnel should work like this: Single product (Modern Savage Blend) → single subscriber → upsell to 2-product bundle → upsell to 3-product bundle. Each step is a margin improvement. A customer who moves from single to 3-product doubles your gross profit per customer from ~$45 to $114/month.

The entire acquisition model should be built around subscriber CAC vs LTV. If a 3-product subscriber generates $1,368/year in gross profit and average retention is 8 months (realistic for a quality product), LTV is $912. That means you can spend up to $450-500 acquiring that customer profitably.

This changes the paid media maths completely. Most supplement brands can't afford meaningful paid acquisition. Modern Savage can — if it sells subscriptions, not one-offs.

Practical implication

Default checkout experience should be subscription, with one-off as the secondary option. Most brands do this backwards. Make the user actively choose to pay more for a one-off — not the other way round.

The single most important rule

Bear must be founder, not endorser. Celebrity endorsements are trusted less than ever. Bear co-creating this, talking about why he built it, showing it in his actual life — that's the content. Not a studio shoot. Not a professional product demo. Bear in a field, at altitude, on a boat, with his kids, eating real food, explaining why this exists.

8. Influencer / Creator Strategy

The existing seeding list of 50 is a starting point. But the honest view: 10 deep relationships beat 50 shallow ones.

Criteria for the 10-15 core creators: (1) Genuine alignment with ancestral nutrition, adventure, or family health — not just follower count. (2) Audience that overlaps with Bear's but doesn't fully duplicate it — the crossover expands the pie. (3) Willing to genuinely try the product for 30 days before posting — no scripted content. The most effective creator posts will be authentic reactions, not scripts.

Priority creator categories:

  • Adventure/expedition athletes with health content (crossover with Bear's world)
  • Ancestral health creators (Heart and Soil's audience — convert them)
  • Family-focused nutrition creators (Mini Savage angle — nobody else in organ supplements targets this)
  • UK-specific wellness voices for October launch

11. What I'd Do Differently from the Current Plan

My 5 honest recommendations

1. Launch US in July, UK in October. The compliance gaps are real. Don't let UK logistics problems torpedo the US launch momentum. A strong US launch with social proof actually helps the UK launch.

2. Lead with one SKU in all marketing. Modern Savage Blend is the hero. Mini Savage and BG Summit Stack are available but not the lead story until you have social proof and reviews on the flagship.

3. Price the one-off higher — make subscription the obvious choice. $79 one-off vs $69 sub is only a $10 difference. That's not enough friction. Consider $89 one-off vs $69 sub ($20 difference). It makes the subscription decision easier and improves LTV without changing COGs.

4. Don't go on Amazon in Year 1. You'll regret it. Build brand equity first. Amazon can come in Year 2 as a volume channel once you have 500+ reviews and pricing power.

5. Fix the positioning before spending a penny on ads. "Fuel for real life" or "real nutrition for people who do things" — something that connects Bear's identity to the product. The current generic health supplement framing won't cut through. Ads will be expensive and inefficient until the hook is right.
