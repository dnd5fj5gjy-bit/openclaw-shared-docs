# Water2 / Craig Robinson — where this stands and how to save it

20 July 2026. Prepared for Jesse Grylls.
Sources: the "Craig/CJ Intro Call" thread, 22 June to 19 July.

## The short version

Craig's email of 19 July is a refusal dressed as reassurance, and it ends with a goodbye. He has declined the only change that matters, offered a redefinition that does not touch the actual problem, quoted Bear's own emotional plea back at him, and signed off "Goodbye and God bless."

The real deadline is not the share purchase. **It is Craig leaving on 31 July.** He is the only person who will fix the royalty wording out of goodwill. After he retires there is nobody left to ask, and the agreement survives him.

Both sides are right, which is why it is stuck. Nobody has yet put the structure on the table that solves both concerns at once. That structure exists and it is not complicated.

## 1. What is actually being argued

Two things are tangled together and should not be.

**The royalty.** Bear has 10% of "Net Profit" under the second Licence Agreement, in exchange for his NIL. Net Profit is calculated after operating costs, including advertising and marketing. Bear's position, stated plainly on 19 July: *"If the company can run my royalty to zero through advertising then the royalty is meaningless. You might not do it but a buyer could."*

**The shares.** Craig retires 31 July, his 68th birthday. He and Charles want to sell Bear enough to take him from 5% to 10 or 15%. Investors came in at a £60M valuation. There is a competing Irish buyer. Craig says he took expensive legal advice to make the sale possible under the Articles and is bracing for a fight at the September investor board meeting.

## 2. Why it has stalled

**Craig's proposed fix does not address the concern, and may not be intended to.** His redefinition of 14 July reads: *"Net profit shall mean operating profit before Depreciation, Bank Interest, Bank Revaluations, Realised Currency Gains, Unrealised Currency Gains, Corporation Tax Charge, Deferred Taxation movement and Year End Adjustments."*

Every item on that list sits below the line that matters. Not one of them is advertising or marketing. It is a list of accounting adjustments that were never the issue, presented as a concession. CJ spotted this on 15 July and said so politely. Craig's 19 July reply confirms it was deliberate: he will not exclude marketing, and calls doing so "commercial suicide."

**On the substance, Craig is not wrong.** For a D2C consumer brand, performance marketing genuinely is a cost of revenue rather than an overhead. A royalty on profit calculated *before* marketing would pay Bear even when the company loses money on every unit sold. No operator signs that, and Craig is right to say so.

**But Bear is not wrong either, and Craig has never engaged with his actual point.** Bear's fear is not that Craig and Charles will game it. It is that the agreement outlives them. Craig's answer each time is a personal assurance ("we have never and would never"), which is precisely the assurance that becomes worthless the day he retires. CJ made this exact point on 15 July: *"our concern is really directed at the Agreement itself and extends beyond the current ownership."* It has not been answered once.

So both are correct, they are talking past each other, and the gap between them is a drafting problem being conducted as a test of friendship.

## 3. The two things in Craig's last email that should concern you

**He used Bear's vulnerability as leverage.** Bear wrote, on 15 July: *"Please help me in return here. I will learn to love again!!!"* Craig quoted that line back at him on 19 July, under the heading "Sale of Shares," as the reason he and Charlie were prepared to sell below market. That is a negotiating move, and a fairly cold one. It tells you Bear should not be carrying this conversation personally any longer.

Plus an unsolicited passage about Bear being 52, having "used up every bit of luck," and needing to stop risking his life. Whatever the intent, the effect is a man closing a door and leaving the royalty exactly as it was.

## 4. What to actually propose

The single most sellable idea, and I would lead with it:

**A change of control trigger.** Craig keeps saying he and Charles would never do it. Take him at his word and make it binding only when it is no longer him. On a change of control, the royalty definition converts automatically: from 10% of Net Profit to a fixed percentage of net revenue.

This is very hard for him to refuse. It costs him and Charles nothing while they run the business, which is exactly the ground he has been defending. It answers Bear's stated fear precisely. And it reframes the conversation from "we do not trust you" to "we trust you, so let us write down what happens when it is not you."

Supporting terms, in order of importance:

1. **A floor.** Bear receives the greater of 10% of Net Profit or a small percentage of net revenue. Even 1% to 1.5% of net revenue guarantees the royalty can never be engineered to zero, while leaving Craig's accounting principle intact. This is the cleanest single fix if only one thing gets agreed.
2. **Anti-avoidance wording.** Marketing deductible only where incurred on arm's length terms with unconnected parties, with no allocation of group or parent overhead not directly attributable to Water2. Blocks the load-up scenario without touching normal trading.
3. **Audit rights.** An annual right to inspect the marketing allocation. Cheap to give, and its existence is most of the deterrent.
4. **The equity swap Bear floated.** Converting the royalty to equity solves it permanently and aligns everyone. But it is a valuation negotiation requiring investor consent, and Craig is already expecting a board fight in September over the share sale. Realistically this cannot be done by 31 July. Keep it as the fallback if the wording cannot be agreed.

## 5. How to run it from here

**Separate the royalty from the share purchase, explicitly and in writing.** They are currently entangled, and the entanglement favours Craig: the 31 July share deadline creates urgency that pushes Bear toward accepting weak royalty wording. Say plainly that the share purchase can proceed or not on its own merits, and the royalty wording is a separate matter. Removing the deadline pressure from the royalty is worth more than anything else here.

**Take Bear out of the negotiation.** His last three emails have been emotionally exposed, and the most recent one was quoted back at him. That is not a criticism of Bear, it is what happens when a principal negotiates a document with a friend. CJ and counsel should carry it. Bear's role from here is warmth and relationship, nothing operational.

**Put drafted wording on the table rather than describing the problem.** Every email so far has been BGV explaining the concern and Craig responding with reassurance. Nobody on our side has sent actual clause language. Send the change of control clause and the floor as drafted text, so the conversation becomes "amend this" instead of "reassure me."

**Get it done before 31 July, and treat the shares as the flexible piece.** If the share purchase slips past Craig's retirement, that is survivable. If the royalty is still loose when he leaves, it is permanent, and the next person to hold his seat has no relationship with Bear and no reason to help.

## 6. The scenario to avoid

Craig retires 31 July. The shares go to the Irish buyer instead. The company sells within five years, as Craig predicts. Bear holds 5%, and a royalty whose definition lets the new owner spend it to nothing. That is the worst available outcome and it is currently the default path.
