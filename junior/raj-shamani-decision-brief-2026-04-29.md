## The Problems

Bollywood legend Anupam Kher publicly called Shamani "fake" after appearing on the show. Kher said Shamani edited out an entire section of advice Kher gave — specifically the parts about humility and substance — leaving only the inflammatory bits. Shamani's reply: silence. No correction, no retraction.

This is the one that matters for Bear. If Shamani edits Bear's content to generate controversy — cuts the nuance, keeps the provocation — Bear has no recourse. Bear's brand is built on authenticity. Edited-for-controversy clips live forever.

**2. Dettol lawsuit (April 2025) — medical claims**
Reckitt Benckiser (Dettol) sued Shamani over medical claims made in a podcast episode. Both parties agreed to remove the content under a Delhi High Court order. This shows Shamani either doesn't vet guest claims adequately or allows content to run that he shouldn't. Relevant for Bear if they discuss Modern Savage, Ted's Health, or any health product.

**3. Vijay Mallya interview (June 2025) — guest vetting**
Shamani ran a 4.3-hour episode with Vijay Mallya (Indian billionaire fugitive, wanted for £1.1B bank fraud, living in the UK to avoid extradition). 20M views. Heavily criticised for providing a platform without journalistic scrutiny. Shows Shamani prioritises reach over due diligence.

## The Judgment Call

**For:** 15M subscribers, 400M views, India market access, legitimate high-profile guests (Macron, Gates are not fools about where they appear).

**Against:** Selective editing with no recourse, lawsuit track record on health claims, willingness to platform controversial figures for views. Em Lovell — who tracks Bear's media and manages his schedule — flagged it with a caution. That matters.

**The real risk:** Bear spends 2 hours in a thoughtful interview. Shamani edits it to 12 contentious clips. They go viral in India. Bear has no approval rights over the edited version. Bear's PR team (Alice Driscoll / ConsultingPartners) spends a week firefighting.
