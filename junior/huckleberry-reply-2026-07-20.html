Good first pass, and quicker than I expected. Before anything else, one thing in there is much better than you seem to realise.

You spotted that the trust ranking and the purchase-intent ranking do not match. Creators lead on trust at 41% but fall to 14% on purchase intent, while media outlets go the other way, 25% up to 35%. You wrote that down and then moved past it in a sentence. That is the most interesting thing in your whole email. It says the people who build belief are not the people who close the sale, which means a funnel that uses one voice throughout is going to leak somewhere. Most marketers never separate those two jobs. Go back to that and push on it, because if you can work out where the handover happens you have something genuinely useful rather than a list of tactics.

Now the part you could not have known, and it changes your question.

Modern Savage has not launched. There are no past campaigns to audit. The site is a coming-soon page building a waitlist, three products listed as unavailable, from $69 a month on subscription: the adult blend, Mini Savage for kids, and the BG Summit Stack. The Instagram is @modernsavagenutrition. So when you asked me to assess what Modern Savage has done well and badly, the honest answer is that it has not done much yet.

That is better news than it sounds. Auditing someone else's old campaigns is the easy version of this job and the team would only half-listen. Getting to shape the launch is the hard version, and nobody has a stronger claim on it than the person who can see the thing cold. So the question I would put back to you is this: the waitlist page and the Instagram are the entire marketing surface that exists today. What is wrong with them, and what would you replace them with? That is real, it is unclaimed, and it is answerable by Friday.

Two challenges on the work itself, meant as a colleague rather than a marker.

Where are your sources? The 81% figure, the 40% on over-polished ads, the trust percentages. I believe you, but in a room with Jesse and the marketing leads the first question will be where the number came from and how big the sample was. An unsourced statistic is worth less than no statistic, because it invites someone to doubt everything else you said. Put the source next to every figure from now on. It takes two minutes and it is the difference between being listened to and being humoured.

Be precise about who you are researching. You have gathered data on how young people respond to advertising. Modern Savage sells a $69 a month organ supplement and a children's formula. The person paying for that is not an eighteen-year-old. So decide deliberately: are you researching the buyer, or are you researching the culture the buyer is downstream of? Both are legitimate and the second is arguably your real edge, but if you do not name which one you are doing, someone will point out the gap before you get to your conclusion. Name it first and it becomes a strength.

What I would do with the rest of the week: go through that Instagram and the waitlist page properly, as a stranger who has never heard of the brand, and write down where you lose interest and why. Then build two or three things that would have held you. Actually build them, do not describe them. The gap between describing a hook and making one is where most people stop, and it is the entire reason the ones who do not stop get taken seriously.

I am not going to do that analysis for you, because it is the part that will teach you the most and the part Jesse will want to hear in your own voice. But I will pressure-test anything you make, argue with your conclusions, dig out whatever you need on the brand, and tell you straight when something is not working. Send me drafts as rough as you like.
