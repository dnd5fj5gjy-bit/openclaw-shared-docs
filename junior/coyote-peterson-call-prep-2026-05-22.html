First Two Minutes

Opening the Call

Jesse has never met Coyote. The first two minutes matter more than people think. Don't open with Bear or business. Open with a specific observation that shows you actually know who Coyote is.

I've been watching your content for years — the Executioner Wasp episode is one of the most watched things in our house. Does it ever get easier?
Ben told me you're in Columbus — I didn't know the Midwest had wildlife worth filming. What's the craziest thing you've found there?
I saw the Brave Wilderness Explorers content. Is the kids angle something you want to build further, or does it mostly serve the family audience?

Pick the one that feels most natural. The point is specificity — he'll have sat through enough "huge fan of your work" openers from people who've never watched the channel.

Key Questions

What You Actually Want to Know

These are genuine questions, not rhetorical. You want the answers. Specifically listen for what Coyote says he wants that he doesn't yet have.

"Where do you want to take Brave Wilderness over the next few years — is it more content, or are you trying to build something beyond the channel?"
Tells you whether he's building a company or a personal brand. Big difference for any collab structure.
"Have you ever done wildlife content in genuinely remote environments — I mean really off-grid, not documentary-remote?"
Tests whether Coyote can operate in Bear's territory. If he's been waiting for an excuse to try, that's the most interesting answer.
"What's the gap you keep seeing in the outdoor adventure content space that nobody's filling right now?"
This is the most important question on the call. His answer tells you what he wants to build. If he says "depth" or "real stakes" or "prestige" — you're aligned.
"Do you have kids? Does the family angle matter to you personally or is it mostly audience strategy?"
Both personal (builds relationship) and strategic. Save this for the right moment, don't force it.
Strategic Position

How to Frame Uncharted Treasures

Don't pitch it formally. Plant a seed. The concept is Bear's IP — the franchise doesn't need Coyote to work. But Coyote could be a compelling guest on one expedition, the same way a great director casts the right person for a specific role.

If the conversation gets to "what's Bear building?", try this:

"We've been developing something around a series of genuine expeditions — places Bear has never taken cameras before. The idea is each expedition has a specific expertise angle, so you bring in the world's best person on that terrain or that wildlife. The guest serves the story, not the other way around."

This frame does several things simultaneously: positions Bear as IP holder, positions Coyote as a potential guest not a co-owner, makes Coyote curious without over-selling, gives Ben a deal shape he can start thinking about without you committing to anything.

If Coyote pushes for more detail, say the format is still in development and you're being selective about which partnerships make sense. That's true. And it's the right position.
