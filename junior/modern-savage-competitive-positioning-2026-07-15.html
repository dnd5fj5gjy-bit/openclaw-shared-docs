The one-line read

The US organ-supplement category is a wall of unflavoured pills sold by niche male influencers. Modern Savage's stack — a good-tasting flavoured powder + protein + fruit + probiotics, fronted by a globally famous founder — is a format, experience and reach advantage that no incumbent currently occupies.

Don't compete on price-per-capsule. Own "the daily organ blend people actually enjoy drinking," priced premium, and weaponise Bear's reach against a field of sub-3M-follower founders. Two real risks, both managed: native organ taste is hard to mask (the bet rides on the flavour system), and FTC claim discipline. Neither slows us down.

Market context

  • Category size: US/global beef-organ supplements ~$658M (2025) → ~$695M (2026), heading to ~$980M by 2032 (~5.9% CAGR). Grass-fed sub-segment ~$205M → ~$297M by 2032. Beef-liver-capsule niche grows faster (~10%).
  • The wave we ride: carnivore/paleo/"ancestral" going mainstream + a hard shift to traceability. The category is moving from fringe to mainstream — exactly the audience the fringe brands can't credibly reach.
  • The playbook is singular: founder-as-face. Heart & Soil = Dr Paul Saladino (~2.8M IG, Rogan, MD). Ancestral = Liver King. MK = Michael Kummer. The supplement is a monetisation layer on a personal brand.
  • How the leader was built: Heart & Soil went $0 → ~$50M in 3 years almost entirely on ORGANIC content — 2 podcasts/week, daily IG, email, then seeding. Paid was secondary. This is the template we can out-run.
  • Claims style: structure/function only ("supports energy / immunity / hormones"). Sourcing is the proof point ("100% grass-fed and finished," non-defatted, glass bottles).

The whitespace — where Modern Savage wins

The incumbent field is overwhelmingly capsule, unflavoured, clinical, and dependent on a niche male influencer. Five open lanes and one structural advantage:

  1. Format gap — the palatable daily powder. ~90% of the category is pills; the "taste" leaders market taste-free as a feature. A genuinely good-tasting powder that mixes into water/smoothie removes both adoption barriers at once (6 pills a day; the gag factor). Our only powder rivals are Noble Origins (polarising, $2.36+/srv) and Pluck (a condiment). Nobody owns "delicious daily organ blend."
  2. Ritual, not chore. A flavoured scoop slots into the coffee/smoothie habit — "a drink I want," not "a supplement I tolerate." Better retention, better word-of-mouth.
  3. Whole-food multivitamin, one hero SKU. Organs + protein + fruit + probiotics = a complete daily foundation. Undercuts Ancestral's confusing 40-SKU wall with one hero product (+ Mini Savage for kids, Summit Stack for performance).
  4. Mainstream and family-friendly, not fringe-carnivore. A premium, approachable brand — with an actual kids' SKU — captures the mainstreaming wave the fringe-male brands structurally can't reach.
  5. Price headroom. Premium powder already clears $2.36–2.63/srv. Price on taste + brand + completeness, not the $0.60–1.00 capsule floor.
  6. The founder advantage — the category's own playbook, maxed out. Every dollar here is made on founder-as-face with 1–3M followers. Bear Grylls dwarfs every incumbent founder, and "wild / primal / resilient" maps onto ancestral nutrition more cleanly than any of them — without the carnivore-influencer controversy. Our single largest structural edge, and it turbo-charges the same organic-content model Heart & Soil used to hit $50M.

Positioning recommendation

Where to plant the flag

"The primal daily blend you'll actually look forward to." Not a pill you endure — a great-tasting scoop of nose-to-tail nutrition (organs, protein, fruit, probiotics) that becomes your morning ritual. Ancestral nutrition, made modern and made delicious.

Wedge = taste + completeness + Bear, against a category that is bland, single-organ, and fronted by niche figures.

Price ladder

  • Anchor the 28-serving adult pouch in a premium powder band — target ~$2.10–2.50/serving (≈ $59–69/pouch): below Noble Origins' top end, clearly above the capsule floor. Says "premium, taste-led, complete" without looking eccentric.
  • Subscription is table stakes (H&S 25% first / 10% recurring). Run a first-order incentive + recurring discount that still protects the margin in the 14 Jul financial model.
  • Bundle Adult + Mini + Summit as a "household" or "performance" bundle — H&S's upsell mechanic, but around our clean three-SKU story, not 40 confusing organs.

Three messaging pillars

1 · Taste is the product. Lead with it — the one thing the entire incumbent field cannot claim. Every asset makes the flavour/ritual visceral (mix shots, smoothie, morning routine).
2 · Nose-to-tail, made complete. Organs + protein + fruit + probiotics = a whole-food daily foundation. One scoop, not a pill cabinet.
3 · Wild by nature. Bear's world — primal, resilient, real food — as the brand universe. The reach multiplier and the emotional wedge the others lack.

The go-to-market motion

Run the Heart & Soil organic playbook, at Bear scale. Their $0→$50M was almost entirely organic content + education + seeding. We have a founder with global reach and a ~1,800-strong warm waitlist. Motion: education-framed organic content (why organs, why taste matters) → founder-led hero moments → creator seeding (Ku Creatives volume already running) → waitlist-first launch offer. Paid amplifies; it does not lead.

What to avoid

  • Don't race the capsule brands on price-per-serving. We lose that game and it kills the premium/taste story. Our comp set is Noble Origins, not Ancestral.
  • Don't out-carnivore the carnivores. Approachable and mainstream beats hardcore; the fringe-male lane is saturated and carries baggage.
  • Don't overreach on claims. Structure/function only. The FTC polices advertising independently of DSHEA (CARSE substantiation); the Liver King / Ancestral $25M suit is the cautionary tale. Stay inside our claims-compliance guardrail (12 Jul).
  • Don't bury the founder. In a founder-as-face category, ours is the biggest asset the space has ever had. Use him early and centrally.

Regulatory guardrails — quick reference

  • Organ supplements = dietary supplements under DSHEA: no pre-market approval, essentially no clinical trials.
  • Permitted: structure/function ("supports," "helps maintain"). Prohibited: disease claims ("treats," "cures") — those make it an unapproved drug.
  • FTC polices ads separately and requires substantiation (CARSE). DSHEA labeling latitude does not shield ad claims.
  • Downside precedent: Liver King / Ancestral $25M misleading-representations lawsuit.
