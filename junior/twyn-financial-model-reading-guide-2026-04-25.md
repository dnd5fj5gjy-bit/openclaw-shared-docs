## The Four Numbers That Matter

When you open Zain's model, ignore the complexity. Find these four:

**1. Year 1 Gross Revenue (Bear's content, specifically)**
Not platform revenue. Bear's content revenue — what his episodes, interactive choices, and audience engagement generate. If Zain mixes Bear's revenue with platform-wide revenue, ask for a breakdown.

**2. Year 3 Gross Revenue (Bear's content)**
This is the number that tells you whether the platform is real. Year 1 is a guess. Year 3 is where Twyn's growth model becomes a testable prediction.

**3. Subscriber/User Growth Assumption**
How fast does Twyn project their user base to grow? This is the core assumption everything else is built on. It's also the assumption most likely to be wrong.

**4. Twyn's Implied Valuation**
If Twyn offers Bear X% equity, what are they implying their company is worth? Ask: "What is the pre-money valuation you're using to set equity?" If they dodge, infer it from the revenue projections and a reasonable multiple.

## Red Flags in Zain's Model

**Flag 1: Hockey stick Year 3**
If Year 1 and Year 2 are modest but Year 3 suddenly jumps 5x, that's the model doing the heavy lifting in a year too far out to hold anyone accountable. Ask: "What specific market event or product milestone drives the Year 3 jump?"

**Flag 2: Platform-wide revenue, not Bear-specific**
If the model shows platform revenue and doesn't isolate Bear's content, Tim is trying to make the numbers look bigger. You need Bear's actual content revenue, not Twyn's total.

**Flag 3: No cost model**
If the model only shows revenue without platform operating costs, you can't evaluate when Twyn becomes cash flow positive — which affects whether the equity is worth anything.

**Flag 4: Equity at uncommitted valuation**
If Tim says "10% equity" without stating the valuation, that percentage means nothing. At a £500K valuation, 10% is £50K. At a £5M valuation, it's £500K. Get the number.

**Flag 5: Conservative growth assumption used to justify low rev share**
Zain may present modest projections to argue Bear should accept a lower rev share because the revenues won't be that high anyway. The counter: if the projections are modest, you need a higher percentage to make the math work. Low projections and low rev share is the worst possible outcome.

## What to Ask Zain on a Call

When Jesse reviews the model, here are the questions worth asking (ideally after May 21 return, before committing to any deal):

1. "What is Twyn's pre-money valuation you're working with?"
2. "Can you show me Bear's content revenue isolated from total platform revenue?"
3. "What's the assumption behind Year 3 growth — what event drives it?"
4. "What are Twyn's Year 1 operating costs, and when do you hit break-even?"
5. "If Bear's content performs at the 25,000-subscriber level, what does his annual income look like at the rev share you're proposing?"

## The Bottom Line Test

When you've read the model, apply this test:

**At Zain's conservative-case projections and the recommended 60-70% rev share, does Bear earn enough to justify (a) the production time commitment and (b) not licensing to Netflix/Amazon instead?**

If yes at conservative case: the deal makes sense to do.
If only at optimistic case: the deal requires a meaningful production guarantee to offset the risk.
If not even at optimistic case: the rev share terms need to move, or the equity needs to compensate.

That's the logic. The model will try to overwhelm you with detail. This is the one question it needs to answer.
