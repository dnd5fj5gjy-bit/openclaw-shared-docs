## Robin Knox-Johnston's Advice (February 13, 2026)

*Sir Robin did the route last year (2025). His notes, summarised:*

**Tasilaq (main port, ~3,000 people):**
- Entered Tasilaq end of June from Reykjavik last year. Ice was still sealing Scorsbysund, but a boat got in 3 weeks later as it retreated north.
- You can go alongside in Tasilaq — but between supply ships. Time your arrival for a gap in their schedule.
- Angmassilik Fjord: small village, berth at the fish jetty. Cost: one tray of soft drinks. ~120 people, "magnificent all-weather football pitch."

**Scorsbysund:**
- 250 people. You anchor off — no going alongside.

**Apillotoq:**
- Small village in the fjords behind Cape Farwell (southernmost tip). Hidden gem.

**Navigation:**
- AIS is essential. Robin didn't use radar but AIS is non-negotiable — gives your position and nearby vessel positions.
- Charts are unreliable. Robin followed his GPS plotter up a Fjord and it showed him halfway up the adjacent mountain. Cross-reference everything. Don't trust any single source.
- Buy: **Cruising Association Guide to Greenland** (theca.org.uk) — based on member experience, includes Greenland reporting instructions.

**Iceland (Reykjavik):**
- Small yacht club marina just inside main harbour to port — NOT the cruise liner harbour. Welcoming, reasonable prices, good hot showers, walkable to supermarkets/car hire.
- Icelandic coastguard: very efficient, contact via VHF or internet when you're picked up. They want to know your destination and when you arrive. Phone them on arrival — numbers are in the CA Iceland guide.
- Buy: **Cruising Association Guide to Iceland** as well.

**Customs and entry:**
- Iceland: Robin cleared customs himself without an agent — he was a non-commercial vessel. "They are friendly and made it easy."
- Greenland: bureaucracy has increased over the last 5 years. Still manageable for non-commercial vessels but do it properly.
- Some Greenland areas are now protected — you need advance permission to visit. Example: Kangerllugsuaq (between Tasilaq and Scorsbysund) now requires special permission. Check which fjords you plan to visit before departure.

**Robin's final note:**
> "If Jessie does not take you, I will. You would love it. Real adventure and most of the interesting places can only be visited by boat."

## Live Resources to Check Before and During the Trip

### Ice Conditions (check daily once in Iceland)
- **DMI Southeast Greenland ice charts:** ocean.dmi.dk/arctic/icecharts_gl_southeast.uk.php
  - Updated regularly. Ice charts show 0-10/10 concentration. 9+/10 = very close drift ice, passage unlikely. 6-7/10 = difficult but possible.
  - As of April 22, 2026: ice charts were active and current.
- **Norwegian Ice Service (for Norwegian Sea):** cryo.met.no/en/latest-ice-charts
  - Norwegian waters ice charts, weekdays.

### Passage Planning References
- **Cruising Association Guides:** shop.theca.org.uk
  - Iceland guide and Greenland guide. Robin specifically recommended both. Get them before you go.
- **Admiralty charts:** admiralty.co.uk/charts
  - Paper backup for the fjords. GPS unreliable in places.

## Ice Timing (from Robin's 2025 experience)

- **End of June:** Tasilaq accessible from Reykjavik. Ice retreating.
- **Early July:** Scorsbysund may open if ice has retreated.
- Jesse is sailing May 7-21 — this is **earlier than Robin went** (late June). East Greenland ice extent in mid-May is typically heavier than late June. Check DMI charts as you approach Iceland. Adjust based on current conditions.

## Polar Bear Protocol (from Robin's notes, general Arctic practice)

Robin mentioned polar bears in his original tips. General guidance:
- Keep a watch when ashore in Greenland, particularly on uninhabited coasts.
- Flares and a loud air horn are standard deterrents. Do not approach.
- Scorsbysund area is polar bear territory. Local knowledge when in port.
