## Council — six lenses

**1. Financial.** The studio play is a capital sink dressed as growth. Boutique PT studios = leases, fit-out capex, fixed staff, weather-proof but rent-and-rates-heavy. The worst possible model for a business that has already gone insolvent twice. The creator play is near-zero capex: leverage brand + domain + an audience you don't have to buy. Monetise through online coaching, programming/app, apparel, supplements, sponsorships and instructor/franchise recruitment. Asset-light, marketing-led. That's BGV's actual competency.

**2. Brand.** "Be Mission **Fit**" renames away from the one word that carries the equity: *Military*. That alone tells you Jay is using BMF as a shell to launch his own studio concept. Meanwhile Bear IS the definitive ex-forces, grit, outdoors figure. An ex-military creator fronting BMF is directly on-brand and reads as "next-generation Bear." A converted-unit PT studio is off-brand LA-fitness aesthetic, not adventure and grit.

**3. Timing.** Military fitness, HYROX, rucking, hybrid-athlete content is having a genuine cultural moment. Ex-forces fitness creators are riding it right now. The creator play surfs an existing wave; the studio play has zero tailwind and worse-than-ever unit economics (rent, rates, energy).

**4. Dependency / risk.** Studio deal: bet on one unproven operator, real estate, and financials that don't exist. Slow, high-burn, hard to reverse (leases). Creator deal: main risk is key-person / reputational, and it's cheap and reversible. Mitigate by having BMF own the channels and running a *bench* of ex-military ambassadors, not staking everything on one personal handle.

**5. Long-term value.** Studios at 2 years, best case: three marginal sites and a lease book. Creator/brand at 2 years: an audience, digital products, an apparel/supplement line, and an instructor pipeline — a sellable IP-led asset, not a property liability.

**6. Quick win (30-90 days).** Sign one anchor ex-military creator as BMF's "Head of Fitness"/face on a performance/rev-share deal (not big cash). Relaunch the socials through them. Run a rucking/bootcamp challenge tied to Bear's audience. Near-zero cost, immediate signal to NM that there's a real direction.

## The honest caveat (so you can defend it on the call)

An influencer is **not a business model** — it's a distribution engine. Tegs-type gets you attention; the money still has to come from something you *sell*. So the recommendation is precise: **influencer-fronted, product-and-digital-monetised brand**, not "hire a face and hope." The monetisation layer is exactly where BGV's existing stack plugs in — supplements (Modern Savage / Ted's supply chain), apparel, an app/programming product, and paid instructor certification/franchise. That's the part to nail down, and it's the part Jay's deck can't give you either.

Two picks-matter points on the creator:
- **Credibility bar.** Choose someone whose service story is unimpeachable and who has operator instinct, not just followers. Tegs (Royal Marine Band Service) is a softer military story than a commando/infantry one — fine as an archetype, worth pressure-testing the specific person.
- **Structure.** BMF owns the handles and IP; the creator is contracted as the face with an ambassador bench beneath. Never hand the brand to one person's personal account.
