## The Core Observation

Jesse moves fast on relationships, slow on documents.

This is not a flaw. It's a deliberate operating mode. The question is whether I understand it well enough to work with it rather than around it.

## Why This Is Actually Rational

Jesse operates in a world where most opportunities move on human relationships, not legal structures. Deal flow at the BG/BGV level comes through trust networks — the Bear connection means everyone wants to do business. The bottleneck is never "can we find a partner?" It's "is this the right partner?"

In that context, moving fast on relationships is the competitive advantage. If Jesse spent 3 weeks negotiating terms before saying yes to Calvin, Calvin might have gone elsewhere or lost momentum. The fast yes preserves the deal. The documents are administrative.

This also explains the equity-not-cash preference. Equity aligns interests for years. A verbal yes on equity is a stronger signal than a verbal yes on a fee — both parties know the real commitment is in the relationship, not the contract.

**His mental model:** The agreement is in the relationship. The contract just records it.

---

## Where It Creates Risk

The gap between "yes" and "documented" is where things go wrong. Three specific vulnerabilities:

### 1. Memory Divergence
Both parties remember the verbal agreement differently. Jesse thinks Calvin's equity vests over 2 years; Calvin thinks it starts now. There's no contract to check. This is the classic startup equity disaster — it has destroyed companies with much larger legal budgets than BGV.

### 2. Commitment Erosion
The momentum that made saying yes easy also makes formalising it feel like bureaucracy. Each week without a contract, the urgency drops. Ships A Lot proposal was agreed two weeks ago. Still not signed. The risk isn't that Jesse changes his mind — it's that he never formally commits, and the counterparty starts to wonder.

### 3. Coverage Gaps During Transitions
Jesse sails May 7. Thirteen days. No signed Calvin contracts. If Jesse is unreachable for 4-6 weeks and a dispute arises — over scope, over payment, over what the equity covers — there's no document to arbitrate with. CJ and the team are exposed.

---

## The Pattern I've Observed in the Data

Looking at 1,000 WhatsApp messages: Jesse intervenes on the execution layer when things are at risk of going wrong, not as ongoing oversight. He delegates completely, then checks back at inflection points.

This means:
- The Calvin equity gap will stay a gap until something forces it closed (a dispute, a contract request from Calvin, or a calendar deadline like Jesse's departure)
- Ships A Lot will stay unsigned until Drew asks for the order
- The James Ray repost will happen at a moment when Jesse is thinking about it, not as a scheduled action

**Nothing gets formalised on Jesse's initiative. Everything gets formalised when the counterparty asks or the situation demands it.**

---

## What This Means for How I Work

### What I Should Do
1. **Track the gaps, not just the agreements.** When Jesse says yes to something, flag the documentation gap immediately in SESSION-STATE.md with a specific deadline. Don't trust that it'll happen.

2. **Create the forcing function.** Jesse doesn't create urgency around documents. I need to surface the deadline. "Calvin contracts — 13 days before you sail. After that, three brands with no signed agreements and CJ holding the phone." That's the kind of framing that produces action.

3. **Separate "agreed" from "done" in my tracking.** Ships A Lot is not done. Email 1 is not done. The Qwilr is not signed. In my notes, agreed and done are different states.

4. **Surface pattern consequences, not just items.** Jesse responds to system-level observations, not checklists. "You have 5 verbal agreements that aren't documented and you're leaving in 13 days" hits differently than "please sign the Ships A Lot Qwilr."

### What I Should NOT Do
- Lecture him about documentation discipline. He's been successful for years with this approach. He's not wrong — he's optimised for a different variable than I default to.
- Send the document for him. Email safety rule: draft only, never send. But more than that — pushing him toward formal documents at the wrong moment reads as distrust of the relationship, which is exactly what he doesn't want.
- Miss the pattern entirely and keep surfacing individual items without connecting them.
