The Category Decision

Five candidates. One choice. What Wildernests calls itself determines who buys it, who funds it, and who copies it.

Prepared 25 May 2026  •  For May 28 weekly call  •  Jesse Grylls / Junior
Recommendation
"Spacecraft for Planet Earth"
"We don't build homes. We build spacecraft — engineered for the most extreme and beautiful places on Earth."
It's the only candidate that (a) can't be copied by a caravan company, (b) signals the level of engineering required for true off-grid performance, and (c) gives Tattarang a fundable narrative about defensible tech. Full rationale below.

Why This Decision Matters Now

Category naming is not a marketing question. It's a strategic one. What Wildernests calls its product determines:

The buyer: "Off-grid cabin" attracts weekenders. "Spacecraft" attracts people spending $300K+ on a second property with serious engineering expectations.

The investor narrative: Tattarang's key question was moat durability. The answer lives in the category name. "Anywhere Home" sounds rentable. "Spacecraft for Planet Earth" implies proprietary systems that competitors can't replicate in a season.

The competitive frame: A good category name makes competitors play on your terms. Tesla didn't call the Model S a "nice electric car" - they made internal combustion the old way. Wildernests needs the same rhetorical leverage.

Phase 0 outreach (July): The call-to-action for pre-launch targeted outreach needs a single, clear line. The category IS that line.

The BCG Digital Ventures workshop on May 20 surfaced five candidates. None is wrong. One is clearly best. Here's the full analysis.

Full Analysis — Each Candidate

"Spacecraft for Planet Earth"
Rank 1  /  Recommended
Winner

The boldest, most ownable candidate. It reframes what a home can be — not a building, but a precision-engineered system designed for hostile and remote environments. That's exactly what a Wildernest must do if you're placing it in Alaska, the Norwegian fjords, or a Costa Rican rainforest.

The word "Spacecraft" does two things at once: it signals engineering grade (not a kit cabin, not glamping) and it implies a mission — you're going somewhere extraordinary. The qualifier "for Planet Earth" is the clever corrective that keeps it grounded. You're not actually going to space. You're going to the most beautiful, inaccessible parts of this planet.

Strengths
Completely ownable — no other company uses this
Signals engineering precision, not just aesthetic
Justifies premium price point through implied tech
Perfect for Bear Grylls — exploration grade, adventure-led
Investor pitch: implies R&D moat, replicable systems
Creates a natural language for the product family
Risks

Key insight: The clarity risk is manageable. The ownability is not. "Anywhere Home" can be stolen tomorrow by Airstream, Airbnb, or a startup with $5M. "Spacecraft for Planet Earth" cannot be stolen — it's too specific to Wildernests' actual product truth.

"Anywhere Home"
Rank 2  /  Best backup if team won't commit to Spacecraft
Backup

The clearest and most emotionally immediate candidate. Everyone instantly understands what it means. It speaks directly to the core product promise: a premium home in any location you choose.

The problem is ownability. "Anywhere Home" is a phrase that dozens of companies could credibly adopt — from Airbnb Anywhere to Domino's (where a home can be "anywhere"). It also risks framing Wildernests as a rental product (Anywhere Home sounds like "you can stay anywhere") rather than an owned asset. At $250K+ prices, buyers are acquiring property, not booking accommodation.

Drop

"No-compromise" is one of the most overused phrases in premium positioning. It is used by mattress brands (Casper), car brands (BMW), supplement brands, kitchen brands, and dozens of others. It communicates quality intention but says nothing about what makes Wildernests unique.

"Post-grid Home" / "Beyond-the-grid Homes"
Rank 4-5  /  Drop both
Drop

Both are defined by what they're NOT — they're not on the grid. Negative positioning is weak positioning. It puts the grid at the centre of the story, which makes utility infrastructure (electricity, sewage) the frame for a luxury product conversation.

More practically: "off-grid" connotes cost-savings, prepper culture, and rural simplicity. None of these associations belong in the same sentence as a $300K precision-built luxury residence. A wealthy Tattersalls buyer doesn't want to feel like they're buying a survivalist cabin.

One real strength
Strong for ESG/sustainability-focused investors
"Post-grid" is forward-looking in framing
Why they fail
Defined by absence, not by ambition
Off-grid connotes prepper, camping, cost-cutting
Alienates mainstream luxury buyer
"Beyond" is one of the most generic prefixes in branding

What "Spacecraft for Planet Earth" Actually Unlocks

A strong category name is not just a tagline — it's a thinking tool that aligns the whole team. Here's what "Spacecraft" unlocks across every major workstream:

Product Engineering
Spacecraft-grade systems: thermal management, power systems, structural integrity under environmental stress. This is why Gunnar's obsession with material quality and realistic renders isn't cosmetic — it's the proof layer that the category claim is real.
Pricing
Spacecraft are not cheap. The category sets price expectations at SpaceX-level engineering premium, not Airstream-level convenience premium. At $250K+, buyers need a mental model that justifies the number. "Spacecraft" provides it.
Marketing / Bear Angle
Bear Grylls has taken his family to some of the most extreme environments on Earth. Wildernests lets anyone do that — in a home built for those exact conditions. "This is how we live" is a uniquely credible story in Bear's hands.
Competitive Moat
Competitors cannot simply claim "spacecraft" without building the systems to back it. The category raises the bar for entry. It's self-defending: if you haven't engineered your home for genuine off-grid performance, you can't credibly use the frame.
Phase 0 Outreach (July)
Targeted HNWI outreach message writes itself: "We build spacecraft for Planet Earth — precision-engineered homes for the world's most remarkable places. Preview access opens [date]." Hooks the right buyer immediately.
Visual Identity
Gunnar's brief for the renders aligns perfectly: cinematic, realistic finishes, luxury camera movement. That IS spacecraft-grade visual production. The category validates what the visual team is already trying to do.
Tattarang / Investor Angle

Tattarang's core question: moat durability. What stops a well-funded competitor from building the same thing?

The "Spacecraft" category provides the strongest answer: a spacecraft-grade off-grid home requires engineering systems that cannot be assembled quickly. Thermal management, energy storage, structural performance under environmental stress, water management — these are not bolt-on features. They take years of R&D to get right. The category signals that the moat is technical, not just aesthetic.

Compare to "Anywhere Home": a well-funded competitor can claim that in a month. Compare to "Spacecraft for Planet Earth": a competitor must first build spacecraft-grade systems, then earn the right to the claim. That's a defensible category.

How to use this in the Tattarang narrative: "We're not building luxury camping. We're building the first off-grid residential system engineered to the standards of extreme-environment infrastructure. The category we're creating — Spacecraft for Planet Earth — requires technical depth that takes years to develop. That's the moat."

One Honest Caveat

The category name works perfectly — IF the product genuinely delivers spacecraft-grade performance. The risk is aspirational overreach: calling something a spacecraft and then delivering what feels like a well-designed shed would be catastrophic for trust.

The question to ask Gunnar and Alex at the May 28 call: "What specific engineering systems make us genuinely spacecraft-grade, and which are we still building toward?"

The category should be adopted now — but the team must then build the product roadmap around earning it. The visual assets Gunnar is developing need to show the systems (energy array, thermal design, structural tolerance) not just the aesthetics. This is the visual direction brief for Joe and Gunnar.
