This is deliberately harder than a research exercise. We're handing you a live product and asking you to think like the person responsible for launching it. There's no worksheet and no set of prompts to follow, because working out the how is most of the skill. What you'll be judged on is judgement.

It pulls together four things that rarely get taught together: marketing, branding, financial modelling, and building with AI. Real operators need all four at once, and the point of this challenge is to make you switch between them the way a founder has to.

The remit, in one line: You are the launch strategist for Modern Savage in the US. Build the case for how it wins, prove the numbers, and ship it as things you've made yourself. Then defend it.

What Modern Savage is

Enough to orient you. The rest you dig out yourself.

  • A US supplement built on primal / ancestral nutrition: a daily blend of freeze-dried grass-fed organ meats (liver, heart, kidney and more) with protein, fruit, vitamins and probiotics. The pitch: eat what our ancestors thrived on, in a form modern people will actually take.
  • Range: the adult daily blend, a kids version (Mini Savage), and a performance add-on (Summit Stack).
  • World: it lives in the Bear Grylls universe, adventure, wildness, doing hard things, honesty about food.
  • Start here, then go deep on your own: modernsavage.co.

The four workstreams

Each one is a question to answer, not a set of steps. The italic line is the real test, the bit that separates a smart answer from an obvious one.

01 · Marketing intelligence

Read the battlefield

Who actually competes with Modern Savage, and how do they win? Map the serious players in the organ / ancestral supplement space, understand each one's game, and find the open ground.

The test: don't just list competitors. Name the single gap in the market most worth attacking, and defend why it's real and winnable.
02 · Branding

Decide what it stands for

What does Modern Savage stand for that a competitor can't just copy? Define the brand in one sentence, its voice, what it's against, and the feeling it should leave. Then show it, not just describe it: a single screen that captures how it looks and sounds and what the promise on the pack is.

The test: could someone read your one sentence and instantly know which product on a shelf is ours? Vague equals wrong.
03 · Go-to-market

Win the first 1,000 customers

You have a modest launch budget (you decide the number and justify it). How do you get the first 1,000 paying customers in the US? Which channels, which message, what content, and in what order across the first 90 days.

The test: it has to be a plan, not a wish. "Go viral" and "influencers" are not strategies until you say exactly who, why them, and what happens if it doesn't work.
04 · Financial modelling

Prove it makes money

Build a real model in a spreadsheet. Unit economics per pouch (price, cost, margin). What it costs to acquire a customer versus what that customer is worth over time (CAC vs LTV). A 12-month launch forecast with every assumption visible. When does it break even?

The test: intellectual honesty. If the numbers don't work at your assumptions, say so, and show exactly what would have to change to make them work.
The build — this is non-negotiable

Ship it, don't describe it

Two things you make yourself, using AI as your build partner:

1. An interactive pitch site

A single scrolling web page that presents your whole strategy, brand, market, go-to-market, the headline numbers. Build it by directing an AI to write the code (or an AI site builder). It should look like something you'd actually show an investor.

2. The financial model

A working spreadsheet, not a screenshot. Formulas that update when you change an assumption. You should be able to be asked "what if CAC doubles?" and just change one cell.

We care that you built them and can explain every number, word and design choice. A thing that exists beats a description of a thing every time.

How it'll be judged

  • Original thinking. We can tell the difference between your view and AI filler. Have an actual opinion.
  • Everything defensible. Every claim checkable, every number traceable to an assumption you'll stand behind.
  • A point of view. Take a clear position and be ready to argue it under questioning.
  • Shipped and working. The site loads, the model calculates. Done beats perfect.
  • You can present it in 10 minutes without reading off the screen, and handle the hard questions after.

Using AI like an operator, not a student

This is the meta-skill and the reason Dad wants you doing it. AI is the best analyst, designer and coder you'll ever have on tap, and it is also confidently wrong all the time. The whole game is: brief it sharply, pressure-test what it hands back, cross-check anything that matters, and own the final call. If you can't defend it, don't ship it.

The goal is not to get AI to do this for you. It's to become the person who can make AI produce excellent work, and who knows when it's lying. That person is worth a great deal.
