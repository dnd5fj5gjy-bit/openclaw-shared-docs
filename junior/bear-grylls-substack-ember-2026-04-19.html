The Ember

On doubt, belief, and the small thing that keeps you going.

I failed SAS Selection the first time.

I know, I know. Not the opening line people expect from me. But it's the truth.

Four months of hard, cold, grinding misery. And at the end of it, I was pulled from the course. Injury, yes. But that doesn't make the failing any easier.

I remember lying in my bivvy that night, dry and warm, listening to the rain. And all I could feel was shame. The weird thing was, being dry and warm felt awful. I had wanted to be out there. Passing. Not lying there, failed.

The weeks after were dark.

Mental turmoil was a new emotion for me then. I felt I had let myself down. Wasted four months. I was depressed and I felt useless. And that was on a good day.

The only silver lining was that my training team had invited me back to try again. From the start. Day one.

Horrendous concept.

But they didn't invite people back who they didn't believe had the right stuff. That was some small glimmer, at least.

Here is what I know now, looking back: my greatest enemy at that point wasn't the course. It wasn't the instructors or the weather or my knackered back. It was myself. Self-doubt can be crushing. Sometimes it is hard to see outside the black bubble.

I tried to look at it clearly. I had failed Selection a third of the way through. What realistic chance did I have of going back and passing?

My family, kindly and with love, said maybe it wasn't meant to be. That I had gained invaluable experience.

That just made it worse.

Yet through all of that, a little part of me, deep down, believed I could do it. That I was capable of passing. It wasn't a big part of me. Not some roaring fire of conviction.

It was an ember.

Sometimes an ember is all we need.

My old friend Ed Amies said something simple that cut right through the noise: "So often, God's callings have a birth, a death and then a resurrection."

I had had the birth. I had had the death. Maybe this was the resurrection.

I went back. I passed.

Most people think the mark of a champion is confidence. Certainty. Knowing you're going to win.

It isn't.

The mark of a champion is never the absence of fear or doubt. It's how you respond when fear and doubt come calling. And trust me, if the stakes are high enough, they will.

Fear forces you to look tough on the outside, but it makes you weak on the inside. I have felt that. Plenty of times. You learn to spot it.

The question isn't whether doubt shows up. It will. The question is what you do with it.

You don't need certainty to take the next step. You just need the ember. That small, stubborn, quiet thing in you that still believes, even when nothing else does.

Replace the doubt with hope. The fear with courage. The self-pity with something that looks like pride.

Then move. Head down. Into the wind.

That's it. That's the whole game.

Never Give Up.

Bear
