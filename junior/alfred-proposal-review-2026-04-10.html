Critical Issues — Fix Before Sending

Critical #1

The narrative journey is in the wrong order

This is the most important problem. Right now the structure is: Hero → Challenge → Why Bear Witness → Brand Voice → Strategy → Channels → Roadmap. That means the reader hits the Bear Witness pitch before they've felt the full weight of the opportunity. You're asking Tom to buy in to you before he's excited about what you're going to do.

The winning emotional journey should be: Feel the opportunity → get excited by the strategy → then find out who's going to execute it. "Why Bear Witness" should be the closing argument, not the second section.

Recommended section order:

  1. Hero — The prize (what Alfred can become)
  2. The Inertia Economy — Make him feel the problem and opportunity
  3. The Strategy — Brand voice, mission narratives, the Inertia Files concept
  4. The Execution — Channels, content pillars, paid, SEO, email
  5. The Roadmap — 24-month phased plan with milestones
  6. Why Bear Witness — The closer: who's doing this, why we're different, track record
  7. The Ask — Clear CTA
Prompt for Claude to restructure section order:
Restructure the navigation and section order of this proposal. The current order is wrong — "Why Bear Witness" appears too early and kills the narrative momentum before the reader is excited about the strategy. Reorder the sections to this sequence: 1. Hero (keep as is) 2. The Challenge / Inertia Economy (currently "The Challenge" section) 3. The Strategy — brand voice, mission narratives, the Inertia Files (currently scattered through the middle) 4. Execution Detail — channels, content pillars, paid, SEO, email (currently in the middle) 5. The Roadmap — 24-month phased plan with milestones (currently near end) 6. Why Bear Witness — move this from section 2 to section 6. This becomes the closer, not the opener. Make it feel like the natural answer to "okay, but WHO is going to do all of this?" Rewrite the opening line of this section to reflect that it's the closing argument: something like "Everything above requires one thing: the right partner. Here's why Bear Witness." 7. The Ask — CTA / contact section Update the navigation anchors to match. Ensure scroll highlighting still works after reorder.
Critical #2

The hero section undersells the prize

"Building the UK's most trusted brand from zero presence" is a solid descriptor but it's process-oriented. It describes what Bear Witness will DO, not what Alfred will BECOME. The hero is the first thing Tom sees — it needs to make him feel the size of the opportunity before he reads a single word of strategy.

A stronger hero leads with Alfred's potential position — not the challenge. Make him feel like he's looking at something historic.

Prompt for Claude to rewrite the hero:
Rewrite the hero section of the proposal. Current headline: "Building the UK's most trusted brand from zero presence." Replace it with a hero that leads with the PRIZE, not the process. Alfred has an extraordinary opportunity: to become the first UK brand that genuinely took the side of the consumer in utilities — and built a movement around it. The hero should make Tom feel the scale of what's possible, not just describe what Bear Witness is going to do. New headline direction: something that captures the idea that Alfred isn't just another utility provider — it could be the brand that finally told the truth and changed the category. Keep the hero subtitle copy short and punchy. No more than two sentences. Maintain the existing visual design (animated gradient, floating shapes). Just change the headline and subtitle text to match this energy.
Critical #3

"Monzo-style" — brand comparison in the roadmap

Phase 2 describes the waitlist launch as "Monzo-style in-app waitlist reveal." This is exactly the kind of brand comparison Jesse has explicitly said not to use — it makes Alfred feel like a copy of someone else's idea rather than something original. Tom will notice this and it undermines the "Alfred is its own thing" positioning the rest of the proposal builds carefully.

Prompt for Claude:
In the Phase 2 section of the roadmap (Waitlist Launch, June), there is a reference to "Monzo-style in-app waitlist reveal." Remove this brand comparison entirely. Replace it with copy that describes the mechanic itself — a members-first, private reveal to people who've been following the brand since the Foundation Phase. The idea is that the waitlist isn't a generic sign-up form — it's an earned moment. The first people who know Alfred is real are the ones who've been paying attention. Rewrite to convey: the waitlist reveal feels like being let in on a secret, not a generic product launch. No brand comparisons. Alfred's launch should feel entirely its own.

Important Issues — Fix Soon

Important #4

No proof that Bear Witness can actually do this

The "Why Bear Witness" section explains the model well (equity-aligned, AI-native, direct leadership) but gives Tom no reason to believe Jesse can execute at scale. There's no track record. No numbers. Tom knows Jesse from lunch — but this proposal may circulate to his co-founders and investors who don't.

One stat anchors everything: Bear Grylls's social channels generate over 100 million monthly impressions. Jesse manages all of it. That single data point turns "Bear Witness is promising" into "Bear Witness has done this at scale, for years."

Prompt for Claude:
In the "Why Bear Witness" section, add a short credibility block after the opening description of the model. It should feel confident but not boastful. Add something like: "Bear Witness is the digital media operation Jesse Grylls has run across the Bear Grylls brand portfolio since 2022. Bear Grylls's social channels — managed entirely by Bear Witness — generate over 100 million monthly impressions across Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, and LinkedIn. This isn't theoretical. The systems, the content engine, and the audience strategy in this proposal are the same approach applied to one of the UK's most recognised personal brands, now being brought to Alfred from day one." Keep it tight — no more than 3-4 sentences. It should slot naturally between the current opening paragraph and the "Traditional Agency Problems" comparison block.
Important #5

"Seek the thank-you" is internal language — it doesn't land in a client pitch

The five brand voice principles are good, but "Seek the thank-you" is a piece of internal strategic shorthand. In a proposal, it sounds vague. Tom won't know what it means without explanation, and the explanation under it ("Content valuable independent of product conversion") is too abstract. Rename it and reframe it so the principle is immediately obvious from its label.

Prompt for Claude:
In the Brand Voice section, rename the fourth principle from "Seek the thank-you" to something more immediately legible to a client. The principle is: Alfred's content should be so genuinely useful that people would want to share it or thank you for it regardless of whether they ever buy. The best content earns its place in someone's feed on its own merits. Rename it to something like "Earn your place in their feed" or "Useful first, always" — something that communicates the idea without requiring explanation. Update the principle name and its short description to match.
Important #6

Phase 2 targets create a credibility gap

Phase 1 targets: 10,000 followers + 2,000 email signups. Phase 2 (just one month later): 15,000 waitlist signups. That's a 7.5x jump from email list to waitlist in 30 days. Without context, this reads as aspirational rather than evidenced. Either add a sentence explaining where those 15,000 come from (the influencer partnerships launching in Phase 2, paid ads, the Inertia Files audience) — or revise the number to be more defensible.

Prompt for Claude:
In the Phase 2 roadmap section (Waitlist Launch, June), the target of "15,000 signups in 30 days" needs brief justification. Without context, the jump from Phase 1's 2,000 email signups to 15,000 waitlist signups in a single month looks ungrounded. Add one short sentence after the target that explains where those signups come from: the combination of the 25,000+ social following built in Phase 1, the influencer partnerships activating simultaneously, the paid advertising launching at this stage, and the Inertia Files audience who've been primed for two months. Format: keep it as part of the existing target/milestone list, not a new paragraph. Just a brief supporting sentence.
Important #7

The closing CTA is too passive

The current closing is: "Bear Witness is ready to begin immediately. Every week of delay is a week less of compounding. The foundation phase is where the most important work happens. We are ready now." Followed by an email address.

This is close — the urgency language is right. But the email address as a CTA is 2012 thinking for a digital-native proposal. Add a calendar link or a specific suggested next step. Tom shouldn't have to figure out what to do next.

Prompt for Claude:
Update the closing CTA section. Keep the urgency copy ("Bear Witness is ready to begin immediately. Every week of delay is a week less of compounding.") but improve the call to action. Replace the plain email address with a more action-oriented closing: - A clear suggested next step: "The next step is a 30-minute call to align on scope, timeline, and structure. Jesse is available this week." - Contact: jesse@beargryllsventures.com - If possible, add a styled button or link that says "Schedule a call →" (can link to jesse@beargryllsventures.com as a mailto for now — can swap for Calendly later) Make the CTA section feel like the natural endpoint of a great pitch — confident, specific, zero friction.

Nice to Have — Polish Round

Polish #8

Elevate Fields in Trust — it's doing more work than it looks

The Fields in Trust partnership (1% of revenue to protect UK green spaces, president Jill Scott MBE) is a genuinely powerful story that currently appears as a minor content pillar ("Field Notes") and a milestone metric. For Alfred's "More Life, Less Admin" mission and their target audience (affluent homeowners who care about lifestyle), this is a significant differentiator. It should appear earlier and be presented as a core brand pillar, not a supporting content lane.

Prompt for Claude:
The Fields in Trust partnership is currently treated as a minor content pillar ("Field Notes — Lane 5"). Upgrade its treatment throughout the proposal. 1. In the Mission Narratives section, elevate "Community Stewardship" from "Supporting" to "Strong Secondary" — it deserves equal billing with "Provide + Protect." 2. In the Fields in Trust / Field Notes content pillar description, add a line about what this means in practice: "Alfred commits 1% of membership revenue to Fields in Trust — the charity that protects UK parks and green spaces in perpetuity. President: Jill Scott MBE. This isn't a marketing gimmick. It's a structural commitment that every piece of Field Notes content can point back to." 3. Consider adding a small "Why Alfred Wins" box somewhere that lists Alfred's three structural advantages: one-place simplicity, wholesale pricing, and the 1% community commitment. These are the three things that make Alfred impossible to replicate without fundamentally rebuilding the business model.
Polish #9

Channel volume numbers may overwhelm in-presentation

The channel strategy section lists posting frequencies for every platform simultaneously — 5-7 Reels, 2-3 carousels, daily Stories, 1-3 TikToks/day, 1 YouTube/week, 3-5 Shorts, 3-5 LinkedIn, 3-5 Facebook, 1-3 X/day. This is correct strategy, but presented all at once it risks making the reader feel like you're throwing a lot of numbers at them. Consider whether an accordion or tiered reveal improves the read.

Prompt for Claude:
In the Channel Strategy section, the posting frequencies are listed for every platform simultaneously. This is strategically correct but can feel overwhelming to a reader who isn't yet bought in. Add a brief framing sentence at the top of this section before the channel details: "The volume below is intentional — content is the engine, and the engine needs to run consistently. But every piece of content has a job: it educates, builds trust, or converts. Nothing is published without purpose." This one sentence transforms the volume from "overwhelming" to "we know exactly what we're doing."
Polish #10

SEO article targets need one sentence of rationale

The targets — 50 articles pre-waitlist, 120 by onboarding, 150+ by launch — are credible numbers but they appear without context. A reader unfamiliar with SEO content strategy won't understand why these numbers matter. One sentence about what search authority means for Alfred would help.

Prompt for Claude:
In the SEO strategy section, add one framing sentence before the article production targets. The sentence should explain why content volume matters: Alfred's search opportunity is in long-tail financial wellness terms ("energy loyalty penalty," "why broadband prices increase after 12 months," etc.) — terms with real search intent but currently weak or misleading results. Being the most trusted source for these searches means Alfred captures consumers at exactly the moment they're feeling the problem Alfred solves. Keep it to one or two sentences. Then the article targets follow naturally from it.

What Not to Change

These elements are genuinely strong. Don't touch them:

  • The Inertia Files six-episode structure — The six-episode arc building to Alfred's reveal in episode 6 is creative and strategically sound. The "earned reveal" is the best idea in the whole proposal.
  • The five brand voice principles (except renaming #4 as noted above) — Collectively, this is a distinctive and precise brief. "No exclamation marks, no body copy emoji, no hard sell" is the kind of specificity that builds trust with a founder.
  • The equity-aligned model positioning — "An agency optimises for their own continuity. Bear Witness optimises for Alfred's growth." This is the sharpest line in the pitch. Keep it exactly as is.
  • The three trust archetypes for influencers (Consumer Protectors, Home Experts, Smart Money) — This framing is smart and will resonate with Tom. Most agencies just say "influencers." You've defined a strategy.
  • The channel partner co-marketing idea — Leveraging M&S, Waitrose, David Lloyd for co-branded social reach is a genuine strategic insight that most people would miss. Keep it prominent.
