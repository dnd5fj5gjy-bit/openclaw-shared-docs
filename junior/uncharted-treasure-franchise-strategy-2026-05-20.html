Franchise Strategy — Confidential

Uncharted Treasure

Bear Grylls x FCS  /  TNS  —  May 2026

The Core Idea

Uncharted Treasure is a multi-platform adventure franchise built on one enduring premise: the world's most dangerous places are hiding the world's most extraordinary gifts. Every expedition ends with a discovery — a landscape, a people, a piece of knowledge, a survival insight — that the audience carries with them long after the credits roll.

This franchise belongs to Bear Grylls. It doesn't depend on any single co-creator, channel partner, or platform deal to survive. It is the IP. The digital series feeds the TV series. The TV series feeds the social. The social keeps the audience engaged and hungry for the next expedition. That flywheel runs indefinitely.

Why This Franchise Stands Alone

The FCS strategy rightly benchmarks against what Coyote Peterson built with Brave Wilderness. That's a real success story. But tethering the Bear Grylls franchise to Coyote's brand is structurally wrong:

  1. Dependency Risk
    If Coyote steps back, restructures, or pivots, Bear's franchise loses a key pillar. Uncharted Treasure should be able to stand in any room without a co-signer.
  2. Brand Ceiling
    Coyote's audience is wildlife-specific. Bear's mandate — survival, adventure, human endurance, discovery — is far broader, with a legacy audience that spans demographics. Attaching to a niche channel undersells the franchise potential.
  3. Franchise Equity
    Every collab that puts Coyote's name on Bear's content builds Coyote's IP. Uncharted Treasure should be building its own equity. Guests are welcome — they serve the franchise, not the other way around.

The right model is National Geographic, not MrBeast collab culture. NatGeo built a franchise that outlasted every presenter. The presenter became famous through the franchise. That's the goal.

Three Franchise Pillars

Pillar 01

Digital Series — "Uncharted Treasure: Expeditions"

The engine. Always on. Always feeding the machine.
  • Format: 12-20 minute episodes, cinematic style, released on YouTube and Bear's owned platforms
  • Cadence: 1 major expedition per quarter, each split across 3-4 episodes
  • Concept: Genuine Bear Grylls adventures — real risk, real environment, real discovery. The "treasure" at the end is broad: a landscape, a skill, a culture, a truth about human nature
  • Jesse's role: Episode 1 is already filmed. Jesse fronts the digital series, building his own following as the next generation of this world
  • Bear appearances: 2-3 episodes per year — protect his bandwidth, maximise his impact
  • Collab logic: Other creators guest as co-expeditioners on specific episodes. They serve the Uncharted Treasure story. The franchise absorbs their audience, not the reverse
Pillar 02

TV Series — "Uncharted Treasure"

The prestige layer. The thing that makes the franchise permanent.
  • Format: 6-8 episode season, cinematic production quality, streaming or broadcast
  • Distinction from digital: Deeper arcs, higher stakes, more narrative complexity, harder locations
  • Relationship to digital: The digital series is the funnel. Audiences who find Bear on YouTube graduate to the TV series. The TV series validates the franchise for brand partners and distributors
  • The FCS angle: This is where the Fox relationship becomes most valuable — distribution that can place a premium series on FOX, Hulu, or a streaming partner. Running Wild is the proof of concept. This scales it
  • Production identity: Separate from Running Wild. Uncharted Treasure is its own IP — its own title card, visual language, music. This is not Running Wild Season 11
  • Story structure: Each TV season follows one macro expedition across multiple chapters. The land and the human story woven together
Pillar 03

Social Content — Always-On

The glue. The thing that keeps the franchise alive between releases.

This is the most underbuilt part of any TV/digital franchise strategy. Uncharted Treasure wins here with three categories of social content:

a) Behind the Expedition

Raw, unscripted moments from each expedition — prep, travel, kit checks, near-misses, crew life. Shot on phone or handheld. Posted on Bear and Jesse's handles across Reels, TikTok, Shorts. No production overhead — this is the footage that already exists from filming the digital series.

b) Franchise Lore

"How to survive..." clips extracted from expedition footage. "What we found" reveal moments. Historical and geographical context around expedition locations. These are evergreen — can be posted anytime, forever.

c) Community Challenges

Audience sent on their own "local uncharted treasure" challenges. Best submissions featured on the franchise's social channels. Builds a UGC layer around the franchise without Bear needing to be present.

Posting cadence: Minimum 4-5 pieces per week, always in season or out. The audience should never feel like the franchise has gone quiet.

The Franchise Arc — 3-Year Build

Year 01 — 2026

Establish the digital series. Jesse fronts it. Bear appears in 2-3 episodes. 4 expeditions released. Social runs in parallel. Zero dependency on any external channel partner.

Year 02 — 2027

TV series enters production. Digital series has built the audience case. A streaming or broadcast partner picks up Uncharted Treasure Season 1. Social continues. Franchise is now cross-platform.

Year 03 — 2028

Multi-season IP. Bear has equity in a franchise that survives him. Jesse is established as the next generation. Social channels have always-on life. Franchise licenses gear, survival products, brand partnerships — independently.

Tone and Identity

What if Bear Grylls made a franchise that was actually designed to outlast him?

  • Cinematic but accessible
  • Dangerous but purposeful — every risk serves a discovery
  • Spiritual without being religious — the wild as a place of revelation
  • Generational — Bear and Jesse, now and next

The word "treasure" does real work. It promises the audience something worth having at the end. That's the contract every episode makes with the viewer.
