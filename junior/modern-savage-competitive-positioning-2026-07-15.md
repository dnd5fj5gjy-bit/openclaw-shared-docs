## The one-line read

The US organ-supplement category is **a wall of unflavoured pills sold by niche male influencers.** Modern Savage's stack — a *good-tasting flavoured powder* + protein + fruit + probiotics, fronted by a *globally famous founder* — is a format, experience and reach advantage that **no incumbent currently occupies.** We should not compete on price-per-capsule. We should own "the daily organ blend people actually enjoy drinking," priced premium, and weaponise Bear's reach against a field of sub-3M-follower founders.

**Two real risks, both managed:** (1) native organ taste is hard to mask — the whole bet rides on the flavour system landing; (2) FTC claim discipline (see our existing claims guardrail). Neither is a reason to slow down.

## Market context

- **Category size:** US/global beef-organ supplements ~**$658M (2025) → ~$695M (2026)**, heading to **~$980M by 2032 (~5.9% CAGR)**. Grass-fed sub-segment ~**$205M → ~$297M by 2032**. The beef-liver-capsule niche is growing faster (~10%).
- **The wave we're riding:** carnivore/paleo/"ancestral" going mainstream, plus a hard shift toward traceability and third-party verification. The category is moving from fringe to mainstream — which is exactly the audience the fringe brands *can't* credibly reach.
- **The category's marketing playbook is singular: founder-as-face.** Heart & Soil is Dr Paul Saladino (~2.8M IG, Rogan appearances, MD credential). Ancestral is Liver King. MK is Michael Kummer's blog. The supplement is a monetisation layer on a personal brand.
- **How the leader was built:** Heart & Soil went **$0 → ~$50M in 3 years almost entirely on ORGANIC content** — 2 podcasts/week, daily Instagram, email, then influencer seeding once the education framing was set. Paid was secondary. This is the template, and it's the template we can out-run.
- **Claims style:** structure/function only ("supports energy / immunity / hormones / nose-to-tail nutrition"), vitamin-and-mineral mechanism language. Sourcing is the proof point ("100% grass-fed and finished," NZ/Argentine, non-defatted, glass bottles).

## The whitespace — where Modern Savage wins

The incumbent field is overwhelmingly **capsule, unflavoured, clinical, and dependent on a niche male influencer.** That leaves five open lanes and one structural advantage:

1. **Format gap — the palatable daily powder.** ~90% of the category is pills. The "taste" leaders literally market *taste-free* as a feature and concede organ powder "has a strong taste." A genuinely good-tasting flavoured powder that mixes into water/smoothie removes the two biggest adoption barriers at once: swallowing 6 pills a day, and the gag factor. Our only real powder rivals are Noble Origins (polarising taste, $2.36+/srv) and Pluck (a condiment). **Nobody owns "delicious daily organ blend."**

2. **Ritual, not chore.** Capsules are something you tolerate. A flavoured scoop is a morning ritual — it slots into the coffee/smoothie habit. That reframes the product from "supplement I put up with" to "drink I actually want," which is a fundamentally better retention and word-of-mouth engine.

3. **Whole-food multivitamin, one hero SKU.** Organs + protein + fruits + probiotics = a complete daily foundation, not a single-organ pill. This undercuts Ancestral's confusing 40-SKU wall with one clear hero product (plus Mini Savage for kids and the Summit Stack for performance).

4. **Mainstream and family-friendly, not fringe-carnivore.** Incumbents skew hardcore-male carnivore with real reputational baggage (Liver King). A premium, approachable, family-safe brand — with an actual kids' SKU — captures the mainstreaming wave the fringe brands structurally cannot reach.

5. **Price headroom.** Premium powder already clears $2.36–2.63/serving (Noble Origins). We can price on taste + brand + completeness instead of racing capsules to the $0.60–1.00 floor.

6. **The founder advantage — the category's own playbook, maxed out.** Every dollar in this category is made on founder-as-face with 1–3M followers. **Bear Grylls dwarfs every incumbent founder**, and "wild / primal / resilient" maps onto ancestral nutrition more cleanly than any of them — without the carnivore-influencer controversy. This is our single largest structural edge, and it makes Heart & Soil's own organic-content growth model (which they used to hit $50M) far more powerful in our hands.

## Positioning recommendation

### Where to plant the flag
**"The primal daily blend you'll actually look forward to."** Not a pill you endure — a great-tasting scoop of nose-to-tail nutrition (organs, protein, fruit, probiotics) that becomes your morning ritual. Ancestral nutrition, made modern and made delicious.

Wedge = **taste + completeness + Bear**, against a category that is bland, single-organ, and fronted by niche figures.

### Price ladder (recommendation)
- Anchor the 28-serving adult pouch at a **premium powder tier — target ~$2.10–2.50/serving band** (i.e. roughly $59–69 per pouch), sitting *below Noble Origins' top end but clearly above the capsule floor*. This says "premium, taste-led, complete" without looking eccentric.
- **Subscription is table stakes** in this category (H&S 25% first / 10% recurring; everyone runs S&S). Run a first-order incentive + recurring discount that still protects the ~margin our financial model needs.
- Bundle logic: Adult + Mini + Summit as a "household" or "performance" bundle — mirrors H&S's bundle upsell but around *our* three-SKU story rather than 40 confusing single organs.

### Three messaging pillars
1. **Taste is the product.** Lead with it. It's the one thing the entire incumbent field cannot claim. Every piece of content should make the flavour/ritual visceral (mix shots, smoothie, morning routine).
2. **Nose-to-tail, made complete.** Organs + protein + fruit + probiotics = a whole-food daily foundation. One scoop, not a pill cabinet.
3. **Wild by nature.** Bear's world — primal, resilient, real food — as the brand universe. This is the reach multiplier and the emotional wedge the others lack.

### The go-to-market motion
Run the **Heart & Soil organic playbook, at Bear scale.** Their $0→$50M was almost entirely organic content + education + seeding. We have a founder with global reach *and* a ~1,800-strong warm waitlist. The launch motion should be: education-framed organic content (why organs, why taste matters), founder-led hero moments, creator seeding (Ku Creatives volume already running), waitlist-first launch offer. Paid amplifies; it does not lead.

### What to avoid (hard rules)
- **Do not race the capsule brands on price-per-serving.** We lose that game and it destroys the premium/taste story. Our comp set is Noble Origins, not Ancestral.
- **Do not out-carnivore the carnivores.** Approachable and mainstream beats hardcore. Leave the fringe-male lane to the incumbents; it's saturated and carries baggage.
- **Do not overreach on claims.** Structure/function only. The FTC polices *advertising* independently of DSHEA and requires "competent and reliable scientific evidence"; the Liver King / Ancestral $25M suit is the cautionary tale. Stay inside our existing claims-compliance guardrail (workspace doc, 12 Jul).
- **Do not bury the founder.** In a founder-as-face category, our founder is the biggest asset any brand in the space has ever had. Use him early and centrally.

## Regulatory guardrails (quick reference)
- Organ supplements = dietary supplements under DSHEA — no pre-market approval, essentially no clinical trials.
- Permitted: structure/function ("supports," "helps maintain"). Prohibited: disease claims ("treats," "cures") — those make it an unapproved drug.
- **FTC polices ads separately** and requires substantiation (CARSE standard). DSHEA labeling latitude does not shield ad claims.
- Precedent for the downside: Liver King / Ancestral $25M misleading-representations lawsuit.
- → Keep all launch copy conservative and documented. Cross-reference: `modern-savage-claims-compliance-guardrail-2026-07-12`.
