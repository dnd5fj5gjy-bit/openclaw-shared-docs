# Fat Cow × Modern Savage — analysis and counter-proposal

20 July 2026. On Ryan Porter's proposal of 18 July (received 05:30 today).
Prepared for Jesse Grylls.

---

## The short version

The 5% is not the problem. Clause 3, second bullet is the problem.

Ryan has asked for **the Bear Grylls name and likeness** across the naming, packaging and marketing of the co-branded range, and priced it at 5% of net sales on his own channel. There is no term, no territory, no minimum guarantee, no approval rights, no category exclusivity and no exit. That single clause is worth more than every other term in the document combined, and it is written as though it were a courtesy.

My recommendation: **take Bear out of phase one entirely.** Launch it as Modern Savage × Fat Cow. If it works, the Bear conversation is worth multiples more in twelve months, from a position of proof rather than hope.

---

## 1. What is actually on the table

Two separate deals wearing one coat.

| Leg | Mechanism | What Modern Savage gets |
|---|---|---|
| MS channel | Fat Cow supplies at 50% of RRP (£20 on a £40 tin) | Retail margin on units MS sells |
| Fat Cow channel | Royalty to MS | 5% of net sales |
| Licence | Modern Savage brand **+ Bear Grylls name and likeness** | Nothing. It is bundled into the 5% |

Ryan's covering note frames this as risk reduction: he funds production, offers low MOQs, sells at wholesale, and takes "our little bit on top." That framing is doing a lot of work. He is taking the manufacturing margin, and asking for Bear's likeness, and the "little bit on top" is the part he is drawing attention to.

## 2. The economics, per £40 tin

UK RRP £40 including VAT is £33.33 net of VAT. COGS on a 90g tallow and honey balm at low MOQ is realistically £6 to £8 landed. **That is my estimate, not his number — get the actual figure.**

**When Fat Cow sells a unit on its own site:**

- Fat Cow net revenue £33.33
- Royalty to Modern Savage £1.67
- Fat Cow retains roughly £24.66 after COGS

**When Modern Savage sells a unit on its own site:**

- MS net revenue £33.33, pays Fat Cow £20
- MS gross £13.33, before shipping (~£3.50), payment fees (~£0.70) and all marketing
- Fat Cow retains roughly £13 after COGS

Two things fall out of that, and they are the argument:

1. **On Modern Savage's own store, Fat Cow earns about the same per unit as Modern Savage does — and Modern Savage pays for every customer.** Fat Cow's £13 is clean. MS's £13.33 still has to fund acquisition, fulfilment and returns.

2. **On Fat Cow's store, Fat Cow earns roughly fifteen times what Modern Savage earns on the same unit** (£24.66 against £1.67), on a product that would carry Bear Grylls' name.

That is the whole case, and it does not require accusing anyone of anything. It is just what the numbers do.

## 3. On the dropship idea

The instinct is right, with one correction.

**Right:** dropship is clearly the correct operating model here. No inventory, no cash tied up, no MOQ commitment, no warehousing, and MS can list the range and see what actually sells before committing to anything. For a brand that has not launched yet, taking stock positions in someone else's skincare is the wrong use of cash and attention.

**The correction:** dropship normally pays the retailer *less* than wholesale, not more, because the retailer is no longer taking inventory risk. So the ask should not be "dropship *and* a bigger number." If Ryan is genuinely offering 50% wholesale, then dropship at 45% to MS is roughly value-neutral and strictly lower risk. That is a very reasonable thing to ask for and easy for him to say yes to.

**Also worth separating:** the 5% and the dropship are not the same lever. The 5% is a royalty on units Ryan sells on *his* channel. Dropship governs units *we* sell on ours. Both need fixing, but they are different arguments and should be made separately or he will trade one against the other.

## 4. What I would propose in return

### A. The licence — the only term that really matters

Take the Bear Grylls name and likeness out of phase one. Co-brand as Modern Savage × Fat Cow.

Reasoning, and this is the strategic point rather than the commercial one: if Bear's name is what sells this range, **Modern Savage learns nothing about its own pull**, at exactly the moment it most needs to find out. Bear's equity would be subsidising a third party's skincare line, in a category adjacent to but separate from what Modern Savage is. Hold it back. It is worth far more later, and it is the one thing on the table that cannot be un-given.

If Jesse wants Bear in regardless, it is priced separately and never inside a supply agreement:

- Royalty on **all channels**, not just Fat Cow's
- 10 to 15% of net sales, with a **guaranteed annual minimum** so it is not worthless if the range underperforms
- 24-month initial term, UK and EU only
- Written approval on every use of name, likeness, packaging and every claim
- Category exclusivity in tallow skincare, so Bear's name is not beside a competitor next year
- Immediate termination and name removal on any brand-safety breach

### B. Trading terms

- **Dropship for the Modern Savage channel.** MS retains 45% of net sales. Fat Cow picks, packs and ships, in Modern Savage outer packaging. No MOQ, no stock commitment.
- **Modern Savage owns the customer.** The transaction, the data and the relationship sit with MS. This matters more than a point of margin.
- If Ryan insists on wholesale rather than dropship, 50% of RRP only holds if it includes fulfilment. Otherwise it is really 40% once shipping and handling are carried by us.

### C. The royalty on Fat Cow's channel

Counter at **10% of net sales**, and define the base tightly. This is the same trap as Water2 and Nero: the base matters more than the rate.

Define net sales as gross receipts less VAT and actual refunds **only**. No deduction for marketing, affiliate commission, discounts, platform fees or shipping. At 5% on a base that permits affiliate and marketing deductions, the effective rate lands nearer 3%.

### D. Leave our marketing commitment blank for now

The document specifies Fat Cow's contribution (100k email list, affiliate push) and leaves Modern Savage's as "[ X ] launch posts." Do not fill that in until the licence and the rates are settled. It is the last thing we should concede, and right now it is the only thing we have already half-offered.

## 5. Diligence before anything is signed

- **Top Dog Now LTD, company number 12542411.** Active, incorporated 1 April 2020, no overdue filings, accounts made up to 31 March 2025. Registered office is a virtual office (International House, 12 Constance Street, E16). SIC code is **47910, internet retail — not manufacturing.** So "Fat Cow manufactures both products" almost certainly means a contract manufacturer. Ask who actually makes it, where, and to what standard.
- **Product liability insurance and recall cover** — limits and named insured. Non-negotiable if Bear's name goes anywhere near it.
- **Cosmetics compliance** — who is the Responsible Person, is there a Cosmetic Product Safety Report, and is it notified on SCPN/CPNP. The footer line "co-branded product claims to remain observational" suggests they already know claims are the sensitive area here.
- **Pull the last filed accounts** to see the real size of the business before depending on it for supply.
- **Which entity contracts.** Modern Savage Inc is a Delaware C-Corp; Fat Cow is UK. Get Raemy to confirm the contracting entity and the royalty withholding position before any licence is drafted.

## 6. My honest view on whether to do this at all

As a low-effort revenue line on dropship, with no stock risk and Bear held back: yes, worth doing. It costs little and it puts Modern Savage next to a product people already buy.

As a co-development with Bear's likeness on the packaging, before Modern Savage has even launched: no. It splits focus at the worst possible moment, it puts a tallow balm beside an organ supplement in a way neither brand needs, and it spends the most valuable asset in the room on the least proven proposition.

Ryan asked for the likeness explicitly. That tells us he wants it. That is the leverage, and it only works if we are genuinely willing not to give it.

Good to see this written down, and thanks for pulling it together quickly.

The structure broadly works for us: you make it, we sell it, nobody is holding stock they do not need to. Two things I would change, and one I want to park.

Given that takes the stock risk off us and leaves it where the manufacturing already sits, I think that lands close to where your 50% wholesale was anyway.

On the royalty for units you sell on your own channels, 5% is light for a co-branded product where half the brand is ours. We would want 10% of net sales, and net sales defined simply as gross receipts less VAT and actual refunds, without deductions for marketing, affiliate or discounting. Happy to keep the quarterly reconciliation as you proposed.

The one I want to park is the Bear Grylls name and likeness. That is not something we license inside a supply agreement, and honestly I do not think phase one needs it. Let us launch this as Modern Savage × Fat Cow, see how the balm performs on its own merits, and if it goes the way you expect we can have a proper conversation about Bear for phase two. It will be a better conversation with numbers behind it.

Two practical ones before we go further: who manufactures for you, and can you send over your product liability cover and the cosmetic safety documentation. Standard for us on anything we put our name on.

Leave our launch posts blank for now and we will firm that up once the above is settled.
