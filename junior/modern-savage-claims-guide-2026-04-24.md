**Prepared:** April 24, 2026
**Author:** Junior
**Purpose:** Legal claims framework for marketing copy, email, and product labels. US + UK.

## The Core Rule

Modern Savage sells a food product (desiccated organ meats), not a drug. That distinction determines everything about what language you can use. Cross it, and you're a drug company without FDA/MHRA approval — which is an enforcement target.

The good news: the truthful nutritional story about organ meats is compelling on its own. You don't need to make drug claims. The science is real. Say what's in it and what nutrients do — not what the product "treats" or "cures."

## UK RULES (Post-Brexit — GB Health Claims Register)

The UK uses the retained EU Regulation 1924/2006 framework. Health claims must be from the approved list and used substantially as worded.

### APPROVED claims Modern Savage can make (for the nutrients it contains):

**Vitamin B12 (liver is extremely high — 1,000%+ RDA)**
- "Vitamin B12 contributes to normal energy-yielding metabolism"
- "Vitamin B12 contributes to the reduction of tiredness and fatigue"
- "Vitamin B12 contributes to normal red blood cell formation"
- "Vitamin B12 contributes to normal functioning of the nervous system"
- "Vitamin B12 contributes to normal psychological function"

**Iron (heme iron from liver/heart)**
- "Iron contributes to normal cognitive function"
- "Iron contributes to the reduction of tiredness and fatigue"
- "Iron contributes to normal formation of red blood cells and haemoglobin"
- "Iron contributes to normal oxygen transport in the body"
- "Iron contributes to normal energy-yielding metabolism"

**Zinc**
- "Zinc contributes to normal immune system function"
- "Zinc contributes to normal DNA synthesis"
- "Zinc contributes to the maintenance of normal testosterone levels in the blood"

**Vitamin A (retinol — the form in liver)**
- "Vitamin A contributes to normal immune system function"
- "Vitamin A contributes to the maintenance of normal skin"
- "Vitamin A contributes to normal vision"

**Copper (from liver)**
- "Copper contributes to normal energy-yielding metabolism"
- "Copper contributes to the maintenance of normal connective tissues"

**Folate**
- "Folate contributes to normal blood formation"
- "Folate contributes to normal psychological function"

**CoQ10 (from heart) — NOTE:** CoQ10 does NOT have an authorized health claim in the UK/EU register. You cannot make a health claim for CoQ10 on a UK label. You can state its presence as an ingredient. You can say what it is (mitochondrial cofactor) as a factual statement, but not as a health benefit claim on the label.

### Condition for using any claim:
The product must contain the nutrient at the level specified (15% NRV per serving = "source of"; 30% NRV = "high in"). With the quantities in grass-fed liver, this is easily met for B12, iron, vitamin A, zinc.

## US RULES (FDA + FTC)

Supplements regulated under DSHEA 1994. Three claim types available:

### Type 1 — Structure/Function Claims (NO FDA pre-approval needed, just notification)

These are your primary claims in the US. They describe how a nutrient affects the body's structure or function — not disease treatment.

**Allowed examples:**
- "Vitamin B12 supports normal red blood cell formation"
- "Iron supports normal oxygen transport"
- "Zinc supports immune function"
- "Supports energy metabolism" (B12, CoQ10)
- "Supports healthy testosterone levels" (Zinc)

**Required disclaimer on label (mandatory):**
> "This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."

Must appear with each structure/function claim. FDA must be notified within 30 days of first marketing.

### Type 2 — Nutrient Content Claims (straightforward)
"High in Vitamin B12," "Excellent source of Iron," "Contains Zinc." These are fine with standard FDA definitions.

### Type 3 — Health Claims (FDA pre-approved only)
These link a nutrient to a disease. Generally not relevant for Modern Savage. Do not use without FDA pre-approval.

## THE GREY ZONE — What Competitors Do vs. What's Safe

Many organ supplement brands (Heart & Soil, Ancestral Supplements, etc.) push language that's technically aggressive. They count on not being enforced against because they're small. Modern Savage, with Bear Grylls attached, is not small. ASA (UK) or FTC (US) complaints become PR stories.

**Aggressive but common (risky for a high-profile brand):**
- "The most nutrient-dense food on earth" → unsubstantiated superlative. Fine in editorial/blog context, not on label.
- "Your body can't get this from plants" → can be challenged; use "heme iron is absorbed at higher rates than non-heme iron" instead.
- "Ancestral nutrition" → fine, it's a narrative frame not a health claim.

**Safe factual statements (not health claims, just facts):**
- "Contains Vitamin B12, Iron, Zinc, Vitamin A, Copper, Folate" → pure fact.
- "Heme iron (found in animal foods) is absorbed at 25-35% efficiency; non-heme iron (from plants) at 2-15%." → peer-reviewed fact, cite NHANES/NIH if needed.
- "Retinol is the form of Vitamin A found in animal foods. Beta-carotene (from plants) must be converted by the body." → fact, not a health claim.
- "Grass-fed, New Zealand/Australia sourced, third-party tested." → sourcing/quality claims, not health claims.
- "No synthetic additives. No fillers." → factual, fine.

## EMAIL MARKETING SPECIFICALLY

UK email marketing is regulated by ASA (Advertising Standards Authority) and follows the CAP Code — same standard as product labels for health claims. The "it's just an email" defence doesn't exist.

US email marketing is regulated by FTC. Same substantiation required as ads.

**What this means for the email sequence:**
- Email 2 says "Vitamin B12: Over 1,000% of your daily requirement. The single most common deficiency in adults over 40." → The deficiency statistic needs a source cited (can be footnote or link). The 1,000% figure needs to be accurate for the serving size.
- "Heme iron: Absorbed at 25-35% efficiency. Compare that to plant-based iron at 2-15%." → Substantiated by published literature. Fine.
- "Mitochondrial fuel your cells use for energy production" re: CoQ10 → Describing mechanism, not making a health claim. This is borderline but defensible as a factual biochemistry statement.

The Email 2 copy as written is well-positioned — it states facts and scientific context rather than making direct health claims. Keep it that way.

## PRACTICAL GUIDE FOR CALVIN

When writing any copy for Modern Savage, run each claim through this test:

| Does the copy say... | Status |
|---------------------|--------|
| "Contains [nutrient]" | Always OK |
| "[Nutrient] contributes to [normal body function]" | OK if on GB approved list |
| "[Nutrient] supports [body function]" | OK in US with DSHEA disclaimer |
| "Treats / cures / prevents [disease]" | NEVER. Not negotiable. |
| "[Nutrient] is essential for [biological process]" | Factual statements OK, disease link not |
| "Most people don't get enough [nutrient]" | OK if statistic is sourced |
| "The only product that..." / "Best in the world" | Risky — needs substantiation |
| "Ancestral / primal / whole food" | Fine — narrative, not a health claim |
