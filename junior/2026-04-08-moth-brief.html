The Opportunity in One Line

MOTH is the biggest name in UK premium RTD cocktails — growing fast, eyeing the US, and looking for a face that gives them credibility with a mainstream male audience. Bear is the most natural fit they could find. The question isn't whether this makes sense — it's what structure works for BGV.

Why This Makes Sense for Bear

MOTH's target customer is 28-45, male-skewing, values quality, drinks socially rather than to excess. That's Bear's mainstream UK audience almost exactly. MOTH aren't looking for a footballer or a reality TV name — they want someone who stands for something real. Bear's "earned it" positioning (SAS, expeditions, genuine credentials) gives MOTH a credibility story that no other ambassador could.

The B Corp angle also matters. Bear increasingly aligns with values-driven brands — MOTH being B Corp certified means there's no awkward question about whether this fits the Grylls brand identity. It does.

On the US side: MOTH is actively trying to crack America. Bear just launched Running Wild's new season on Fox. The overlap is obvious — Bear can be the face that gives MOTH US credibility while MOTH gives Bear a premium drinks association in the UK. Both sides benefit from the other's market.

Possible Partnership Structures

Equity + Ambassador Fee Most Interesting
Bear takes a small equity stake (1-3%) in exchange for a multi-year ambassador role. Fee is lower but the upside is real if MOTH exits or hits a Series B. Given they've already raised £4.6M and are eyeing US, the trajectory is positive. Bear's involvement also makes the company more fundable. This is the Ryan Reynolds / Aviation Gin model — and it works.
Paid Ambassador Deal
Annual fee (likely £150-400K range for a brand at this stage) for content, appearances, and social. Cleaner, no complexity, no risk. Less upside. Good if the relationship isn't there yet for equity conversation.
Content Partnership / Series
Bear and MOTH co-produce a piece of content — adventure series, challenge video, social campaign. One-off or seasonal. Good as a starting point to test whether the creative chemistry works before a longer commitment.
Co-branded Limited Edition
A Bear Grylls x MOTH limited run — a flavour, a pack design, a campaign. Generates press and tests the audience overlap before a bigger deal. Lower commitment, measurable results.

What to Ask / What to Find Out

01What does Rob actually want from Bear — content, distribution, credibility for a raise, US entry, or all of the above? His answer tells you how much leverage you have.
02What's their current revenue run rate and where are they targeting to be at the next raise? This tells you whether the equity conversation is worth having.
03Do they have US distribution secured or is it just ambition? If they're serious about the US, Bear's Running Wild Fox platform is a real asset to them — price it accordingly.
04What other ambassadors or investors have they approached? If Bear is their first call, that's a very different conversation than if they're shopping around.
05Who else is on the cap table? ProVen VCTs and Beringea are credible UK VCs — worth knowing if there are any strategic investors already in.

Things Worth Knowing

Rob Wallis has a background in sales and digital marketing (pre-MOTH). He's described as founder-led, values the culture he's built. He'll respond well to genuine enthusiasm for the brand rather than a transactional pitch.

MOTH's name stands for "Mix of Total Happiness" — their positioning is joy, not performance. That's a slightly different lane to Bear's usual territory, which creates interesting creative tension: the man who survived the wilderness, unwinding with a MOTH. Contrast is the story.

They did a national UK campaign in summer 2025 (75% more marketing spend than 2024). They're investing in growth aggressively, which means they need the ambassador story to land before the next raise.
