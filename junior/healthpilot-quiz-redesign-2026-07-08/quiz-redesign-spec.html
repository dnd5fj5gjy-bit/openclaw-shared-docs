HealthPilot — Quiz Redesign

Junior · 8 July 2026 · Confidential — never publish · pairs with the 27-second walkthrough video and the clickable prototype

Two changes you asked for: stop gating everyone through the intake, and make the quiz far richer and genuinely premium — collecting a lot more, feeling interactive rather than laborious, and routing people to revenue without ever feeling like a sale. This version adds two more of your calls: premium, tactile sliders, and wearable connectors (Oura, WHOOP, Fitbit, Garmin, Apple Health) that pull real sleep, HRV and activity into the assessment. The prototype and video show it all working end to end. This is the spec behind it.

Entry (dashboard-direct + a quiet nudge) · wearables sync real health data in · the insight-first report

1. The entry: guidance offered, never forced

Logged-in patients land on their dashboard as normal — book a test, book a consult, see results, all one tap. The assessment is offered by a calm prompt ("Not sure where to start? Take a short, private assessment — no pressure") rather than standing between the patient and their account. New/public visitors still get the full branded funnel. The quiz becomes a helpful concierge, not a tollgate.

2. What makes it feel premium, not laborious

  • One idea per screen, mostly taps. Cards and chips, not text fields. A four-minute assessment that feels like ninety seconds.
  • A considered, human tone. Serif headlines, generous space, plain-spoken copy ("Ten quiet minutes, in confidence"). It reads like a good clinic, not a marketing funnel.
  • It reads between the lines. After the symptom screen, a reflective beat plays back what it heard ("Low energy and lost drive tend to travel together — and there is usually a reason"). The patient feels understood, not interrogated — and it earns the questions that follow.
  • Depth is a choice. "Full picture (4 min)" or "Just the essentials (90 sec)" up front; every sensitive question is skippable; answers already given let later ones be skipped.
  • Progress you can feel. A quiet progress bar and honest "about 2 min left / almost there" pacing.

3. Clever ways it gathers information

  • Priority-first framing. "If we could put one thing right first, what would it be?" tells us how to lead the report before we ask anything clinical.
  • An either/or trade-off. "Certainty about what's going on" vs "the quickest route to feeling better" — a snap choice that reveals whether to sell the panel on answers or on speed, without asking a sales question.
  • Expressive, tactile scales. Energy and stress as premium sliders - a large serif readout, a gradient-filled track and a weighted thumb, with human labels ("Up and down through the day") - captured in a second, felt like a considered instrument rather than a form field.
  • It can answer for you. Connect a wearable and real sleep, HRV, resting heart rate and activity flow straight in - so we ask less and know more (see section 5).
  • Permissioned sensitivity. The hormonal/sexual-health screen names the awkwardness and hands over control ("skip anything you'd rather not say") — which is exactly why people answer it.
  • A profile that builds. Every answer becomes a pill on the final report ("Energy: low", "No recent bloods"), so the patient sees their own picture assemble — the reward for sharing.

4. Routing to revenue without the upsell feeling

The report leads with insight, not an offer. It reflects the patient's own words back first and earns trust. Only then does it suggest a next step — and the blood panel is framed as "the single fastest way to turn how you feel into a clear reason," chosen visibly because of their answers. It is a logical conclusion, not a pitch.
  • Reason, then recommendation. Every suggestion is tied to something the patient said ("Chosen because of your symptoms and no bloods on file").
  • Give before you ask. Free sleep & recovery guidance sits alongside the paid steps, so the report is a gift, not a checkout.
  • Soft social proof, explicit no-pressure. "Most men who start here begin with the baseline panel — but there's no rush, and nothing here is automatic."
  • Price stays out of the emotional moment. The recommendation is about care and clarity; cost lives one click later on "See what's included".
  • The account is the conversion. "Create your account to unlock your full report" — the lead is captured at the peak of relevance, which is exactly what the clinic's claim-conversion metric measures.

5. Wearable connectors — real data, fewer questions

The single biggest upgrade to the "gather more, ask less" goal. Early in the flow the patient can connect a device; we pull the signals that matter and fold them straight into the assessment and the report. It reads as premium and effortless, it makes the output far more accurate, and it deepens the data moat for the clinic.

How it feeds the assessment

  • Auto-fills and cross-checks. If Oura shows 6h broken sleep, we can skip the sleep question and instead say "your ring shows your sleep is more broken than most" — fewer taps, more authority.
  • Sharper recommendations. Low HRV + low readiness + the reported symptoms is a much stronger case for a baseline panel than symptoms alone — and it shows in the report ("your wearable backs this up").
  • Ongoing, not one-off. Once connected, the clinic can track trends between assessments — the retention and re-assessment loop gets real longitudinal data behind it.
Build reality: the experience and data model are designed and prototyped here. The live connectors themselves are a Felix build — each vendor needs a registered developer app and OAuth credentials, plus a normalisation layer that maps every source into a common set of intake signals (sleep, HRV, resting HR, activity, readiness). Apple Health specifically must be read on-device through the patient app, not server-side. Sensible sequence: Oura + WHOOP first (cleanest APIs, most relevant men's-health signals), then Fitbit/Garmin, then Apple/Google via the app.

7. How it ships

  • The quiz is a new flow version authored in the existing section/field schema — buildable through the owner console's flow editor or seeded, no engine changes. The richer question set is what fixes the "two same-goal patients get identical output" problem the review flagged.
  • The entry nudge is a small front-end addition to the patient dashboard (a dismissible prompt component that deep-links into the assessment). I can prototype this in our sandbox where I have access.
  • The reflective/report copy is model-authored from the answers — the AI layer already exists; this just gives it much more to work with and a premium tone to match.
  • The wearable connectors are the one genuinely new engineering piece — per-vendor OAuth apps plus a normalisation layer (section 5). Design and data contract are ready; it's a Felix build, sequenced Oura/WHOOP first.
