# Wildernests — Investor-Grade 3D Feedback Guide
**For:** Jesse Grylls — Tuesday sprint, task 7
**Context:** Calvin is delivering 3D rotation renders. Gunnar (BCG X) is building the UHNWI pitch video from these. Your feedback is the quality gate before that video is made.
**Goal:** Renders that stop a Tattarang-level investor mid-scroll and make them want to own one.

---

## What "Investor-Grade" Actually Means

A consumer sees a nice house. An investor sees a viable product. The render has to work for both simultaneously.

For institutional/UHNWI investors, a render is evidence of three things:
1. The product is real (not vaporware)
2. The developer has taste
3. The experience is worth the premium

If the render doesn't communicate all three in 5 seconds, it won't make it into a BCG deck.

---

## The Seven-Point Checklist

Work through these when Calvin sends the renders. If the answer is no — that's your feedback.

### 1. Context: Is the home inside nature, or floating in space?
**What to check:** Is the home placed in a real landscape — trees, hills, water, light — or is it in a white/grey void?

**Why it matters:** Wildernests' entire proposition is the location. A render that strips out the landscape strips out the product. A Tattersall-grade investor needs to feel the seclusion, not imagine it.

**Feedback if wrong:** "The render needs to be set in an actual environment. Show me the home in the trees / beside water / on the hillside. The landscape IS the product."

---

### 2. Materials: Can you tell what it's made of?
**What to check:** Look at the walls, deck, roof, windows. Do you know immediately if it's cedar, stone, glass, steel, composite? Or does it look plastic/generic?

**Why it matters:** UHNWI buyers buy materials, not shapes. A render that looks like a Minecraft block — even at a distance — signals budget. A render where you can almost feel the texture of the wood signals quality.

**Feedback if wrong:** "The materials aren't reading. The cladding looks flat — can we see the grain/texture more clearly? The deck should look like it's real timber."

---

### 3. Light: Is it alive?
**What to check:** Is there a light source — sun angle, time of day, shadows? Does the interior glow warm, or is it flat and empty?

**Why it matters:** Light is how renders communicate "this is a place you'd want to be." Cold, overcast, flat renders feel like an engineering drawing. A late-afternoon sun hitting cedar cladding, warm light visible through floor-to-ceiling windows — that's what closes deals.

**Feedback if wrong:** "The lighting is flat. Can we get a golden-hour version? And can we see warm light coming from the interior — it should feel inhabited."

---

### 4. Scale: Is there a human?
**What to check:** Is there a person — even a silhouette — visible anywhere in the render?

**Why it matters:** Without a human figure, investors and buyers consistently underestimate the size of a structure. A single person standing on the deck tells you instantly: this is a full home, not a cabin. It also makes it emotionally real.

**Feedback if wrong:** "Add a figure on the deck or approaching from the path. One person. Just to give scale."

---

### 5. Arrival: How do you get there?
**What to check:** Is there any sense of how you approach the home? A path, a bridge, a clearing, a dock? Or does the home just start?

**Why it matters:** UHNWI buyers think about the arrival experience obsessively. The moment you leave the car / the boat / the helicopter and approach your home — that's the emotional peak. If the render doesn't hint at this, it's missing a beat.

**Feedback if nice-to-have:** "Could one render show the approach — looking toward the home from 20 metres out? That's the money shot for a video."

---

### 6. Night render: Does one exist?
**What to check:** Is there a dusk or night version — home lit from within, dark trees behind it?

**Why it matters:** Night renders are where architectural imagery becomes genuinely emotional. Warm amber light glowing from large windows against a dark forest is the single most powerful still image in luxury property marketing. Gunnar will almost certainly need this for the pitch video.

**Feedback if missing:** "We need a night version. Home lit from within, dark surroundings. This is critical for the video."

---

### 7. Aerial/drone view: Can you see how it sits in its landscape?
**What to check:** Is there a bird's-eye or 45-degree elevated view that shows the full home in its environment?

**Why it matters:** This is the view investors use to assess site planning, privacy, density possibilities. It's also the most dramatic single-image for a pitch deck cover.

**Feedback if missing:** "Can we get a drone angle — elevated, showing the home and maybe 50-100m of surrounding landscape?"

---

## What NOT to Comment On

- Structural or engineering aspects (Calvin isn't an engineer)
- Floor plan efficiency (not Calvin's brief for this deliverable)
- Colour choices unless something looks actively wrong
- Interior furniture/finishes unless you've been sent interior renders

---

## Language to Use With Calvin

Be specific. "This looks great" is useless feedback. Good feedback:

- "The materials need to read more clearly — I can't tell what the cladding is from a distance. Can we see the texture?"
- "Love the context but the light feels flat. Can you give me a golden-hour version of the exterior?"
- "This is the one I'd use for the deck cover — the aerial. Can we get a higher resolution version?"
- "Missing a night render. That's the one Gunnar will want for the video."

---

## The Single Most Important Question

When you've looked at all the renders, ask yourself:

**"Would someone who has a second home in Aspen, a yacht in the Med, and a flat in Mayfair — would they stop scrolling for this?"**

If yes, the renders are good enough.
If no, go back with the relevant checklist point above.
