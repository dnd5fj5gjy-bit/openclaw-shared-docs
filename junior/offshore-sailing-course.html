Sailing Terms

Before anything else — the language. Every sailor needs these terms immediately. Learn them now and everything else will make sense.

The rear of the boat. "Aft" means towards the stern.

"Abeam" = directly to the side, at 90° to the boat's length.

The centre of the boat, roughly halfway between bow and stern.

⛵ Parts of the Boat
Hull
The body of the boat
The main structure — everything from the keel to the deck edge.
Deck
The floor you walk on
The horizontal surface on top of the hull. The foredeck is the front section.
Cockpit
Where you sail from
The sunken area at the stern where the helm, winches, and sheet controls are.
Helm
Steering position
The wheel or tiller used to steer. "The helm" also means the person steering.
Keel
Underwater fin
The heavy fin below the hull. Prevents the boat sliding sideways and provides ballast to keep it upright.
Mast
The vertical spar
The tall pole from which the sails are hung. Supported by the standing rigging.
Boom
Horizontal spar
Attached to the base of the mast. The foot of the mainsail runs along it. It swings across when you tack or gybe — watch your head.
Gunwale
Top edge of hull
Pronounced "gunnel." The rail running along the top of the hull sides.
Bilge
Lowest interior point
The lowest part inside the hull where water collects. The bilge pump removes it.
Tiller
Alternative to a wheel
A lever directly connected to the rudder. Push right to go left. The opposite of a car's steering.
🌬️ Wind Terms
Windward
Towards the wind
The side from which the wind is blowing. The "weather side." Upwind is in the windward direction.
Leeward
Away from the wind
Pronounced "loo-ard." The sheltered side, downwind. A "lee shore" is a coastline you're being pushed towards — dangerous.
Heel
The boat's lean
When the wind pushes the boat over sideways. Heeling to starboard = leaning to the right.
Leeway
Sideways drift
The sideways movement of a boat caused by wind. The keel minimises it but doesn't eliminate it.
Knots
Speed at sea
One knot = one nautical mile per hour. A nautical mile = 1,852 metres. Never say "knots per hour" — knots already means per hour.
Nautical mile
Distance at sea
1 NM = 1,852m (about 1.15 land miles). One minute of latitude = one nautical mile — useful for chart reading.
💡 A tiller steers opposite to a car — push the tiller to port (left) and the bow turns to starboard (right). Takes a little getting used to. With a wheel, it's like a car: turn right to go right.
📋 Quick Reference — Commands You'll Hear
"Ready about"
Prepare to tack
Crew gets ready. Helm waits for "Ready" before calling "Lee-oh!" and turning.
"Lee-oh" / "Tacking"
Tacking now
The helm is turning the bow through the wind. Release and re-trim the headsail.
"Gybe-oh" / "Gybing"

The stern is going through the wind. Control the boom — it's about to cross.
"Ease" / "Sheet in"
Let out / pull in
Ease = let the sheet out. Sheet in = pull it in. Simple but used constantly.
"Stand by"

A manoeuvre or action is coming. Pay attention and prepare.
"Man overboard!"
Person in water
Shout it immediately. Everyone has a role. Never take your eyes off the person in the water.
⚠️ "Watch your head!" always means the boom is about to cross. Duck immediately — the boom moves fast and is at head height. This is one of the most common causes of crew injury.
Module 02 of 06

Wind & Points of Sail

Understanding wind is the single most important skill in sailing. Everything else follows from it.

🌬️ True Wind vs Apparent Wind

True wind is the actual wind blowing across the water — what you feel when standing still.

Apparent wind is what you feel on the boat when it's moving. It's a combination of true wind and the wind created by your forward motion. Apparent wind always comes more from ahead than true wind when sailing.

⚡ The sails respond to apparent wind, not true wind. This is why instruments show apparent wind angle — and why you trim sails based on what the telltales tell you, not what you think the wind is doing.

As the boat speeds up, apparent wind shifts forward. As you slow down, it shifts aft. This is why a fast yacht like a skiff can "sail faster than the wind" on a broad reach — the apparent wind is nearly abeam even though the true wind is behind.

🧭 Points of Sail

The angle between your boat's heading and the wind direction determines your "point of sail." Each requires different sail trim.

📐 The "no-go zone" is roughly 45° either side of the wind direction. Point too close to the wind and you stall. To make progress upwind you must tack back and forth (zigzag).
🔄 Tacking vs Jibing

Tacking means turning the bow through the wind. You go from close-hauled on one tack to close-hauled on the other. Relatively safe — the sail crosses from one side to the other under control.

Jibing means turning the stern through the wind. The boom swings across — often fast and with force. A controlled gybe is fine. An accidental gybe can be dangerous. Never stand in the path of the boom.

⚠️ An uncontrolled gybe (accidental gybe) is one of the most common causes of crew injury offshore. Always know where the wind is relative to your heading. When running downwind, keep slightly to one side of dead downwind — never directly dead run for long periods.

Tack: The side from which the wind is blowing. Starboard tack = wind over the starboard (right) side. Starboard tack has right of way over port tack.

🌊 Wind & Weather Basics

Beaufort Scale: The standard measure of wind speed at sea. Force 0 is flat calm. Force 4 (11–16 knots) is a pleasant sail. Force 6 (22–27 knots) is strong breeze — experienced crew required. Force 8 (34–40 knots) is gale — well offshore, reef early and sail conservatively.

💡 Reef early, shake out late. It is always easier to add sail than to reef in a rising wind. If you're thinking about reefing, do it now.

Veering = wind shifting clockwise. Backing = wind shifting anticlockwise. A veering wind in the Northern Hemisphere often indicates improving weather; backing can signal a deteriorating situation.

Sails & Trim

Sails are wings. Understanding how they generate power — and how to control it — is what separates a sailor from a passenger.

🪁 Types of Sails
Mainsail
The primary sail
Attached to mast and boom. Controls most of the boat's steering force.
Jib / Genoa
Headsail
Forward of the mast. Jib is smaller (within foretriangle). Genoa overlaps the mast.
Spinnaker
Downwind sail
Large, ballooning sail for broad reach and running. Symmetric or asymmetric.
Gennaker
Reaching sail
Asymmetric. Easier to handle than a spinnaker. Better for reaching angles.
Staysail
Inner headsail
Set inside the jib. Used offshore in heavy weather for balance.
Storm Sail
Heavy weather
Small, heavy-duty. Used in Force 7+ conditions. Always have one on board.

🎯 Sail Trim — How to Get It Right

Sails work like aerofoils. When trimmed correctly, air accelerates over the leeward (downwind) face and creates lift — just like an aircraft wing. The boat is pulled forward and slightly sideways. The keel resists the sideways force, so you go forward.

Telltales are small ribbons of wool or ribbon attached to the sail. They're your most important trimming tool.

🔑 The basic rule: ease the sheet until the sail just starts to luff (flutter at the front), then sheet in slightly until it stops. That's the sweet spot.

Upwind trim: Sails sheeted in hard. Aim to have the sail as flat as possible while maintaining flow. Traveller centred or slightly to leeward.

Downwind trim: Sails eased right out — close to 90° from centreline on a run. Boom should be out as far as possible without pressing against the shrouds.

💡 When in doubt, let it out. Easing the sail slightly and finding the luff point costs nothing. Over-sheeted sails (pulled in too tight) are a very common beginner mistake and significantly slow the boat.
🌀 Reefing

Reefing means reducing the sail area when the wind increases. The golden rule offshore: reef early. A reefed boat is more comfortable, safer, and often not significantly slower.

When to reef the mainsail: When you're consistently heeling more than 25–30°, when the helm is heavy and fighting you, or when the forecast shows wind building beyond Force 5.

⚠️ A heavily heeled boat is not faster — it's less efficient. Excessive heel increases leeway (sideways drift), makes the helm heavy, and exhausts the crew. Reef and sail comfortably.

Slab reefing: The most common system. A reef line pulls the boom down and reduces the mainsail by pulling the clew aft and down to the boom. First reef typically reduces sail area by about 25%.

Rolling away the jib: Most modern boats have a roller-furling headsail. Ease the sheet, pull the furling line — the sail rolls around the forestay. Simple and fast.

Lines & Ropes

On a boat, ropes are called lines. Knowing their names, what they do, and how to use them safely is fundamental.

🪢 Rope Terminology at Sea
Sheets
Control sail angle
Mainsheet, jib sheet. You trim these to set the sail angle.
Halyards
Raise the sails
Main halyard, jib halyard. Named from "haul yards" — the spars they pull up.
Topping Lift
Holds boom up
Supports the boom when the mainsail is lowered. Don't forget to ease it when sailing.
Kicker / Vang
Controls boom angle
Pulls the boom down to flatten the mainsail and prevent it rising.
Backstay
Supports the mast aft
Often adjustable — tighter backstay flattens the mast and sail for upwind work.
Forestay
Supports mast forward
The jib or genoa is attached here. Critical — if this goes, the mast goes.
Shrouds
Support mast sideways
Cap shrouds (top) and lower shrouds. Check tension regularly — loose is dangerous.
Mooring Lines
Docking lines
Bow line, stern line, springs (prevent fore/aft movement).
⚓ Essential Knots

🔵 Bowline — The King of Knots

Use: Making a fixed loop that won't slip. Attaching sheets to sails, tying fenders, securing a line to a ring or cleat where the loop won't tighten under load. The most useful knot in sailing.

Method: "The rabbit comes out of the hole, around the tree, and back down the hole." Make a small loop (the hole), pass the working end up through it, around the standing part (the tree), and back down through the loop. Pull tight.

Key feature: Will not jam under load. Will not slip or loosen. Can always be untied even after heavy loading.

🟡 Figure-Eight — The Stopper Knot

Use: Preventing a line running through a block, clutch, or fairlead. Tie one at the end of every sheet and halyard to stop them disappearing aloft or through the deck.

Method: Make a loop, pass the working end over the standing part, back under, then through the original loop. It looks like a figure 8.

Key feature: Simple, reliable, never jams. Every sheet and halyard end should have one.

🟢 Round Turn & Two Half Hitches

Use: Tying a line to a post, ring, cleat, or any fixed point where it must hold under load but needs to be released. Good for tying fenders to rails, mooring to posts.

Method: Take a full round turn around the object (two wraps), then make two half hitches around the standing part.

Key feature: The round turn takes the load while you complete the hitches. Secure and adjustable.

🔴 Cleat Hitch

Use: Securing any line to a cleat — halyards, docking lines, sheets. The cleat hitch is used dozens of times every day on any boat.

Method: Take a full turn around the base of the cleat (the horns). Figure-eight across both horns. Finish with a locking hitch — twist the working end under itself before laying it across. Tug firmly.

⚠️ Never add more than one locking hitch. Too many turns and the line jams and cannot be released quickly in an emergency. One full turn, one figure-eight, one locking hitch. That's it.
🎛️ Using Winches Safely

Winches multiply your pulling power. They are essential for hauling in sheets and halyards under load — but they are also one of the most common sources of injury on boats.

Clockwise wraps
Always clockwise
Line wraps clockwise around a winch drum. Always. Wrong direction = line slips or overrides.
Minimum wraps
3 turns
Three wraps is enough for most loads. Add a 4th for very heavy loads.
⚠️ NEVER wrap a line around your hand, wrist, or body when it is on a winch. If the load surges, the winch will take your hand with it. Always tail (hold) the line but never wrap it around any part of your body.

Ovride: When a loaded line wraps on top of itself on the drum, jamming. To clear: use a loop of spare line to take the load, then free the override. Don't panic — stay calm and systematic.

Easing a loaded sheet: Keep wraps on the drum. Use the friction of the drum to ease the line slowly. Never just release it — it will fly out uncontrolled.

Self-tailing winches: Have a built-in grip at the top to hold the line. Still need wraps. Still never wrap round your hand.

💡 Coil your lines neatly and stow them. A coiled line runs free cleanly when needed. A tangled line (called a "spaghetti") can jam at exactly the wrong moment — during a tack, gybe, or emergency.

VHF Radio

Your VHF radio is your lifeline offshore. Knowing how to use it correctly — and in an emergency — can save lives.

📻 VHF Basics

VHF (Very High Frequency) radio operates line-of-sight. Range is typically 20–50 nautical miles ship-to-ship, more ship-to-shore. It is the primary means of distress communication in coastal and offshore sailing.

📡 Channel 16 is the international distress, safety, and calling channel. It must be monitored at all times at sea. All boats fitted with a VHF must monitor Ch 16. Think of it as the emergency frequency — never have music or anything else playing instead of it.

DSC (Digital Selective Calling): Modern VHF radios have a dedicated DSC controller. Press and hold the red distress button for 5 seconds and it automatically transmits your MMSI number (your vessel's ID), your GPS position (if connected), and a distress signal on Ch 70 — the digital distress channel. Coast guard receives it automatically. This is faster and more effective than a voice Mayday in many cases.

Your MMSI number (Maritime Mobile Service Identity) must be registered before you use the radio. It is the VHF equivalent of a phone number — unique to your vessel.

🏖️ Calling a Marina

To call a marina on VHF: switch to Ch 80 (or the marina's specified channel). Press transmit and say:

"[Marina name], [Marina name], this is yacht [your vessel name], on channel 80, over."

Wait for response. They will ask your vessel name, where you're coming from, number of people, arrival time, and length of vessel. Have this information ready.

💡 Call the marina 30–60 minutes before arrival to book a berth and get navigation information. Don't wait until you're at the entrance.

Safety at Sea

Safety is not optional offshore. Understanding the equipment, procedures, and mindset that keeps a crew safe is the most important thing you'll learn.

🦺 Lifejackets & Harnesses
150N Lifejacket
Offshore standard
Auto-inflate on water entry. Will turn unconscious person face-up. Required offshore.
100N Lifejacket
Inshore
Will keep conscious person afloat. Not offshore standard.
Harness
Clip to the boat
Tether attaches here. Keeps you on the boat — better than being in the water.
Combined
Lifejacket + harness
Most modern offshore jackets combine both. Check yours has a harness attachment point.
🦺 Wear your lifejacket. The most common reason people drown offshore is they weren't wearing one when they went in. There is no second chance. Clip on before going on deck in rough weather, at night, or any time you'd be in trouble alone in the water.

Check your lifejacket: the gas cylinder should be secure and the green indicator showing armed. Check the oral inflation tube is accessible. Rearm after inflation with the correct gas cylinder size.

🪝 Jacklines & Tethers

Jacklines are webbing or wire lines rigged along the deck, from bow to stern. You clip your tether to them so you can move around the deck without falling overboard.

Tethers attach from your harness to the jackline. Most good tethers have two clips and two lengths — a short (1m) and a long (2m). The short keeps you close to the boat; the long gives more movement.

🔗 The golden rule: one clip on before the other clip off. When moving from one jackline to another, always clip the second tether before unclipping the first. You should never be unclipped and moving on deck offshore at night or in rough weather.
⚠️ Position your jackline attachment point carefully. If you fall overboard on a tether that's too long, you can end up dragging in the water alongside the hull — which is dangerous in itself. Short tether when working on deck. Clip to strong points.

Strong points for tether clips: the mast base, cockpit strongpoints (often u-bolts), through-deck fittings. Never clip to stanchions, guard rails, or anything that can bend or fail under bodyweight load.

🌊 Man Overboard (MOB)

MOB is one of the most critical emergencies at sea. Speed and organisation are everything. Practice this before you need it.

1. Alert
"MAN OVERBOARD"
Shout it immediately. Keep eyes on the person in the water — never look away.
2. Act instantly
Throw buoy NOW
Throw the horseshoe buoy and any floating equipment immediately. Don't wait.
3. Press MOB
GPS / VHF
Hit MOB button on chartplotter — marks position. Also hit DSC distress if needed.
4. Designate
Eyes on person
One crew member's ONLY job is to point at the person in the water. Never take eyes off them.
5. Return
Controlled approach
Figure-8 or reach-and-tack. Approach upwind, come alongside. Engine on.
6. Recover
Get them aboard
Use the boarding ladder, or a loop of line. A person in cold water may be unable to help themselves.
⚠️ A conscious person in cold water (below 15°C) may become incapacitated in 30 minutes or less. Cold water shock can cause cardiac arrest in seconds upon immersion. Speed of recovery is critical.

After recovery: Treat for hypothermia — insulate, remove wet clothes, warm the core (not the limbs first). Seek medical advice via VHF.

🔦 Safety Equipment on Board
EPIRB
Emergency Position Indicating Radio Beacon
Satellite distress beacon. Activates manually or automatically on water contact. Sends GPS position to MRCC. Register it.
PLB
Personal Locator Beacon
Personal EPIRB. Worn by individual crew. Activates if they go overboard. Highly recommended offshore.
Flares
Visual distress signals
Red handheld (within 3nm), red parachute (night, distance), orange smoke (day, air rescue). Check expiry dates.
Life Raft
Last resort
Only abandon ship when sinking is certain and imminent. Stay with the boat as long as possible — it's easier to see than a raft.
Fire Extinguishers
At least two
One in galley, one at engine access. Dry powder or CO2. Know how to use them.
Bilge Pump
Manual & electric
Know where the manual bilge pump is and how to use it. Electric pumps can fail. Manual doesn't.
📋 Safety briefing: Before every offshore passage, brief ALL crew on: where flares are, where the EPIRB is, MOB procedure, VHF use, fire extinguisher locations, and how to issue a Mayday. Even experienced sailors need briefing on an unfamiliar boat.
🌙 Night Sailing

Offshore passages often involve night sailing. The rules don't change — the vigilance required increases.

Navigation Lights
On at dusk
Red (port/left), Green (starboard/right), White (stern + masthead). Know what you're seeing on other vessels.
Watch system
3 hours on, 3 off
Keep a proper watch rota. Fatigue kills. Never sail offshore without a watch system.

Reading lights on other vessels: Red on left, Green on right — you're seeing them head-on. Green only on your left side — they're crossing right to left, they have priority. Red only — they're crossing left to right, you have priority.

💡 Give yourself 20 minutes to adapt to darkness before going on watch. Don't use white light — it destroys night vision. Red light torches preserve it. Keep your eyes moving — a stationary target at night is easy to miss.
