## 1. Website — "Longevity" Claim

**Current language:** "Empowering You for Longevity & Family Health"

**Risk:** "Longevity" implies extending lifespan. The FTC considers claims about extending life to be health claims requiring substantiation — and under current FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance (2022), "longevity" claims need clinical evidence or must be accompanied by clear qualifications.

**The problem isn't the idea, it's the unqualified word.** Bear's brand is genuinely about living fully and lasting longer — that's not a false claim. But a regulator looking at "longevity" as a lead claim on a supplement site will want clinical backing.

**Suggested rewording:**
- "Built for strength. Designed for life." (descriptive)
- "Real food. Built to last." (aspirational, not medical)
- Or qualify: "Supporting vitality and family health" (structure/function, FDA-acceptable)

**Who needs to fix it:** CJ or Felix (website copy). Simple swap before launch.

## 3. Email Sequence — One Line to Reword

**Email 2 (The Science), current text:**
> "The fatigue people report from B12 deficiency is measurable and reversible."

**Risk:** Mentions "B12 deficiency" — a diagnosable medical condition — and implies the supplement treats it ("reversible"). This crosses from nutrient education into implied disease treatment. Under FTC guidance, claims that reference a deficiency state and a remedy are disease claims.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "B12 depletion is measurable. When the body gets it back in the form it recognises, the difference is felt quickly."

This retains the message (B12 depletion is real and correctable) without referencing a diagnosable deficiency or implying treatment.

**All other email sequence language reviewed and is clean.** The B12/iron/CoQ10/vitamin A educational language is factual, nutrient-based, and uses proper structure/function framing. No disease claims found elsewhere in the sequence.
