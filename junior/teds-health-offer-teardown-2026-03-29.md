# Ted's Health Offer Teardown & Repositioning Brief

**Date:** 2026-03-29  
**Prepared by:** Junior

## Executive takeaway

Ted's should not try to beat Numan or Manual at being a prettier direct-to-consumer front end. The better play is to become the **operator behind clinics, pharmacies, and niche men's-health brands** that want the economics, treatment breadth, and conversion mechanics of a modern telehealth funnel without building the stack themselves.

The market signal is clear: leaders win by combining **low-friction online consultation, subscription economics, discreet fulfilment, clinician trust markers, and category expansion**. Ted's opening is to productise those capabilities into a partner-ready backend offer rather than spend heavily trying to out-brand larger incumbents.

## What the leaders are doing right

### 1. They sell outcomes, not prescriptions
- Numan leads with confidence and symptom relief: stronger erections, better sex, treatment that suits you.
- Hair-loss offers focus on prevention, regrowth, and visible change over time.
- Messaging stays consumer-simple even when the underlying clinical stack is complex.

**Implication for Ted's:** partner materials should promise business outcomes for clinics just as clearly: faster launch, higher conversion, recurring revenue, lower ops burden.

### 2. They make the consultation step feel almost weightless
- Repeated CTA pattern: **Get started** / **free online consultation** / answer simple questions.
- Friction is hidden until the user has committed psychologically.
- The perceived journey is: quick assessment -> clinician review -> discreet delivery.

**Implication for Ted's:** the backend offer must include a turnkey intake and triage flow clinics can drop in immediately.

### 3. They use subscriptions to lift LTV and predictability
- Numan and Lloyds both foreground repeat supply and subscription-style convenience.
- Lloyds explicitly mixes one-time and subscription options with price incentives.
- Convenience messaging matters almost as much as price.

**Implication for Ted's:** every partner pitch should frame recurring scripts, repeat fulfilment, and continuity care as the core profit engine.

### 4. They stack trust aggressively
- CQC / licensed pharmacy / clinician bios / UK regulation show up everywhere.
- Review volume and social proof are not decoration; they reduce medical anxiety.
- Discreet packaging, UK clinicians, and support access are repeated reassurance signals.

**Implication for Ted's:** the Ted's partner package needs a standard trust layer: regulatory copy blocks, clinician-profile module, pharmacy credentials, FAQ templates, and packaging language.

### 5. They expand from one wedge into a broader men's-health wallet
- Numan bundles ED, hair loss, diagnostics, weight loss, low testosterone, supplements, and consultations.
- Manual is also clearly broader than one treatment category.
- The category winner is not a single-product business; it is a men's-health operating system.

**Implication for Ted's:** position the business as a multi-condition backend that can launch with one wedge but expand into adjacent profitable pathways.

## Specific strategic conclusion

Ted's should be packaged as:

> **The operating system for modern men's-health clinics**  
> Launch or upgrade ED, hair loss, diagnostics, TRT-support pathways, and repeat-prescription revenue without building the telehealth stack yourself.

That positioning is stronger than trying to say:
- "we're another online men's clinic"
- "we have great treatments too"
- "we're like Numan/Manual but smaller"

Those are weak positions. The backend / white-label operator angle is sharper and more defensible.

## Recommended offer architecture

### Offer 1 — Launch-in-a-box
Best for independent clinics, pharmacies, and specialists that want to add a men's-health revenue line quickly.

Includes:
- branded intake flow
- online consultation and triage workflow
- prescribing and clinician-review process design
- pharmacy / fulfilment coordination
- discreet packaging language and patient comms templates
- 2-3 starter pathways: ED, hair loss, and one diagnostic or hormone pathway

### Offer 2 — Conversion and retention engine
Best for clinics already offering men's health but losing revenue through bad UX and poor repeat economics.

Includes:
- funnel and landing page rewrite
- treatment comparison pages
- subscription / repeat cadence design
- abandoned-checkout and reactivation flows
- patient FAQ and objection handling
- KPI dashboard: consultation start rate, approval rate, first-order conversion, repeat rate

### Offer 3 — Full white-label backend
Best for ambitious operators that want the economics of a telehealth brand without building operations internally.

Includes:
- complete operational layer
- category roadmap and launch sequencing
- partner onboarding pack and SOPs
- clinician / pharmacy network coordination
- diagnostics integration plan
- retention CRM and upsell structure
- commercial reporting and margin model

## The 7 messaging lines Ted's should use

1. **Launch a profitable men's-health service without building the backend from scratch.**
2. **Turn one-off prescriptions into recurring patient revenue.**
3. **Give patients a modern digital journey while your clinic keeps the brand relationship.**
4. **Start with ED or hair loss, then expand into diagnostics and broader men's-health pathways.**
5. **Use a clinically safe, discreet, regulation-aware operating model from day one.**
6. **Reduce operational drag across intake, triage, fulfilment, support, and repeat scripts.**
7. **Own the patient brand; let Ted's run the machinery underneath.**

## Simple ROI frame for partner conversations

A clinic does not need huge volume for this to matter.

Example:
- 150 qualified men's-health leads / month
- 20% first-order conversion = 30 paying patients
- £55 blended gross profit / initial order = £1,650
- 60% retained into repeat cadence = 18 recurring patients
- £35 monthly gross profit each on repeats = £630 monthly recurring gross profit layered on top

That makes the pitch tangible: Ted's is not just software, it is a recurring revenue engine.

## Risks Jesse should keep in mind

- If Ted's public-facing trust signals are weak, partner sales get harder. Regulatory and operational credibility need to be visible.
- If the offer sounds too bespoke, sales cycles will drag. Productise it.
- If Ted's lacks clean case studies, partner proof will be thin. Even one credible pilot matters.
- If fulfilment / clinical operations are not truly turnkey, the white-label promise will break under load.

## My recommendation

The next best move is to stop talking about Ted's like a clinic and start selling it like **infrastructure with revenue attached**.

Numan and Manual prove the consumer demand. Lloyds proves convenience and product breadth convert. Ted's does not need to outspend them in D2C. It needs to package the winning mechanics into a partner-ready operating model and sell that to clinics, pharmacies, and niche brands that want speed, compliance, and recurring revenue.
