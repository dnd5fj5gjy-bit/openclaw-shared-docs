## What Twyn Actually Is

Twyn (Twyn Holdings Ltd, UK incorporated 2018) is an interactive storytelling platform. Tim Levy describes it as "powering the future of conversational media." Their pitch to Bear: an "interactive MasterClass" model where audiences make choices that shape the narrative — the same format as You vs. Wild on Netflix, but on Twyn's platform rather than licensed to a third party.

Bear has already proven this exact format works. You vs. Wild (Netflix, 2019) was Netflix's first interactive non-fiction series. It ran to multiple episodes across two seasons. The demand was demonstrated. Twyn is effectively asking Bear to anchor a new version of that model, but on a proprietary platform where Bear is a stakeholder rather than just a talent-for-hire.

Tim Levy: serial entrepreneur, also runs Kyosei Collection (luxury real estate). He's built and sold companies before. This is a platform play — he wants Bear as the anchor creator who gives Twyn credibility and audience access at launch.

## What the Deal Is Worth to Bear

**Bear's actual assets in this negotiation:**
1. **Proven interactive content demand.** You vs. Wild exists. Bear didn't just hypothesize this would work — he demonstrated it at Netflix scale.
2. **24.8M followers globally.** Twyn has no audience without a creator. Bear IS the launch strategy.
3. **The interactive survival format is uniquely his.** No other creator has Bear's combination of credibility, narrative arc, and audience-tested interactive appeal.
4. **Twyn needs this more than Bear does.** Bear can license interactive rights to Netflix again, or sell to Amazon, Apple, or any number of streaming platforms. Twyn has one shot at landing an anchor creator. That's leverage.

This is NOT a "talent deal" where Bear gets paid a flat fee. This is a founding creator partnership. Price accordingly.

## Jesse's Position Going Into the Negotiation

**What to push for:**

**1. Equity: 10-15%**
Bear is the anchor creator who makes Twyn credible. Without him, Twyn is a platform without content. Typical founding creator equity on early-stage platforms: 5-15%. Given Bear's follower base and proven track record, argue toward the top of that range. If Tim pushes back below 10%, counter with higher revenue share.

**2. Revenue share: 60-70% of Bear's content revenue**
For content Bear creates exclusively for Twyn, he should receive the majority of what that content generates — subscriptions, pay-per-view, sponsorship allocated to his episodes. Compare: Substack keeps 10%, Patreon keeps 8-12%. Twyn keeping 30-40% is fair for providing the platform, distribution, and tech. Bear keeping 60-70% reflects the value creation.

**3. Production guarantee: £150-300K/year**
Bear's time and production costs need covering regardless of platform revenue. This isn't a bonus — it's the baseline that makes the deal work operationally. Without a guarantee, Bear bears all the production risk on a platform that may not scale.

**4. IP ownership: Bear retains it**
Bear's content, likeness, and survival IP belong to Bear. Twyn gets a platform license to distribute — they do not own the content. If Twyn is acquired or goes bust, Bear's content reverts to him. Non-negotiable.

**5. No platform exclusivity**
Bear cannot be locked out of other interactive content deals. Twyn gets first right of refusal on interactive content Bear produces, not exclusivity. He needs to be able to continue with Netflix, Amazon, or anyone else.

## Red Lines — Walk Away If These Are In The Agreement

1. **Twyn owns Bear's content or likeness** — licensing is fine, ownership is not
2. **Exclusivity clause** preventing Bear from doing interactive content elsewhere
3. **Auto-renewal with no equity adjustment** — if Twyn scales, Bear's equity shouldn't dilute without a mechanism
4. **No reversion clause** — if Twyn fails, Bear's content must come back to him
5. **Equity below 5%** for an anchor role — below that it's talent payment dressed as equity
6. **No approval rights** on how Bear's likeness/content is used on the platform
