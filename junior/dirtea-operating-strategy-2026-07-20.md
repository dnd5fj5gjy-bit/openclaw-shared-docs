# DIRTEA — what I would change if I came in to run it

20 July 2026. For Jesse Grylls. Private, third-party confidential, never publish.
Built from the Dirtea mail folder, Jesse's full mailbox, and the WhatsApp record Jan to Jul 2026.

---

## The headline

DIRTEA does not have a growth problem. It has a **contribution margin problem funded by debt**, and it is currently scaling the one thing that consumes cash fastest.

On the last full month we can see, October 2025: revenue £899k, CM3 contribution £179k, EBITDA minus £80k. That is a **20% contribution margin**. A DTC supplement brand should be running 40 to 60%. Every extra pound of revenue at 20% contributes twenty pence to a fixed base of roughly £2m a year, which is why the best trading month in the company's history still lost money.

Meanwhile the borrowing is moving fast. From Raemy's note on the September position: **loan £1.2m, up from £800k in August. Director loan £2.9m, up from £1.9m in August.** That is £4.1m of debt and it grew £1.4m in a single month.

That reframes everything else. The £3m asked for in July was almost certainly not growth capital.

## 1. Verify this before anything else

**Is James Khazaei the lender behind that £4.1m?**

He appears from nowhere in July 2026. There is **no email trace of him anywhere in Jesse's mailbox** - zero results across every folder. He goes directly to Bear, says the full situation has not been presented properly, and describes a next step he can take unilaterally where *"everyone loses out except me ironically."*

A step available to one party alone, triggered by shareholders refusing to fund, in which that party is made whole while everyone else is wiped, is the behaviour of a **secured creditor enforcing**. Nothing in writing says so. But if it is right, then:

- Bear's £2.45m sits behind £4.1m of debt and is the first thing to be written off
- "Do not dilute" becomes the position that loses the entire stake rather than the one that protects it
- The turnaround has a hard deadline set by someone else's patience

**Nothing below is safe to act on until we know what he holds, what security sits over it, and what the covenants are.** That is one question to Jessica or AJ and it should go today.

## 2. What is actually wrong

**Revenue is being bought.** Raemy's own line on the best month of 2025: *"when you exclude Shopify discounts this is our biggest month ever."* Discounting is doing the work. Blended CAC is £70 against a ceiling of £72 that Andy says is *"aligned against LTV"* - meaning the model only works if the LTV assumption holds, and on a discounted first order it probably does not.

**£1.8m of stock on £9.7m of revenue.** Nineteen pence of every revenue pound is sitting in a warehouse, while the business borrows to fund itself. This is the largest single pot of trapped cash and it requires nobody's permission to release.

**SKU sprawl, and two contradictory stories.** The February shareholder letter sells Creatine and Collagen as *"the next layer of the DIRTEA system."* The internal plan, which Jesse agreed with on 10 July, is to *"strip the SKUs back to just do coffee."* Shareholders are being told expansion while management privately plans retrenchment. At this revenue with this stock position, the retrenchment is right and the letter is wrong.

**The retail programme is the real danger.** Boots 100 to 300 stores, Holland & Barrett 355 stores, Vitamin Shoppe 650 stores, national grocer from January. Retail at 20% contribution margin means funding stock up front, waiting 60 to 90 days for payment, absorbing listing fees, and carrying deduction and returns risk. **They are scaling the most working-capital-hungry channel in the business while already cash absorptive and already leveraged.** This is how brands with good products die.

**Governance has stopped.** There has been no management information to shareholders since February. Five months of silence, followed by a request for £3m. Raemy asked for the loan detail in November 2025 and it was never delivered. That is the strongest card BGV holds and it has not been played.

## 3. What I would change

**Week one**
1. Get the debt schedule. Every instrument, holder, security, covenant, maturity. Refuse to discuss anything else until it exists.
2. Stop the discount ladder on subscription first orders. Revenue will fall. Contribution will rise. Measure CM3 weekly, not monthly.
3. Freeze all new stock purchasing. Build a SKU-level ageing report on the £1.8m.

**First month**
4. Cut to the coffee range plus the one supplement that actually repeats. Liquidate the rest at whatever it fetches. Cash today beats margin never.
5. Pause the retail rollout that is not yet contracted. Keep Boots and Holland & Barrett, both proven. Do not open a national grocer in January on these economics. Grocer listings at 20% CM will consume more cash than they generate for at least a year.
6. Rebuild the marketing function. Jesse's own read on 10 July was *"it is crazy to employ 4 people to do it."* Correct. One strong performance marketer plus BGV's creative capacity does more than four generalists.
7. Reinstate monthly MI to shareholders, with a fixed pack and a fixed date. Cheap, and it changes the relationship immediately.

**First quarter**
8. Get CM3 above 35%. That is the number that decides whether this company survives independently. It comes from pricing discipline, COGS renegotiation at the new lower SKU count, and killing unprofitable acquisition.
9. Convert to genuine subscription economics. The February letter claims a *"repeat-led, subscription-anchored"* model. Prove it: publish cohort retention, subscriber count and churn. If those numbers were strong they would already have been shown.
10. Only then decide about the US. It is the cash-hungriest thing on the table and the least proven.

## 4. The deal Jesse should ask for

BGV will not put in more cash. Fine - but then it must contribute something else, or "no" is just a refusal and the relationship keeps degrading.

What BGV actually has is exactly what DIRTEA lacks: brand, creative capability, marketing operations and adult governance. That is a real contribution and it should be priced.

**The proposal:** Jesse comes in operationally for a defined period, six months, with a specific remit over marketing and the cost base, plus a board seat for BGV. In exchange:

- **Anti-dilution protection on the existing 7.8%** through any restructuring, or a re-rating of the stake to reflect that the second cheque went in as a rescue at half the first valuation
- **Milestones that bind both ways** - CM3, cash, subscriber retention - with defined consequences if management misses them
- **Information rights in writing**: monthly MI by a fixed date, no exceptions
- **A say on any further borrowing**, since debt is what is actually diluting Bear economically even while his percentage stays intact

That converts "we will not fund" from a refusal into a contribution, which is the only way this stays friendly with the Salters.

## 5. The risk nobody has flagged

**Do not accept a directorship until the solvency position is clear.**

If DIRTEA is at or near insolvency - and £4.1m of debt growing £1.4m a month against a business that has never made money is at minimum a question - then a director carries personal exposure for wrongful trading under section 214 of the Insolvency Act 1986. That is personal liability, not company liability, and it attaches to anyone acting as a director whether formally appointed or not.

There is a version of this where Jesse comes in to help, the company fails anyway, and he is the one with assets worth pursuing. **Get the debt schedule and a solvency opinion before accepting any board seat or operational title.** An adviser or consultant role carries far less exposure and achieves nearly the same thing.

This is the one piece of advice in this document I would not compromise on.

## 6. On Bear's position

Bear said *"keen to sell as soon as we can"* in November 2025 and that has never been actioned into a process. In July he said *"do not move from our position of not diluting."*

Those two are compatible but they are not a strategy. If the intent is still to exit, the turnaround is the route to a sale rather than an alternative to it, and it should be run to a timetable with an exit in mind. If the intent is now to keep and fix, that is a multi-year commitment to a business that has already taken two cheques. **Worth deciding explicitly rather than by drift**, because the answer changes whether Jesse should spend six months inside it.
