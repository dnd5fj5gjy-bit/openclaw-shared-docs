## The one-line rule

**We sell food, not medicine.** A supplement may say what it *supports* in a normal, healthy body (structure/function). It may never say what it *treats, cures, prevents, or fixes* (disease). The entire compliance game is staying on the left side of that line while still sounding compelling. It is very winnable — the ancestral-supplement category does it every day — but only on purpose, never by accident.

## The two markets, in one screen

| | **United States (launch)** | **United Kingdom (follow-on, ~late Oct/Nov)** |
|---|---|---|
| Who enforces | **FTC** (advertising truth + substantiation), **FDA** (labels, disease claims, DSHEA) | **ASA/CAP** (all ads), **DBT/Trading Standards** (claims register), **MHRA** (medicine borderline) |
| Governing frame | DSHEA 1994; FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance (Dec 2022); FTC Endorsement Guides (2023) + Fake Reviews Rule (in force Oct 2024) | GB Nutrition & Health Claims Register (retained EU Reg 1924/2006); CAP Code s.13 & s.15; Human Medicines Regs 2012 |
| Structure/function claims | **Allowed** with substantiation + mandatory disclaimer + FDA notification within 30 days of first marketing | **Only claims on the GB authorised register**, worded to their approved meaning. No "general health/vitality" claim stands alone — it must ride on an authorised specific claim |
| Disease claims | **Banned** (makes product an unapproved drug) | **Banned** (makes product a medicine → MHRA licence) |
| Testimonials/influencers | Must reflect typical results; **#ad** disclosure clear & conspicuous; **fake/AI-generated reviews now illegal** | Must be identifiable as ads; endorsements can't make claims the advertiser itself couldn't |
| Mandatory disclaimer | *"This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."* | No equivalent blanket disclaimer; compliance is claim-by-claim against the register |

**The trap for us:** the US and UK draw the "acceptable claim" line in *different places*. The US lets us say "supports energy and vitality" as a structure/function claim with a disclaimer. The UK does **not** accept vague "vitality/wellness" claims at all — it only accepts specific authorised claims tied to a nutrient present in a meaningful amount. **So UK copy must be re-written from scratch, not lifted from US copy.** Do not assume one set of emails works in both markets.

## The organ-supplement landmine (why this category specifically)

Ancestral/organ brands (Heart & Soil, Ancestral Supplements, Fire & Blood) survive by being *disciplined*: they describe the traditional/nutritional rationale and let the customer connect the dots, but they do **not** put "boosts testosterone" or "cures fatigue" in the ad. The cautionary tale is **Liver King** — the reputational and regulatory heat came from over-claiming and from an authenticity blow-up, not from the product. Our brand is Bear Grylls-adjacent and press-visible; we get *more* scrutiny than an anonymous DTC brand, not less. We cannot afford a single "treats low T" screenshot.

**Two structural facts in our favour:**

1. **Our formula is nutrient-dense in nutrients that have *authorised* claims.** Bovine + bison liver, heart, kidney, spleen deliver vitamin B12, vitamin A, iron, copper, riboflavin, zinc, selenium; the formula also carries fermented K2 (MK-7); the Summit Stack carries vitamin D3, creatine, electrolytes. **Every one of B12, vitamin A, iron, copper, zinc, selenium, riboflavin, vitamin D, K2 has approved health claims on the GB register and defensible structure/function framing in the US.** That means we can make *strong, specific, legal* claims — we just have to route them through the micronutrient, not through the organ mystique.
2. **Zinc has an authorised claim that "contributes to the maintenance of normal testosterone levels in the blood."** If — and only if — the final per-serving zinc meets the significant-amount threshold (≥15% of the reference intake, GB NRV 10 mg → ≥1.5 mg/serving) **and** we use the register's exact wording, this is a *legal* way to touch the testosterone benefit our audience actually cares about. This is the single most valuable compliance insight in this document. **It is gated on the ACF/Drew spec sheet** (the same blocker holding up everything else) because we need the verified per-serving amount. Do not use it until the spec confirms the number.

## The claims lexicon — RED / AMBER / GREEN

Give this table to Calvin and Olly. It is the working reference for every piece of copy.

### 🔴 RED — never say these (any market, any channel, including influencer scripts and UGC)
- "Boosts / increases / optimises testosterone" (as an unqualified promise) — *see zinc route below for the one legal path*
- "Treats / cures / reverses / fixes" anything — fatigue, low libido, ED, low T, poor sleep, brain fog, joint pain
- "Prevents / protects against" disease — cancer, heart disease, diabetes, dementia, illness
- "Balances your hormones" / "resets your gut" / "detoxes" / "heals your gut lining"
- "Clinically proven" / "doctor recommended" / "as effective as [drug]" — unless we hold the specific study and sign-off in hand
- "Replaces your [medication / TRT / multivitamin]"
- Named-disease adjacency — do **not** put the product next to words like "IBS", "thyroid", "adrenal fatigue", "andropause"
- Before/after body transformation implying the product caused it
- Any weight-loss promise ("burns fat", "melts", "lose X lbs")

### 🟠 AMBER — allowed only with substantiation on file AND correct qualification
- Structure/function claims for US ("supports X"): permitted, but each needs (a) competent and reliable scientific evidence on file, (b) the FDA disclaimer, (c) FDA notification within 30 days. Keep a one-line substantiation note beside each claim in a master sheet.
- Any UK use of a register claim: exact approved wording only, and the nutrient must be present at the significant amount. No paraphrasing.
- "Energy" / "vitality" language: fine in US as structure/function; in UK it must be anchored to an authorised nutrient claim (e.g. B12/iron "reduction of tiredness and fatigue"), never freestanding.
- Comparative or superiority claims ("more bioavailable than", "better than a multivitamin"): need head-to-head evidence.
- "Founder's story / ancestral wisdom" narrative: fine, but the moment it implies a health outcome it inherits that claim's rules.

### 🟢 GREEN — say these freely (compelling AND compliant)
- **Compositional / true-fact claims:** "grass-fed, freeze-dried organs and bison liver," "whole-food, not synthetic," "no artificial sweeteners — sweetened with maple," "nose-to-tail nutrition your ancestors ate," "27 whole-food ingredients," "made from real food."
- **Authorised nutrient claims (route the benefit through the nutrient present in meaningful amount — confirm amounts against final spec):**
  - Vitamin B12 / Iron / Riboflavin → *"contributes to the reduction of tiredness and fatigue"* and *"normal energy-yielding metabolism"*
  - Vitamin A / Copper / Iron → *"contributes to normal function of the immune system"* (A, copper) / *"normal oxygen transport in the body"* (iron)
  - Zinc / Selenium → *"contributes to the protection of cells from oxidative stress"* and *"maintenance of normal cognitive function"* (zinc)
  - **Zinc → *"contributes to the maintenance of normal testosterone levels in the blood"*** (the gated, high-value one — spec-dependent)
  - Vitamin D (Summit) → *"contributes to the maintenance of normal muscle function"* and *"normal bones"*
  - Creatine (Summit) → the GB register authorises *"creatine increases physical performance in successive bursts of short-term, high-intensity exercise"* at ≥3 g/day
- **Sensory / lifestyle:** taste, mixability, routine, convenience, "one scoop," provenance, sustainability of sourcing (only if true).
- **Category education** (not product promise): "organ meats are among the most nutrient-dense foods on earth" — a statement about food, not about what our product does to your body.

**The move:** every time a copywriter wants to say something in RED, find the GREEN nutrient claim that gets 80% of the emotional payload legally. "Boost testosterone" → "zinc contributes to normal testosterone levels" (if spec allows) + "the nutrition your body is built to run on." Same feeling, defensible.

## Testimonials, influencers & the AI-video exposure

This is our nearest-term risk, so it gets its own section.

1. **Audit and pull the April AI "testimonial-style" videos now.** Fabricated or AI-generated testimonials are (a) squarely an ASA breach in the UK, (b) now **illegal under the FTC Fake Reviews Rule** in the US (in force since Oct 2024, civil penalties per violation). This is the highest-severity, lowest-effort fix on the board. Action owner: confirm with Calvin/Olly what is currently live on modernsavage.com and any ad account, and take it down before any spend scales. **Flag to Jesse for a decision, do not action externally without approval.**
2. **Real testimonials must reflect typical results.** If a customer says "I have so much more energy," that is fine as a genuine experience, but pairing it with an implied promise ("you will too") pulls the advertiser's own claim rules onto it. Keep endorsements experiential, not clinical. No customer may say anything we ourselves can't say (no "cured my fatigue").
3. **Disclosure is mandatory and must be clear & conspicuous.** #ad / "paid partnership" up front, not buried in hashtags. This applies to Bear, to Ross Edgley if the affiliate goes ahead, and to every seeded creator. Brief every creator with the RED list — they carry our liability.
4. **Bear's involvement raises the bar.** A high-profile, trusted endorser making a health claim is exactly what regulators make examples of. Bear's content should stay in GREEN (provenance, "food I'd feed my family," taste, philosophy) and never touch a health outcome.

## Mandatory elements checklist (label + site)

- [ ] **FDA disclaimer** on every page/asset carrying a structure/function claim: *"This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."*
- [ ] **Supplement Facts panel** (US format) with correct serving size and %DV — pulls from the ACF/Drew spec sheet.
- [ ] **Allergen declarations** — Mini Savage contains **milk** (colostrum); adult blend must declare per FDA/FSA rules. Confirm every allergen against spec.
- [ ] **"These statements…" disclaimer** placed near claims, not only in the footer.
- [ ] **FDA structure/function claim notification** filed within 30 days of first US marketing (Kylee/Weinberg Gonser to lodge).
- [ ] **Not for use by / consult a physician** cautions where appropriate (pregnancy, under-18 for adult blend, medication interactions).
- [ ] **UK, when we get there:** every on-pack and on-site claim cross-checked against the GB register with amounts meeting the significant-amount threshold; no US-only structure/function language carried over.
- [ ] **Substantiation master sheet** — one row per live claim: the claim, the market, the evidence/register basis, sign-off status. This is the document a regulator asks for; having it ready is 90% of surviving a challenge.
