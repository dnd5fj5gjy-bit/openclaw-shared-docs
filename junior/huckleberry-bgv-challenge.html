Mission 01 · the main family brand

Modern Savage: Next-Gen Growth Scout

Find the growth the current team can't see, because they're not you.

The 60-second brief

Modern Savage is a US supplement built on primal / ancestral nutrition: freeze-dried grass-fed organ meats (liver, heart, kidney and more) with protein, fruit and vitamins. The idea is to eat what our ancestors thrived on, in a form modern people will actually take. It lives in the Bear Grylls world. Range: adult daily blend, kids (Mini Savage), performance (Summit Stack). Start at modernsavage.co and go deep.

Already done — do NOT redo any of this: the business plan, the financial model, the packaging and formula, the hiring, and the current cinematic-video content series. If your idea overlaps these, you've missed the point. Build beyond them.

What to bring back

1. The fresh-eyes audit Where is Modern Savage invisible or uncompelling to under-30s, and why? Give us the honest outsider read the rest of us can't, what lands, what feels off, what a young person would actually think seeing this for the first time.
The test: tell us something that stings a little and we didn't already know. Polite and obvious is useless.
2. Native content that would actually work Design content hooks and formats that would genuinely make your generation stop scrolling and care about organ nutrition. Then mock up two or three real examples using AI, so we can see them, not just read about them. This is the raw, platform-first stuff, not a version of the cinematic series.
The test: would you stop and watch it? Would you send it to a mate? If not, it's not ready.
3. Where to show up Which emerging creators, communities and subcultures should Modern Savage be inside that we're not? Then pick your single best growth idea and give it a light cost-versus-payoff read, roughly what it'd take and what it might return.
The test: specific names and places, and a number you'll defend, not "do more TikTok."
Mission 02 · after Modern Savage

Ted's Health: The Ultimate Quiz Funnel

Design the front door to a health platform: maximum personalisation, minimum friction.

The 60-second brief

Ted's Health is becoming a white-label health platform that clinics run under their own brand, starting with men's hormone therapy (TRT) and expanding into weight loss, hair loss, blood panels and more. The front door is a health quiz. A new patient lands, takes the quiz, gets a personalised result, and flows on to the next stage (blood test, consultation, treatment). Right now the questions aren't good enough, and that's your problem to solve.

The core tension you're solving

Every question you add captures more data to personalise treatment and recommend the right supplement stack, but every extra question costs you people dropping out. The whole craft is deciding what earns its place, and sequencing it so it feels like the quiz is helping them, not interrogating them. Master that trade-off and you've designed a great funnel.

Guardrail — it's a medical product: the required clinical safety questions are fixed and owned by the medical team; you don't invent medical rules. Your job is the experience: which questions, in what order, how it feels, what data it captures, and how it keeps people moving. Design it to work across different treatments, not just one.

What to bring back

1. The full funnel map Landing → quiz → personalised result → next step. Show the whole journey and where people currently drop, and how your design keeps momentum through each stage.
2. The question set Every question, in order, with the branching logic, and a one-line reason each one earns its place, what it captures, and why it's worth the friction. Cut anything that can't justify itself.
The test: could you defend every single question on "data value vs. drop-off cost"? If a question doesn't personalise or qualify, kill it.
3. The personalisation payoff What does the user see at the end? Show how their answers turn into a result that feels genuinely tailored to them, and how it naturally tees up the treatment and a supplement stack without feeling like a hard sell.
4. Build it Don't just describe it. Prototype the quiz as a real, working interactive flow using AI, something we can actually click through.
The test: it runs, it branches, and clicking it feels better than the current one.

How both are judged

  • Additive, not a redo. The whole point is value we don't already have.
  • Original thinking. We can spot AI filler. Have a real opinion and a real eye.
  • Everything defensible. Every claim checkable, every number and every question justified.
  • Shipped and working. The mock-ups exist, the quiz clicks. Done beats perfect.
  • You can present it in 10 minutes without reading off the screen, and handle the hard questions.
