1. Executive Summary

Modern Savage is the ancestral-nutrition answer to the daily greens scoop: a family-owned, animal-based "real food" supplement system founded and wholly owned by Bear Grylls and his family. It enters a market being reshaped by the ancestral-health and "Make America Healthy Again" movements, fronted by the one globally famous figure whose entire persona — survival, resilience, primal living — is the product thesis itself.

The business sells three complementary powders on subscription, direct-to-consumer: Modern Savage (the adult daily organ + protein + whole-food blend), Mini Savage (the same standards dosed for children, with colostrum), and the BG Summit Stack (a creatine, electrolyte and longevity layer). Pricing is $69/month per pouch on subscription, or $180/month for the full three-product household stack.

The opportunity in one paragraph

Every component of the product sits on a documented growth tailwind — organ meats (carnivore/ancestral), creatine (~25% CAGR), protein (the #1 nutrient consumers seek), colostrum (gut health) and whole-food vitamins (clean-label premium). The organ-supplement category today is dominated by capsule products from influencers and clinicians (Heart & Soil, Ancestral Supplements) with no famous consumer face, and by a small powder all-in-one segment (Organised, Noble Origins) with no scaled brand engine. No competitor combines genuine mass fame with authentic ancestral credibility, a multi-SKU household system, and a kids tier. That is Modern Savage's white space.

Why this wins

  • Structural customer-acquisition advantage. In a niche where podcasts and influencers are the dominant discovery channel, Bear Grylls brings a built-in audience in the tens of millions — a CAC advantage virtually no DTC entrant has.
  • Authentic founder-category fit. Unlike borrowed-celebrity plays (Beckham/IM8) or pure hype plays (Prime), Bear's brand is the proposition. The site states it plainly: "This is not an endorsement or collaboration. It is ours."
  • Household economics. Three SKUs across adult, child and performance create natural average-order-value expansion and a household subscription that competitors with a single pouch cannot match.
  • Format and values differentiation. A flavoured powder (not capsules) in compostable packaging, animal-based (not greens), positioned as the ancestral alternative to AG1.

The central risk

Celebrity drives trial, not retention — the lesson of Prime's 76% collapse. The entire model rests on subscription repeat purchase, which means taste, mixability and felt benefit of a daily organ powder must be excellent. Management has already prioritised this: the formula was reworked through 2026 to replace artificial sweetener with maple, add berries and beetroot, with the principals judging it "better tasting than Organised." Month-2 retention is the make-or-break metric to instrument from day one.

Status & ask

The brand is built, the formula is locked and ordered, the US company is incorporated, and a compostable-packaging partner is selected. The immediate strategic decision is finalising a UK GMP manufacturer to unlock a compliant UK launch alongside the US. The venture is self-funded through Bear Grylls Ventures; this plan quantifies the capital and operating requirements to launch and scale.

2. Company & Vision

Mission

To rebuild family health on the foundation our ancestors took for granted — nutrient-dense, real food — and to make that as simple as one daily scoop, for every member of the household.

The founding thesis

Modern Savage exists to answer a problem the founders frame directly on the brand: modern food is "nutrient-vacant," depleting health rather than building it. The answer is ancestral — grass-fed red meat and organs, eggs, bone broth, raw milk, honey and fruit — delivered in a modern, convenient, palatable format. The name captures the duality: "Modern by Design, Savage by Nature."

Ownership & structure

  • 100% family-owned by Bear Grylls and family (Bear, Shara, and sons Jesse, Marmaduke and Huckleberry are named in the brand story). Parent: Bear Grylls Ventures.
  • US operating company incorporated (2026), EIN issued, US banking established — enabling US-first trade.
  • Trademarks filed in both the US and UK (UK application filed directly; currently within the standard opposition window).
  • Jesse Grylls operates the business day-to-day; Bear is founder and brand engine.
Strategic principle: Modern Savage is deliberately positioned as owned, not endorsed. This is the asset that separates it from every other celebrity supplement and from Bear's own prior endorsements in the category — and it must be protected in all messaging.

Product differentiators

  • Powder, not pills. A single palatable daily scoop versus the high pill-burden of capsule organ brands.

  • Bison as a premium signature. Most competitors are beef-only; grass-fed bison liver is a distinctive, on-brand "savage" sourcing story (subject to supply — see Operations).
  • A genuine kids product. No major organ brand offers a credible children's tier. This is a defensible, household-expanding differentiator.
  • Clean-label and sustainability. No fillers, no synthetics, made in the USA, home-compostable packaging.

4. Market Analysis

Market size & growth

  • Global dietary supplements: ~$188–210bn (2024–25), growing at roughly 9% CAGR to an estimated $275–320bn by 2030 (Grand View, Precedence).
  • United States — the launch market: the largest national market, with ~75% of adults taking supplements and a median spend of $50/month per user (CRN 2024 Consumer Survey). Modern Savage's segment skews well above that median.
  • United Kingdom: ~$3.4–4.8bn (2024–25), growing ~8–10% CAGR — roughly a tenth of the US in absolute terms but a comparable growth rate. Validates US-first, UK-second sequencing.
  • Organ-supplement sub-category: estimated ~$565–585m (2024) at ~5–6% CAGR (low-confidence; niche is thinly tracked and these figures likely understate the post-2025 cultural acceleration).

The aspirational "daily scoop" wallet

The buyer Modern Savage targets is already asked to spend $50–100/month on foundational scoops: AG1 ($79 sub, greens), IM8 (David Beckham, $79), LMNT ($39, electrolytes), Momentous (athlete/Huberman-led), Seed ($50, science-led), Ritual ($54). At $69, Modern Savage is priced competitively within this band while offering a differentiated, animal-based proposition. The cleanest positioning line: Modern Savage is the ancestral answer to AG1.

Pricing position

At ~$2.46/serving, Modern Savage sits squarely in the premium powder all-in-one band — alongside Organised and Noble Origins, and above capsule-only organ products ($1.00–1.75). The premium is justified by the all-in-one breadth and brand, but the value story must lean on breadth + brand + taste, not price. Note the 28-serving pouch (vs the category-standard 30) yields a slightly faster reorder cycle.

Lessons from the celebrity playbook

  • What works (Feastables, AG1, IM8): owned audience + subscription/ritual + genuine product rigour, sustained by relentless distribution. AG1 turned a commodity into a category king through podcast saturation and subscription lock-in.
  • What fails (Prime): $1.3bn peak → ~76% collapse. Hype without differentiation or repeat-purchase loyalty in a commodity category. "A brand cannot live on hype alone."
  • Implication: Bear's fame is a launch accelerant, not a moat. The subscription consumable model is structurally better for retention than Prime's one-off drinks — if the product earns the reorder. Bear's persona is uniquely on-thesis, which is a rarer asset than borrowed celebrity. The reputational tightrope — having endorsed Ancestral and Organised before — should be owned directly: "I tried these; here's why I built my own."

White space for Modern Savage

  1. The only celebrity-owned, authentically-fit ancestral brand.
  2. A credible kids tier (Mini) — unoccupied by any major organ brand.
  3. Powder + Bear + family system — the unoccupied corner of the powder all-in-one segment.
  4. The explicit "ancestral answer to AG1" for consumers who reject greens powders.
  5. The Summit Stack as a longevity upsell that lifts LTV and bridges into the Momentous/longevity market.

7. Go-to-Market Strategy

Sequencing

  1. US first. Larger market, the most permissive claims environment (DSHEA), the cultural epicentre of the ancestral/MAHA movement, US manufacturing in place.
  2. UK second. Gated on resolving animal-origin import/manufacturing compliance (see Regulatory). The current UK manufacturer search is the enabling step.
  3. EU/Canada as a later phase (the site already lists shipping to these regions).

Launch channels

  • Owned audience activation. Bear's channels and the family story as the primary launch engine — the lowest-CAC lever and the one no competitor can copy.
  • DTC subscription site (modernsavage.co) as the conversion hub, with the three-product bundle as the flagship household offer.
  • Live/experiential. A Modern Savage stand at the Health Optimisation Summit puts product in the hands of exactly the target buyer and seeds word-of-mouth and trade interest.
  • Podcast strategy. Bear's appearances on major health podcasts (already underway) drive the highest-intent traffic in this category. Treat podcast placement as a core, ongoing channel, not a one-off.
  • Affiliate & creator network. The category's primary growth lever. Build a commission-based affiliate programme; recruit aligned athletes/creators on commission-plus-equity where they can move volume (the model already contemplated for figures such as Ross Edgley — commission first, equity on proven delivery, mirroring the Calvin structure).
  • Email/CRM. Convert the existing waitlist at launch; build lifecycle flows for onboarding, replenishment and cross-sell.

Cross-sell & AOV expansion

The three-SKU architecture is the LTV engine: acquire on the Adult blend, expand into the household (Mini) and the performance layer (Summit Stack). The bundle at $180/month (vs $237 unbundled) is the anchor offer and the single most important driver of both AOV and retention.

8. Marketing & Content Engine

  • Always-on social. A daily cadence of short-form reels and carousels (educational, doc-style, "we built this" first-person family voice), produced by an offshore editing pod (Ku Creatives) and scheduled via Metricool. Content approvals run through Olly Westmacott to protect brand voice.
  • Education over hype. Given the category's claims sensitivity and the "show me the science" gap on the current site, lean into educational content (why organs, why whole-food vitamins, ancestral nutrition explainers). This also future-proofs against the regulatory line.
  • Founder/family content. Bear and family using the product in real life — the highest-trust, most differentiated asset, and the one that converts in this category.
  • Production assets. Recurring product/lifestyle shoots (e.g. a bison/farm shoot in Wales) to keep a fresh content bank for paid and organic.
  • Social proof build-out. Reviews, testimonials and UGC are currently absent and must be a launch priority — they are the second-biggest conversion lever after the founder.
Conversion gaps to close before scale (from the live-site audit): no live checkout yet (pre-order only), no reviews/social proof, no science/references page, no GBP/currency localisation, no visible lab-testing/certifications, and only one social channel linked. Each is a direct drag on conversion and trust; closing them is the cheapest available growth.

9. Operations & Supply Chain

Manufacturing

  • US: contract-manufactured by ACF Pharma / Lightning Labs (Miami). FDA facility registration and GMP documentation in place; this enables the US launch.
  • UK (in progress): a dedicated UK GMP contract manufacturer is being selected to enable a compliant UK launch and avoid the cost and friction of importing animal-origin product. Outreach is live to a shortlist of all-in-one UK manufacturers. Selecting this partner is the critical-path decision for UK revenue — see the UK Manufacturer Scorecard (separate deliverable) for the evaluation framework, where animal-origin (POAO) handling capability is the primary qualifying gate, not price.

Packaging

Home-compostable kraft pouches from Salt of the Earth Packaging (New Zealand), with design support from Belmark. Samples received and approved. Sustainability is both a values fit and a marketing asset; freight cost from NZ to the US manufacturer and lead times are the variables to manage.

Fulfilment

US fulfilment via a third-party logistics partner (ShipsaLot). A parallel UK/EU fulfilment path will follow UK manufacturing. Flat $9 shipping currently; revisit thresholds for free-shipping offers as a conversion and AOV lever.

Key supply-chain risks

  • Bison liver supply is structurally limited (small herds); plan for beef-primary formulation with bison as a "when available" premium signature rather than a guaranteed every-batch input.
  • Single-manufacturer dependency per region early on; qualify a backup as volume grows.
  • Cross-border freight (NZ pouches → US/UK manufacturing) introduces lead-time and duty exposure to manage.

10. Technology & Infrastructure

  • Custom commerce stack (Next.js on Vercel, Supabase) rather than off-the-shelf Shopify — giving full control of subscription logic, data and UX, and avoiding platform fees, at the cost of more in-house engineering ownership (led by Felix).
  • Payments: Stripe, gated on the IRS letter/EIN and Chase banking (the recent setup blocker now resolving).
  • Subscription engine: 28-day cadence, cancel-after-second-delivery logic, household bundle management.
  • Priorities: robust subscription billing and dunning (failed-payment recovery is a hidden retention lever), analytics instrumented for cohort retention and LTV from day one, and a reviews/UGC system.

Strategic note: the custom build is a strength for control and margin but concentrates key-person risk in the engineering function. As volume grows, document the stack and ensure continuity coverage.

11. Regulatory & Compliance

United States

  • Regulated under DSHEA as a food category — no pre-market approval, but claims discipline is mandatory.
  • Permitted: structure/function claims ("supports energy/immune function") with substantiation held before use, FDA notification within 30 days of first marketing, and the mandatory disclaimer (already on the site).
  • Prohibited: any disease (treat/cure/prevent) claim — this reclassifies the product as a drug. Train the whole content team on this line; it is the most common way brands in this category get into trouble.

United Kingdom — the chokepoint

  • No medicinal claims (FSA/MHRA boundary).
  • Products of Animal Origin (POAO): importing animal-origin organ supplements requires product/establishment certification, mandatory veterinary checks at GB entry and APHA compliance — materially heavier than for non-animal supplements. This is the single biggest regulatory hurdle and the core reason for the UK manufacturing pivot.
  • Novel foods: traditional desiccated organ is unlikely to be "novel," but specific processed ingredients/extracts (e.g. some Summit Stack actives such as NMN) should be checked market-by-market.
Compliance posture: US-first is the correct sequencing precisely because the UK animal-origin pathway needs dedicated work. Budget time and specialist compliance spend for the UK, and resolve it through UK-based manufacturing rather than import where possible.
