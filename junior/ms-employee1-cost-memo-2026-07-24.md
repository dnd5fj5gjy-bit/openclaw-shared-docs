## 1. His two quotes are not comparable as written

Both are for 20-24 hours a week. Call it 22 hours, which is 0.55 of a full-time week and 1,144 hours a year.

A 1099 number is the whole cost. A W-2 number is not: on top of salary you pay employer FICA (6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare = 7.65%), FUTA (0.6% effective on the first $7,000, so about $42), state unemployment insurance and workers' comp. For a desk role with no benefits that lands at roughly **9% on top**, call it 8.5-10.5%.

Two caveats on that 9%. State UI and workers' comp rates depend on which state Beau sits in, which we do not have on file, and new-employer UI rates are usually at the higher end of a state's band for the first few years. The Social Security wage base does not bite here because every number below is under it.

| | Low | Mid | High |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1099** monthly | $8,500 | $11,750 | $15,000 |
| **1099** annual, all-in | **$102,000** | **$141,000** | **$180,000** |
| **W-2** monthly | $6,500 | $9,250 | $12,000 |
| W-2 annual salary | $78,000 | $111,000 | $144,000 |
| **W-2** annual, all-in at +9% | **$85,000** | **$121,000** | **$157,000** |

**W-2 is cheaper than 1099 across his entire range**, by $17,000 at the bottom, $20,000 at the midpoint and $23,000 at the top, for identical work.

That gap is not him trying it on. The delta is almost exactly the self-employment tax he would carry himself (7.65% of $141,000 is about $10,800) plus an individual health plan he would have to self-fund. His two quotes are internally consistent, which is a decent signal on how he negotiates.

## 3. Take the W-2, and not only on price

The classification question has a right answer here and it is not the flexible-looking one. Employee #1, ongoing, core to the business, working hours you set, with an explicit path to full-time is close to a textbook employee under the IRS common-law control test and the DOL economic-reality test. Two of Beau's own four questions, "set hours or flexible" and "does 3 days become full-time", are themselves employee indicators. If he is in a strict state such as California the contractor route is close to unusable for a role like this.

Getting it wrong is back taxes, penalties and interest, and it is the kind of thing that surfaces in diligence on Modern Savage Inc later. W-2 is cheaper AND it is the lower-risk answer, which is a rare combination. Take it.
