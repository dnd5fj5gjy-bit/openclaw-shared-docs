What they've built:

Source is working with Fox to build the food vertical, which launched with Gordon Ramsay, Rosanna Pansino, Jolly, and others. Source Golf (launched April 6-7, 2026) Independent launch (not Fox). Roster: Bryson DeChambeau, Grant Horvat, Bryan Bros (Wes and George). Backed by Bolt Ventures. In summary: Source is real, they've built something real, and their model is working. Golf launched weeks ago. They are not speculative.

The LFG Outdoors Pitch

Owen's email to CJ lays this out clearly:

"Del brought a hybrid concept... where Source and Team Bear could explore an ownership model around outdoors. This model is Source's core business; launching cable-like networks directly with rights and capital partners in specific categories."

The concept:

  • Build an outdoors YouTube/digital network, same structure as Source Golf
  • Bear and BGV as ownership/capital partners (not just talent)
  • Content from Bear + other top outdoor creators under one network
  • Ad sales via Fox (extending the Fox Creator Studios model into Outdoors), UTA, or a direct sales team

The Anker reference: Owen says cash flow goes positive "as soon as we settle on a sales partner and move two or three Anker-like deals." Anker (power banks/charging gear) runs an active content creator program. In the outdoors context, the analogous brands would be Yeti, REI, Columbia, Arc'teryx, Garmin, STIHL. These are brands that already spend heavily on outdoor content marketing. A single network buy across the top outdoor YouTube creators is a genuinely compelling ad product.

First year costs: "very low 7 figures" — Owen's words. That means the range is likely $1M-$3M to stand up an MVP.

The Key Questions Jesse Must Answer

1. Who is expected to put in the capital? Source Golf was backed by Bolt Ventures. Who funds LFG Outdoors? Owen says "rights and capital partners" — is Bear/BGV expected to write a check? If yes, how much, and what ownership does that buy? This must be modelled before any term discussion.

2. What is Bear's actual role — talent, owner, or both? If Bear is just the network anchor (content contributor), the equity conversation is different from Bear being a capital partner. Both structures exist. Owen's pitch language ("ownership model") implies they want Bear as an owner, not just a face. That's good and potentially lucrative — but it's also more exposure.

3. Does this conflict with Natural Studios/Banijay? Bear's existing TV deals run through Natural Studios, which involves Banijay Group. Does a co-owned Bear Grylls digital network sit cleanly outside those obligations, or does it intersect? Del is already on the email chain — he's presumably aware — but Jesse needs to confirm there's no conflict before engaging.

4. What does Fox's involvement actually mean? Owen says they could use Fox for sales, extending the Fox Creator Studios model. But Fox Creator Studios is a food vertical. An outdoors network is a new vertical. Does Fox want to expand? Or is this aspirational? This needs to be tested before Jesse treats it as a given.

5. What happened to Source Golf in its first weeks? Source Golf launched April 6-7, 2026. By April 17, Owen was pitching a second network. That's either because Golf is working well and they're scaling fast — or because they want to build track record before Golf has proven anything. Jesse should ask Owen directly: what are the Golf numbers?

Deal Structure Jesse Should Aim For (if he pursues)

If Jesse decides to move forward, the structure to target:

  • Bear contributes: Content, name, audience, 1-2 years of committed video output
  • Source contributes: Network infrastructure, ad sales, Fox relationship, operational model
  • Capital from Bolt Ventures or a third party — not Bear/BGV. Bear's contribution is the brand and content, not the check.
  • Equity split: Bear 20-30% ownership in exchange for content commitment + brand value. Source holds operating equity. Capital partner holds their own slice.
  • No personal guarantee. Bear's maximum exposure = opportunity cost of content time.

If Owen insists Bear writes the capital check too, that changes the risk profile significantly. "Low 7 figures" is $1-3M — that is real money for a startup venture with no revenue history.
