The World Is Getting Harder. Good.

Most people preparing for hard times are focusing on completely the wrong things. Here's what actually keeps people alive.
BEAR GRYLLS  ·  RESILIENCE  ·  SURVIVAL
🌏
Never Give Up

My kids think it's funny that their dad is the "survival guy" and yet I'm the one who can't get the TV remote to work.

Fair point, honestly.

But lately I've been thinking a lot about what's coming. Not in a paranoid way. Just in the way that anyone paying attention probably should be. The world feels different to how it felt even five years ago. More brittle. More unpredictable. The systems we've all quietly relied on, the grid, the supply chain, geopolitical stability, they've all shown cracks in the last few years. And I think most of us have quietly noticed.

I'm not someone who enjoys that. I've got three boys. I want a stable world for them. But wishing things were simpler and preparing for when they're not are two different conversations, and I think it's time we had the second one.

"The people who come through hard times are rarely the strongest or the best equipped. They're the ones who made a decision, somewhere quiet, that they weren't going to quit."

So what does real preparedness actually look like? Because I think a lot of people have a completely wrong picture of it. They imagine bunkers, tin-foil-hat stuff, ten years of freeze-dried meals stacked in a garage. That's not what I'm talking about. Every extreme environment I've moved through has taught me the same thing over and over: the gear is almost irrelevant. It's the person carrying it that matters.

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

I've been in places that broke people. Not physically weak people, some of the strongest men I've ever been around have completely fallen apart in the field. Because survival is a head game before it's anything else. The ones who make it are the ones who have already decided, somewhere deep and quiet, that they will not stop. That's not a personality trait you're born with. It's something you build. Slowly, through hard things, on ordinary days.

Most people, when things go wrong, their mind immediately goes to what they don't have. No signal. No map. No dry clothes. The people who survive redirect that energy to what they do have. Two matches. A jacket. Half a bottle of water. That shift is the difference between panic and problem-solving. And you can train it before you ever need it.

Start small. Do something hard on purpose. Run when you don't feel like it. Have a cold shower. Sit with discomfort instead of immediately reaching for your phone. It sounds trivial but it isn't. You're building the mental muscle that will carry you when things get genuinely difficult.

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

Then there are skills. My dad taught me to build a fire on Exmoor when I was about seven. I remember the smell of it, the frustration when the kindling wouldn't catch, the satisfaction when it finally did. I didn't know then that I'd spend the next few decades building fires in places far colder and far harder. But those basic skills, fire, water, navigation, basic first aid, they never leave you. They're ancestral. The things humans have always known how to do, before we handed all of it over to infrastructure and convenience.

Five skills worth learning this year

  • Water. You last three weeks without food and three days without water. Know how to find it, move it, and make it safe to drink. Purification tablets, a filter, and knowing how to boil in the field. All three.
  • Fire. Not just a lighter. Friction fire, reading what burns, keeping a flame in wet conditions. Fire is warmth, food, and morale rolled into one.
  • Navigation. Map and compass. Sun position. How to use landmarks and stars to orient yourself. It sounds old-fashioned but it's perishable if you don't practice it.
  • First aid. Most emergencies involve injury before anything else. A basic field first aid course is genuinely one of the most useful things you can do for the people around you.
  • Shelter. Hypothermia kills quickly and quietly. Know how to improvise, insulate, and stay warm with what you've got.

None of this needs a crisis to learn. The best time to get good at these things is when there's no pressure. Take a course. Teach your kids on a camping trip. Go a weekend without the apps and gadgets. The knowledge just sits there until you need it, and when you do, it's worth everything.

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

The part people get most wrong is community. Every real survival situation I've studied or lived through comes back to the same truth: people come through hard things together, not alone. The lone survivor is a film trope. In reality, the ones who make it find each other. Shared fire. Shared food. Someone to take the watch while you sleep.

Ask yourself this: do you know your neighbours' names? In a genuine emergency, that relationship is worth more than any amount of stockpiled supplies. A doctor three streets over. A farmer who knows the land. A mechanic who can fix things without a diagnostic screen. People laugh when I say this, but I genuinely believe the most valuable person in your community in a real crisis is the local plumber. Know who he is.

"The lone survivor is a film trope. In reality, people come through hard things together."

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

One more thing on fear. There's a whole industry built around it now. The doomscrolling, the threat content, the bunker-selling. And I understand why people get pulled in, the world does feel genuinely uncertain. But fear is a terrible foundation for anything lasting.

The reason I prepare, and the reason I think you should, isn't dread. It's the opposite. It's the desire to be someone your family and your community can lean on. To be useful when things get hard rather than part of the problem. That motivation produces very different behaviour to fear. It produces training instead of hoarding. Skill-building instead of panic-buying. And a kind of quiet confidence that no amount of stockpiled tinned food can give you.

I've been genuinely cold. Lost. Exhausted in the way where you stop feeling your hands and start making bad decisions. And in those moments the thing that held me together wasn't any piece of kit I was carrying. It was something deeper. A certainty that I wasn't alone in it. That something greater was moving through all of it with me.

I know that sounds intangible. But it's the most real thing I know.

Prepare your body. Build your skills. Know your neighbours. And underneath all of it, build the kind of inner foundation that doesn't crack when things get hard. That is the real kit. And nobody can take it from you.

The world is getting harder. I think that's an invitation, not a sentence. An invitation to become someone who can meet it.

Never Give Up.
