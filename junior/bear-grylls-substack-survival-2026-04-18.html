The World Is Getting Harder. Good.

Most people preparing for hard times are focusing on completely the wrong things. Here is what actually keeps people alive.
BEAR GRYLLS  ·  RESILIENCE  ·  SURVIVAL
🌏
Never Give Up

I have made a lot of mistakes in the field over the years.

On one occasion filming Man vs. Wild, I decided at the end of a long day to do a river rapids sequence rather than call it. The river was in flood. We were tired. We hadn't scouted ahead to check the water downstream. Three mistakes, one on top of another, and by the time I rounded the next bend and saw the wall of rock where the entire power of the river was banking up into a frothing cauldron of white water, there was no going back. I got dragged under and pinned in a submerged shelf beneath the wall. Dave pulled me out by the wrist as the raft came over the top.

We all knew it had been close. Very close. Three small elements to create a disaster. And Dan said to me afterwards, as we hiked out along the river bank: 'Bear, don't forget, it's only TV. It ain't worth dying for.'

I needed to be reminded of that more often than I'd like to admit.

Big disasters are almost never one big thing. They're three or four small things piled quietly on top of each other, until suddenly you're in trouble.

Forget to recharge a battery. Lose a glove. Break your compass. Up a mountain, that combination can kill you.

I've been thinking about this a lot lately in the context of the world we're all living in. The systems most of us have quietly relied on are showing cracks. Not all at once, but one thing at a time. And I don't think enough people are paying attention.

The Wrong Kind of Preparation

There is a whole industry built around our unease right now. Bunkers. Freeze-dried food for ten years. Bug-out bags. I understand the impulse behind it. But I've spent enough time in genuinely hard situations to know that the gear is almost never what makes the difference between who comes through and who doesn't.

It is the person carrying the gear that matters.

I have watched highly equipped people fall completely apart when things went wrong. And I have watched people with almost nothing find a way through, calmly and methodically, because something inside them had already decided it was going to be all right. That quality, that quiet unshakeable resolve, is not something you either have or you don't. It is built. Slowly, through small hard things, on ordinary days.

Most people, when things go badly, immediately catalogue what they don't have. No signal. No map. Wrong kit. The people who survive tend to redirect that same energy to what they do have. Two matches. A jacket. Half a bottle of water. That shift, from scarcity to resource, from panic to problem-solving, is the real survival skill. And you can build it long before you ever need it.

Get comfortable being uncomfortable. Do hard things when there is no pressure to. Cold. Rain. Hunger. Fatigue. These things stop being enemies once you've practised sitting in them a while.

Skills That Don't Expire

My dad used to take me out on the hills above our house as a kid and we'd practise building fires, reading the weather, navigating. It never felt like training. It just felt like being outside, which I loved. But those skills have never left me, through every expedition and every situation where I needed them for real.

The knowledge that is worth building isn't exotic. It's the oldest knowledge there is. The things human beings knew how to do before we handed all of it over to infrastructure and electricity and smartphones.

Where to start

  • Water. Three weeks without food. Three days without water. Know how to find it, how to move it, and how to make it safe. Purification tablets, a filter, and knowing how to boil in the field. You need all three options.
  • Fire. Not just a lighter. Friction methods. Reading what burns in wet conditions. Fire is warmth and food but it is also morale. Morale keeps people alive. Never underestimate it.
  • Navigation. Map and compass. Sun position. Stars. Local landmarks. This is a skill that goes quiet if you stop using it. Practise it before you need it. It does not work the first time in a crisis if you have never done it in calm conditions.
  • First aid. Most emergencies involve injury before anything else. A solid field first aid course is one of the most genuinely useful investments you can make for the people who depend on you.
  • Shelter. Hypothermia kills fast and quietly. Know how to improvise, insulate, and keep warm with what is actually around you, not the kit you wish you had brought.

None of this requires a disaster to learn. The best time to build these skills is exactly when there is no pressure. Take a course. Go somewhere without the apps for a weekend. Teach your kids to build a fire. The knowledge sits there, quietly, until the day you actually need it.

K.I.S.S. Keep it simple. The basics, done well, save lives.

You Will Not Get Through This Alone

Every serious survival situation I have studied comes back to one thing. People come through hard times together, not alone.

The lone survivor is a film. In reality, the ones who make it find each other. Shared fire. Shared food. Someone else on watch while you rest. The cooperative instinct is not soft. It is the most practical survival advantage you have.

So before you buy another piece of kit, ask yourself this: do you actually know your neighbours' names? Because in a genuine extended crisis, that relationship is worth more than anything else on the list. A doctor three streets over. Someone who can fix an engine. Someone with a garden. Someone who knows the land. The skills distributed across your immediate community are remarkable, if you take the time to know who has them.

We have spent a generation convincing ourselves we can go it alone.

We can't. We were never built that way.

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

One more thing before I sign off, and to me it is the most important.

A lot of what drives the prepping world is fear. And I understand why. The uncertainty is real. The news is relentless. But fear is a terrible foundation for anything lasting. Fear makes people hoard and isolate. It leads to bad decisions made in a panic rather than clear-headed ones made in advance.

The reason I prepare is not dread. It is the same reason I trained hard before every expedition I ever went on. Not because I was terrified of what might happen, but because I wanted to be the kind of person, and the kind of team, that could handle whatever came. Useful. Capable. Not part of the problem.

That is a completely different starting point. And it builds completely different people.

I have been cold enough to stop feeling my hands. Lost enough to have no real idea which way was out. There have been a handful of moments in my life where I genuinely did not know if I was going to get home. And the thing that held me together in all of those moments wasn't the kit. It wasn't even the training, though the training mattered. It was something deeper than both. A certainty, however unreasonable it might have seemed, that I was not facing it alone.

There by the grace of God go I. I believe that. In the wild and out of it. Do everything in your power to prepare. Then trust God with the rest.

That is the real survival kit. And nobody can take it from you.

The world is getting harder. I've always thought that is less a threat and more an invitation. An invitation to become the kind of person who can actually meet it.

Never Give Up.
