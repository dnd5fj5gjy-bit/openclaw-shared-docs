The World Is Getting Harder. Good.

Most people preparing for hard times are focusing on completely the wrong things. Here is what actually keeps people alive.
BEAR GRYLLS  ·  RESILIENCE  ·  SURVIVAL
🌏
Never Give Up

People sometimes ask if I'm scared of what's coming.

I think it's the wrong question.

The world feels more uncertain than it did a few years ago. We all know it. Energy grids. Supply chains. Geopolitics. The things we grew up just assuming would always work, quietly, in the background. They've shown cracks. And a whole industry has sprung up around our unease, selling bunkers and freeze-dried food mountains and bug-out bags as if the answer to uncertainty is stuff.

It isn't.

I've spent a good part of my adult life in places where things go wrong. Badly wrong. And the one thing I know for certain after all of it is this: the gear is almost never the difference between who makes it and who doesn't.

It's the person carrying the gear that matters.

"I've seen well-equipped people fall completely apart. And I've seen people with almost nothing find a way through. Every single time, the difference was the same thing."

The Mind Goes First

Survival is a head game before it is anything else.

The people who kept going when everything was working against them, they almost never won on fitness or equipment. They won because they had made a decision, somewhere quiet and deep, that they were not going to quit. That was it. That was the whole thing.

And that quality isn't something you're born with. It's built. Slowly. Through the small hard things on ordinary days. The run when you'd rather not. The cold. The difficult conversation you stop avoiding. None of those things feel significant at the time. But they are. They're building the capacity that carries you when the stakes are real.

When things go wrong, most people's minds go immediately to what they don't have. No signal. No map. Wrong shoes. The people who survive redirect to what they do have. Half a water bottle. A jacket. A lighter with two flints left in it. That shift, from scarcity to resource, is the difference between panic and problem-solving.

You can train it long before you ever need it.

Skills That Stay With You

My dad got me building fires on Exmoor as a kid. Reading weather. Finding water. Moving without a map. It didn't feel like training at the time. It just felt like being outside, which I loved.

But those skills never left me. They were still there in the places where it was much colder and much harder than Exmoor.

The skills worth building aren't exotic. They're the oldest ones there are. The things every human being knew how to do before we handed it all over to infrastructure and convenience.

The ones that matter

  • Water. Three weeks without food. Three days without water. Know how to find it, make it safe, move it. Purification tablets, a filter, boiling. Know all three.
  • Fire. Not just a lighter. Friction methods. Reading what burns in wet conditions. Fire is warmth and food, but it is also morale. Never underestimate morale.
  • Navigation. Map and compass. Sun position. Stars and landmarks. This is a skill that goes quiet if you stop practising it. Use it before you need it.
  • First aid. Most emergencies involve injury before anything else. A field first aid course is one of the highest-value things you can do for the people who depend on you.
  • Shelter. Hypothermia moves fast and quietly. Know how to improvise and insulate with what is actually around you, not with what you wish you had brought.

None of this requires a crisis to learn. The best time to build these skills is when there is no pressure at all. Take a course. Teach your kids on a camping trip. Go a weekend without the apps and the gadgets. The knowledge sits there, waiting, until the day you actually need it.

And when you do need it, it is worth everything.

No One Gets Through This Alone

Every serious survival situation I have studied or lived through comes back to the same truth.

People come through hard things together.

The lone survivor is a film. In reality, the ones who make it find each other. Shared fire. Shared food. Someone else on watch while you rest. The cooperative instinct, not the competitive one, is what actually keeps people alive.

So before you buy another piece of kit, ask yourself this: do you know your neighbours' names? Because in a genuine extended emergency, that relationship is worth more than anything else on the list. A doctor a few streets over. Someone who knows how to fix an engine without a computer. Someone who has a garden and knows how to grow things.

We have spent a generation convincing ourselves we can do this alone.

We can't. We were never designed to.

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

One last thing, and I think it's the most important.

A lot of the prepping content out there runs on fear. The news feeds it. There is a whole industry built on selling into it. And I understand why people get drawn in, because the uncertainty is real and the fear is real. But fear is a terrible foundation. It leads to hoarding, to isolation, to decisions driven by panic rather than clear thinking.

The reason I prepare, and the reason I think you should, isn't dread. It's the opposite. It's the desire to be someone your family and the people around you can lean on. To be useful when things get hard rather than part of the problem.

That is a completely different starting point. And it produces completely different people.

I've been cold enough to stop feeling my hands. Lost enough to have no idea which direction was out. There have been moments where I genuinely didn't know if I was going to make it home. And the thing that held me in all of those moments wasn't the kit. It wasn't even the training. It was something deeper. A certainty that I wasn't alone in it. That something greater than me was moving through it all alongside me.

'Trust in the Lord with all your heart and lean not on your own understanding.' Proverbs 3:5. That is not passivity. That is the deepest form of strength I know. Do the work. Build the skills. Know your people. And trust God with the parts that are beyond you.

That is the real survival kit. And nobody can take it from you.

The world is getting harder. I think that is less a threat and more an invitation.

An invitation to become the kind of person who can actually meet it.

Never Give Up.
