The World Is Getting Harder. Good.

Most people preparing for hard times are focusing on completely the wrong things. Here's what actually keeps people alive.
BEAR GRYLLS  ·  RESILIENCE  ·  SURVIVAL
🌏
Never Give Up

Something has shifted in the last few years and I think most of us feel it, even if we can't quite name it.

The world feels less stable than it did. Supply chains. Energy grids. Geopolitics. The systems we grew up just assuming would always be there, they've all shown cracks. And people are noticing. There's a whole industry that's sprung up around it, bunkers, freeze-dried food mountains, bug-out bags. I see the ads. I read the content. And honestly, I think most of it is building on the wrong foundation.

Because the thing that keeps people alive in genuinely hard situations isn't gear.

I've seen extremely well-equipped people fall completely apart in the field. And I've seen people with almost nothing find a way through. Every single time, the difference came down to the same thing. Not what they were carrying. What was carrying them.

"Survival is a head game before it is anything else. The kit matters, but the person carrying it matters more."

So if you're thinking about how to be more prepared, what actually follows from this? I think it comes down to three things. And none of them are the things being sold to you.

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

The Mind Comes First

I've been in environments that break people. Not weak people. Some of the physically strongest people I've ever been around have completely fallen apart when things got hard. And the ones who kept going, who found a way through when everything seemed to be working against them, they almost never won on fitness or kit. They won because somewhere, in some quiet moment before things got bad, they had made a decision that they were not going to quit.

That's not a personality trait. It's trained. It's built through all the small hard things we do on ordinary days. The run we do when we don't feel like it. The cold we sit in rather than turning up the heating. The difficult conversation we have rather than avoiding. These things seem small. They aren't. They're building the muscle that carries you when things get genuinely serious.

Most people, when things go wrong, their mind immediately goes to what they don't have. No signal. No map. No dry clothes. The people who make it redirect that energy to what they do have. Two matches. A jacket. Half a bottle of water. That shift, from scarcity to resource, is the difference between panic and problem-solving. And you can build that capacity long before you ever need it.

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

Skills That Don't Expire

My dad taught me to build a fire when I was a kid. I spent a lot of my childhood on Exmoor learning to read weather, find water, navigate without a map. None of it felt particularly dramatic at the time. It just felt like being outside. But those skills never left me. They were still there when I needed them in places far colder and far harder than Exmoor.

The skills worth building aren't exotic. They're ancestral. The things every human being knew how to do before we handed all of it over to infrastructure and electricity and smartphones.

Five skills worth your time

  • Water. Three weeks without food, three days without water. Know how to find it, move it, and make it safe. Purification tablets, a filter, knowing how to boil in the field. All three.
  • Fire. Not just a lighter. Friction methods, reading what burns, keeping a flame going in wet conditions. Fire is warmth and food but it's also morale. Don't underestimate that.
  • Navigation. Map and compass. Sun position. Stars. Local landmarks. This skill goes to sleep if you stop using it. Practice it before you need it.
  • First aid. Most emergencies involve injury before anything else. A field first aid course is one of the most genuinely useful things you can do for the people around you.
  • Shelter and warmth. Hypothermia moves fast and quietly. Know how to improvise, insulate, and stay warm with what you've actually got around you.

None of this needs a crisis to learn. The best time to build these skills is when there's no pressure. Take a course. Teach your kids on a camping weekend. Go somewhere without the apps and gadgets. The knowledge just sits there, quietly, until the day you actually need it.

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

You Cannot Do This Alone

I've studied a lot of survival situations. Real ones. Disasters, collapses, extended crises. And almost without exception, the people who came through did it together. The lone survivor narrative is a film thing. Real survival is cooperative. Shared fire. Shared food. Someone else keeping watch when you can't.

So here's a more useful question than "do I have enough supplies?" It's this: do you know your neighbours' names? In a genuine prolonged emergency, that relationship is worth more than any amount of tinned food. A doctor a few streets away. Someone who knows how to fix things without a diagnostic screen. The skills distributed across your immediate community are remarkable if you actually know who has them.

This is how humans have always lived. We're the ones who decided, somewhere in the last few generations, that we could go it alone. We can't. We weren't built to.

⋅ ⋅ ⋅

I want to say something about fear before I finish, because I think it matters a lot.

A lot of the prepping content out there, and the anxiety around it, runs on dread. The news feeds it. The industry sells into it. And I understand why people get drawn in, because the uncertainty is real. But fear is a terrible foundation for preparation. It leads to hoarding, to isolation, to decisions driven by worst-case thinking rather than clear-headed planning.

The reason to prepare isn't fear. It's the same reason we trained hard before any expedition I ever went on. Not because we were terrified of what might happen. But because we wanted to be the kind of team that could handle what came. We wanted to be useful to each other, and capable, when it mattered.

That's a completely different starting point. And it produces completely different people.

I've been cold enough to stop feeling my hands. Lost enough to not know which way was out. Exhausted in the way where your thinking goes and you start making poor decisions. And the thing that held me in those moments wasn't the kit. It was something deeper. A certainty that I wasn't facing it alone. That something greater than me was moving through all of it alongside me.

Proverbs 3:5 says trust in the Lord with all your heart and lean not on your own understanding. That's not passivity. That's the deepest form of strength I know. Prepare everything you can prepare. Train everything you can train. Build your skills, know your people, do the work. And then trust God with the parts that are beyond you.

That's the real survival kit. And nobody can take it from you.

The world is getting harder. I think that's less a threat than it sounds and more an invitation. An invitation to become someone who can actually meet it.

Never Give Up.
