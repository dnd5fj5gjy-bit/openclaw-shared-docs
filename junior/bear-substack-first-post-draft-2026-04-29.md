## The Wrong Question

When I was 21, I broke three vertebrae in a parachuting accident.

I lay in a hospital bed for months not knowing whether I'd walk properly again. And I spent most of that time asking the wrong question.

Why me. Why now. Why this.

It took a long time — and a faith I didn't fully understand at the time — to realise that *why* is the least useful question you can ask in a hard moment. Why is a question for when you're safe. For the debrief, after the crisis, when you have the luxury of reflection. The moment you're in real trouble, why has never once helped anyone take the next step.

The question that helped me — in that hospital, and on every difficult thing since — was simpler.

*What's next.*

Not "what does this mean." Not "what did I do wrong." Not even "will this be okay."

Just the next right thing.

In the hospital, the answer was small. Can I move my feet today. Can I walk to the door. The stairs. The car park. Each one felt impossible before I did it. None of them were.

On Everest — three years after the accident, youngest Briton to reach the summit — the answer was the next ten steps. That was all. Not the summit. Not even the next camp. Ten steps.

I've been climbing and surviving in one form or another ever since. And the question is always the same.

---

I'm sailing north in a few days. Southampton to Norway, Iceland if the wind allows, Greenland if we're lucky. My son Jesse is running things while I'm at sea.

People ask me why I keep doing it. There it is again — why.

The honest answer is I'm not sure I know how not to. The wild keeps teaching me things I can't learn anywhere else. And the older I get, the more I think the lessons are the point, not the destinations.

What I'm bringing with me on this voyage, alongside whatever foul-weather kit Jesse's organised: a notebook. I want to write some things down properly. Things I believe. Things I've learned from being genuinely frightened and making it through.

That's what I'm going to write here.

Not survival tips. There are enough of those. I want to write about what actually matters when things go wrong. About faith, which kept me going in ways I still don't entirely understand. About my boys, and what I want for them. About the moments the cameras weren't rolling — and what those were actually like.

I don't know how often I'll write. I'll try to be honest, which is harder than most people think.

If that sounds like something you want to read, you're in the right place.

### Notes for Jesse / Bear

**Why this works as a first post:**
- Opens with the accident. Bear rarely talks about it in depth. Substack readers will feel they're getting something they haven't had access to before.
- The "why vs what's next" frame is Bear's genuine survival philosophy — it's not invented, it's what he actually teaches and believes.
- The sailing voyage grounds it in the present. It's happening now, which makes it alive.
- "Not survival tips" is important — it signals this is something different from the TV show and the books.
- Ends on "I'll try to be honest" — sets the tone for a Substack that isn't corporate.
- Signed "B" — informal, personal. The opposite of a newsletter.

**What Bear might want to change:**
- The Everest detail: accurate (youngest Briton 1998, age 23, 18 months after accident). If Bear wants to add texture, he knows the story better than anyone.
- The sailing section: should reflect where he's actually going. Jesse will know the route.
- "A faith I didn't fully understand at the time" — Bear can go deeper or lighter on faith depending on how much he wants to lead with it in post one. This version is gentle. He could make it more explicit or leave it here.

**Length:** 480 words. Right for a first post. Leaves them wanting more, doesn't overstay.

**Title alternatives:**
- "The Wrong Question" (current — works because it's unexpected)
- "What I'm Bringing North" (more personal, voyage-focused)
