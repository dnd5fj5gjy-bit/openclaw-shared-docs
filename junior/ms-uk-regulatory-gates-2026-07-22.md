# Modern Savage UK: the five gates nobody owns

**Wed 22 July 2026. Written for the UK manufacturer decision, not for the Belmark call.**

---

## THE 30-SECOND VERSION

The brief sitting in your drafts asks four UK manufacturers to build the finalised US spec, explicitly "not a reformulation". That premise does not survive contact with UK law.

Two ingredients in the BG Summit Stack cannot lawfully be sold in Great Britain in the form we make them. Separately, the UK cannot use the US pouch artwork, and Modern Savage Inc cannot legally be the name on a UK pack. None of this is fatal. All of it is cheap to fix in July and expensive to fix in October.

There is also a free test sitting here. You are choosing between four UK manufacturers, every one of whom says they will own regulatory. Not one of them has raised any of the below. That tells you something, and it costs nothing to find out.

---

## GATE 1: the Summit Stack is not sellable in GB as formulated

This is the one that changes a decision this week.

**NMN is not authorised in Great Britain.** It is a novel food, and novel foods need authorisation before sale, not after. The only application in the GB pipeline is Effepharm's (FSA reference RP-2116) and it is still in risk assessment, the first of three phases. There is no version of the calendar where that completes before a launch. Products do sit on UK shelves containing it, which is a statement about enforcement priorities rather than about legality, and it is not a position to build a Bear Grylls brand on.

**Trans-resveratrol is authorised, but not in a powder.** The GB authorisation is specific: food supplements in **capsule or tablet form**, **adult population only**, maximum **150 mg/day**, and the label must carry the statement that people using medicines should only consume the product under medical supervision. Summit is a 12 g powder. A powder sits outside the authorisation as written.

**PQQ is fine, with a condition worth noting.** Authorised, maximum 20 mg/day, adults only. That last part means UK Summit is an adults-only product regardless of what else changes.

**So:** UK Summit is a different product from US Summit. That is not a rounding error in a spec sheet, it is a reformulation, and someone has to decide who does it and when.

**My recommendation: launch the UK with Adult and Mini Savage, and park Summit.** It is the smallest of the three SKUs, it carries essentially all of the regulatory exposure in the range, and holding it back is the cheapest possible way to remove that exposure. Revisit when the NMN application lands or when someone has costed a capsule SKU. One decision, and Gate 1 disappears.

## GATE 2: the UK pack is a separate artwork and a separate print run

A GB label must carry the name and address of the food business operator responsible for the product, and that address must be in the UK, the Channel Islands or the Isle of Man. On top of that sit UK-format nutrition declaration and %NRV, allergen emphasis, the legal name "food supplement", the recommended daily portion, the do-not-exceed-the-stated-dose statement and the not-a-substitute-for-a-varied-diet statement.

**Consequence:** the run going to print at Belmark this week is a US run. If anyone suggests the same pouches cover both markets, they are wrong, and finding that out after 15,000 pouches are printed is a five-figure mistake.

**The cheap move, today:** the artwork file is open and hot this week for the first time in months. Have the designer build the UK back-of-pack variant now, off the same front-of-pack, rather than reopening the project cold in October when everyone has moved on. It costs a few hours now and a re-engagement fee later.

---

## GATE 3: who is the UK food business operator, and the VAT trap underneath it

Modern Savage Inc is a Delaware C-corp. It cannot supply the GB address the label requires. Two routes:

1. **A UK subsidiary.** Slower to stand up, but it keeps the regulatory relationship, the label and the customer inside the group.
2. **Name the UK manufacturer or importer as the FBO.** Faster, and it is what most first-time importers do. The catch is that it hands your regulatory relationship to a supplier, and switching that supplier later means a new label and a new print run.

Underneath this sits the item that is genuinely the longest lead on the UK track and appears on no plan I can find:

- An overseas company selling into the UK with stock held here is a **non-established taxable person**. The VAT registration threshold for an NETP is **zero**, not £90,000. Registration has to be in place **before the first taxable supply**, and stock landing at a UK 3PL is what triggers it.
- The fulfilment house is separately obliged under the **Fulfilment House Due Diligence Scheme** to check the VAT position of its overseas clients. This is exactly why SCEND will ask, and why they cannot simply hold stock for a Delaware entity as a favour. It is not paperwork, it stops stock moving.

**This is Raemy's, not the manufacturer's.** A question to the accountants this week costs nothing. Discovering it in October costs the launch window.

## GATE 4: the organ supply, and where it actually comes from

Your stated position is to source organs from UK farms. That is the right instinct and it removes most of what follows.

If any organ material is imported from the US instead, it is a **product of animal origin** and the controls are heavy: it has to come from an approved country and, for most categories, an establishment specifically listed to export to Great Britain; an export health certificate has to be raised on the US side; the consignment has to be pre-notified on IPAFFS and cleared at a border control post. Establishment listing is a government-to-government process measured in months. It is not something a supplier fixes with a purchase order.

Calita have already told us organs are uncommon in the UK and they may need to import. Take that as the honest answer it probably is, and read it as a reason to weight **Protein Partners** more heavily, since they already claim to source and handle grass-fed bovine organs and bison liver domestically with Halal-certified segregation. That claim is now worth verifying properly rather than taking at face value: ask which farms, which abattoirs, and whether they hold the approvals themselves.

**One inversion worth carrying into the ACF conversation.** The adult formula contains bovine lung. Great Britain permits lung for human consumption. The United States does not: USDA rules prohibit livestock lungs being saved for human food (9 CFR 310.16), with no species exception and no preparation method that lifts it. Supplement ingredients sit under a different regulator than meat, which is how organ blends containing lung are on sale in the US, but the gap between those two facts is worth understanding before 30,000 pouches are filled. Ask Andrew how the lung is sourced and classified. Ask it as a question, because it probably has a clean answer, but ask it.

## GATE 5: the website copy does not transfer

Only claims on the **GB nutrition and health claims register** may be used. Testosterone, hormonal, vitality and "primal" performance language is not on it, and botanical claims are still parked "on hold" from the EU exit. Enforcement runs through the ASA first and Trading Standards behind it, and a Bear Grylls product is a soft target for a complaint precisely because it is visible.

The workable route is the boring one: authorised claims attach to declared **nutrients**, not to blends. The K2, the D3, the minerals, those carry real permitted claims. The organ blend carries none. Build the UK page around the nutrients and the story, not around the hormone angle.

**Mini Savage is the sharper risk of the two.** Marketing a supplement for children brings CAP rules into play and separate vitamin and mineral maxima for the child population. Also: I could not settle the novel-food status of bovine colostrum from public sources, and neither should a manufacturer wave it through. Get it confirmed in writing before it is quoted.

## WHAT THIS CHANGES ABOUT THE MANUFACTURER DECISION

Stop asking four suppliers to price the US spec. Ask them to price the **UK** spec, and make them tell you what the UK spec is. Five questions, and they double as a scoring sheet:

| Question | What a good answer looks like |
|---|---|
| Which ingredients in this range cannot be sold in GB as formulated, and what would you use instead? | Names NMN and trans-resveratrol unprompted. This is the whole test. |
| Are you the FBO on the label, or are we? Whose address goes on pack? | A clear answer either way, and they understand why it matters. |
| Do you source bovine and bison organ material domestically or import it? If imported, from which listed establishments? | Named farms or named approved establishments, not "we can source that". |
| Is the vitamin and mineral blend within GB permitted forms, and within child maxima for the kids SKU? | They ask to see the quantitative spec before answering. |
| Is regulatory work inside the quote or billed separately? | Either is fine. Vagueness is not. |

**The signal to watch for:** any manufacturer who quotes the US spec without raising Gate 1 is a blender, not a compliance partner, whatever their proposal says. Jamie Christie at NutraSolve bills compliance separately from Calita, and that is a feature rather than a markup, because it means someone is actually accountable for it.

4. **Send the five questions to all four UK leads.** Same email, same day. The spread in the answers will pick the partner for you.
5. **Appoint a UK regulatory consultant before signing a manufacturer**, not after. It is the cheapest insurance in the entire launch.
