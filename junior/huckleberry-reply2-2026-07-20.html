Huckleberry,

That is a serious piece of work for day one, and better than most agency decks I have been sent. Three things in it are genuinely good, and I want to be specific because vague praise is useless to you.

"A parent's brand wearing a savage's name." Three sons named, a family portrait, a scripture signoff, and a 22-year-old sees their friend's dad's company. That is the sharpest line in the deck and it is the sort of thing only someone your age looking at it cold would say out loud. It is uncomfortable, which is how you know it is worth something.

"Shy about its best asset." The brand is called Modern Savage and it apologises for the savage part. Bull testicles get one sanitised mention and then retreat into "carefully sourced." That is a real marketing observation, not a stylistic one.

The Liver King framing. "Liver King overclaimed a persona; Modern Savage underclaims a genuinely real one." That is the whole strategic argument in one sentence, and you got there by studying a failure rather than a success, which most people never think to do.

You also sourced everything and put the shortlist caveats in yourself. That was the thing I challenged you on yesterday and you closed it properly.

Now the part that will actually make you better.

You did not send me the plan. I asked for four or five lines before you started, and you went and built the whole thing instead. It happened to be pointing the right way, so you got away with it. That is the point though: you got away with it. The habit is worth having precisely because on the day you are pointed slightly wrong, it costs you two minutes instead of three days. Do it next time even when you are certain.

The strategic hole, and it is the interesting one. You found the dad's-brand problem, and then assumed the answer is to make under-30s buy the product. But your own competitor table says the audience skew for this category is parents and families, and you correctly flagged that $69 a month has no entry point for a student budget. So which is it? Are under-30s the customer, or are they the culture that makes the actual customer want it?

Those are two completely different plans. If they are the buyer, you need a trial size and a price point, and everything else is decoration. If they are the amplifier, price does not matter at all, and the job is cultural credibility that a 40-year-old buyer sees reflected. You have written the second plan while implying the first, and never argued for either. Pick one, argue it, and be willing to tell us the brief was pointed the wrong way if that is what you conclude. That would be a more valuable answer than the one you were asked for.

Two smaller things. Twelve unverified creators is not a shortlist, it is a search result. Verify five properly, with real follower counts, engagement rates and a brand-safety check, and delete the rest. Five you can vouch for beats twelve you have to caveat.

And nothing in the deck can be proved wrong. You have proposed spending eight to fifteen thousand pounds seeding creators, but not what success looks like or what would make you stop. Put a number on it: what result at four weeks means keep going, and what result means the idea was wrong. A recommendation that cannot fail is not a recommendation, it is a suggestion.

What I would do next: answer the buyer-versus-amplifier question properly, in one page, and pick the five creators you can actually stand behind. That is a day's work and it turns a good deck into something Jesse can act on.

Send me the plan first this time.
