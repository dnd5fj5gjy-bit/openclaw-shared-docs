## Part 1 — What Email 2 Should Do

Email 1 established the "why" — Bear's lived experience, 30 years of field evidence, the observation that traditional cultures prioritized organ meats. Email 2 delivers the "what" — the science that backs it up, the ingredients, the sourcing decisions, and why each choice was made.

The psychology shift here is important. Email 1 activated identity ("I'm the kind of person who takes this seriously"). Email 2 validates the decision rationally ("here's why the smart choice is to wait for this rather than buy something else"). Research on supplement purchase decisions shows that after initial emotional interest, customers seek information to justify the purchase they already want to make. Give them that information in Bear's voice, not in a supplement catalog voice.

The risk to avoid: going too clinical. The moment this reads like a white paper or a supplement label, you've lost the Bear Grylls quality that makes this brand different from every other organ supplement. The science should feel like something Bear learned in the field and is passing on — not like something a marketing team assembled from PubMed abstracts.

**What the email must accomplish before the CTA:**
1. Name the ingredients and explain them plainly in 2-3 sentences each
2. Address the most common objection ("isn't this just desiccated liver in a capsule?") before the reader voices it
3. Tell the sourcing story in a way that closes the skeptic
4. Introduce the family angle — make it clear this isn't a gym-bro product

**The sourcing question.**

The most common question we get is: where does the animal come from?

Ours comes from New Zealand and Australia. Grass-fed. Pasture-raised. No grain finishing. The reason this matters isn't just ethical — it's nutritional. A grass-fed animal has a fundamentally different fatty acid profile from a feedlot animal. The ratio of omega-3 to omega-6 in the organ tissue is measurably different. You can't replicate that with a pill from an animal that never ate grass.

We use freeze-drying, not the high-heat desiccation most brands use. High heat destroys enzymes and some of the more fragile cofactors. Freeze-drying preserves them. It's slower, it's more expensive, and it's the reason the capsules work the way they do.

Third-party tested. Every batch. Not because we have to — because we want the result in writing.

## Part 3 — Subject Line A/B Test Notes

| Version | Subject Line | Psychology | Hypothesis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A (primary)** | `What's actually in it.` | Directness, transparency, confidence | Short. Implies other brands don't answer this. Strong for people who are skeptical of supplements. |
| **B** | `The three ingredients. Why they work.` | Specificity, education signal | Tells the reader exactly what they're getting. Higher skim-read engagement. |
| **C** | `I got the question about where the organs come from.` | Social proof, FAQ frame | Implies others asked, creates belonging. Slightly longer open. Good for audiences who value peer validation. |

**Recommendation:** A or C depending on what Email 1 A/B winner tells you about the list. If "I owe you an update" (personal accountability) won Email 1, lean A. If the longer subject won, try C.
