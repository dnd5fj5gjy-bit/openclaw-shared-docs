Content and Media Strategy — The Kids Blend

What Will Land Well

Instagram/short video: Bear in the kitchen, morning routine, kids blend next to the water glasses. "We don't make a ceremony of it." Low-key, normalising. The anti-performance-supplement aesthetic. Family in the background, not staged.

Editorial story: "The family nutrition gap: why Bear Grylls built a supplement for his sons." MindBodyGreen, Daily Mail Femail, Telegraph Magazine. The hook is not "organ meats for kids" — it's the family mission, the dissatisfaction with synthetic kids vitamins, the ancestral tradition broken by food industrialisation.

Podcast: Bear on a parent-focused podcast (not his usual audience). The School of Greatness (Lewis Howes), Motherly podcast, or a functional medicine practitioner's podcast. The kids blend story is the entry point — the Modern Savage launch is the destination.

What to Avoid

Leading with "organ meat for kids" without context. This will trigger reflexive media backlash ("Bear Grylls is feeding organ meat to children"). The story is about the gummy vitamin category failing parents — organ nutrition is the solution, positioned after the problem has been established.

Images of children with raw/visible organ meat. The product is a scoop of powder that looks neutral. Lead with the routine and the result, not the ingredients.

Health claims that approach therapeutic territory. In the UK, the Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) and MHRA draw strict lines on what can be claimed for children's supplements. Phrases like "boosts immunity," "supports brain development," or "improves growth" require clinical evidence to substantiate. The safe zone: "contains nutrients," "part of a varied diet," "grass-fed bovine nutrition."

Allergen omission. Every social post featuring the kids blend must include the milk allergen declaration. No exceptions.
