# HealthPilot in the Ted's White-Label Ecosystem
**Prepared:** 4 July 2026
**Author:** Junior
**Status:** Confidential, decision-ready. Companion to the HealthPilot business plan (same date) - that document covers HealthPilot as a standalone business; this one answers a different question: what HealthPilot does for the Ted's white-label offering.

---

## 1. The One-Sentence Answer

**HealthPilot is the missing first step of the white-label flow - "partner screens patient" - built once by Ted's, branded to every partner, and wired straight into the paid pathway.**

Here is the argument. The white-label TRT flow, as Thomas Filmer set it out on 26 March, starts with the partner screening the patient by blood test or symptoms, then the patient buys the WL TRT diagnosis package (Baseline Blood Test plus consult), then BBT, doctor consult, medication. The commercial engine of the whole offering lives in step two - the paid diagnosis package. But the flow's very first step is delegated to the partner, and the team's own emails say partners cannot do it: Filmer's stated reason for forcing Ted's own BBT panel is that "most [partners] dont actually test for all the right biomarkers we need to create a prescription." Partners have no proper screening tooling either - the platform map confirms there is no general triage or symptom intake anywhere in the stack, only the AMS and ED questionnaires.

So the offering as designed begins with a step its customers cannot perform. Every partner clinic either improvises its own screening (inconsistent, unauditable, wrong biomarkers) or does nothing and pushes cold traffic at the diagnosis package (poor conversion, wasted consults). HealthPilot closes that gap: a standardised, AI-driven, clinic-branded screening layer that qualifies the patient, produces a structured record, and routes them into the BBT plus consult wedge. Ted's owns it, the partner wears it. It also fits the offering's stated design philosophy - Filmer's 26 March principle that "the more flexibility we could give the potential partners, the more attractive the offering is… We're going to try and WL pretty much the whole flow." Screening was the one part of the flow left un-white-labelled. Now it isn't.

## 2. Five Ecosystem Jobs

**(a) Standardised partner screening feeding the paid wedge.** The BBT plus consult package is where white-label revenue starts, and the emails are explicit that partners cannot reliably fill its funnel: wrong biomarkers (Filmer, 26 Mar), no tooling (confirmed in code - no triage intake exists). HealthPilot gives every partner the same disciplined front door: symptoms in, qualified patients out, each one landing on the package Ted's actually gets paid for. Instead of hoping each clinic's receptionist or Typeform sends the right people, Ted's controls the quality of what enters its own clinical machinery - and controls it identically across every partner from day one.

**(b) Fixing the drop-off behind the May revenue fall.** May revenue fell to £5,642 from £10,058 in April, attributed in the team's own reporting to "a reduction in new patient intake" and a long lag "between initial engagement and reaching the medication phase" - the same drop-off points marked in pink on the team's Miro board. HealthPilot attacks both ends: it converts anonymous site visitors into engaged, named leads at the moment of intent (the public lead-capture flow, section 4), and its routing cards shorten the engagement-to-medication lag by putting the specific next step - buy the panel, book the consult - in front of the patient immediately, rather than leaving them to wander off between steps. This is the difference between a funnel with pink notes on it and a funnel with a hand on the patient's shoulder.

**(c) The cross-sell layer the team says is missing.** Tom, 25 March: the business is "missing some marketing opportunities/conversions from ED only to TRT… vital if we decide to push forward with the supplements too." There are 74 ED actives against roughly 50 TRT - the ED book is the larger pool and nothing moves patients across. There is no real CRM: SendGrid is transactional only, Klaviyo dormant. HealthPilot's intake data plus its recommended-actions engine is exactly this layer - an ED patient whose answers surface fatigue, low libido and poor recovery gets routed toward a testosterone panel and the TRT pathway; a wellness-goal patient gets routed toward supplements. Cross-sell stops being a marketing project nobody owns and becomes a property of the intake itself.

**(d) Clinician time - the scribe problem, solved upstream.** Filmer proposed paying £65/user/month for a regulated healthcare AI scribe (Heidi, or Tandem) to handle consultation documentation and "legal defensive" transcripts. That tool documents the consultation after it happens. HealthPilot's Clinician Brief does the equivalent work before it happens: every completed intake now produces a structured brief in clinical register - presenting concerns, relevant history, risk flags, suggested actions, a screening note - sitting in the clinician's view before the patient joins the call. The doctor starts the consult already briefed instead of spending the first ten minutes taking history. Same pain, attacked upstream, at zero per-seat licence cost, and the structured record serves the same defensive-documentation purpose.

**(e) Scalable patient due diligence.** After the Daily Mail sting, the team's stated aim was "to do as much DD on patients as we can… Easier said than done to do at scale." Manual DD does not scale; structured intake does. Every HealthPilot completion is a timestamped, auditable triage record - what the patient reported, what was flagged, what was recommended, what escalated - attached to the patient file and visible in the staff portal alongside the existing audit-CQC tooling. When the next journalist, regulator or partner's lawyer asks "how do you screen who you treat?", the answer is a system, not a shrug. For a CQC-registered business selling its registration as the core asset, that is not a nice-to-have.

## 3. The Competitive Frame

The market comparison in the team's own research is Evaro: £250/month platform fee, partner earns 2.5% (branded) to 10% (unbranded) per prescription, integration via a simple script tag. Evaro wins partners on simplicity - but Evaro owns the patient and the data, and Evaro has no blood testing capability at all.

Ted's plus HealthPilot matches the thing Evaro wins on and beats it on everything else. Same script-tag-simple integration (the public health-assessment embed drops onto a partner's site in an afternoon). But the partner keeps its own brand and its own patients, blood testing is attached - the differentiator Evaro structurally cannot copy, and the thing TRT prescribing actually requires - and now there is an AI concierge front door Evaro does not have. A patient landing on a partner clinic's site gets an elite, editorial, clinic-branded AI health assessment ending in a designed Health Report, not a webform. That is the demo moment: in a clinic sales meeting, "here is your website with a luxury AI intake experience wearing your brand" closes rooms that a feature list never will. Lead with it against OM, Mint, GymWolfPT and every clinic after them.

The same asset works in the Voy conversation. Voy came to the 1 July call open to buying Ted's or making it their B2B arm; the strength of Ted's position is the completeness and defensibility of the platform. An AI intake layer with a data flywheel - every intake plus its downstream outcome improving the routing - is precisely the kind of asset that turns "a white-label clinic backend" into "an intake-to-treatment operating system with proprietary conversion data." It strengthens the asset story whichever structure Bear and CJ land on. No need to say more than that on Thursday; the demo says it.

## 4. What Was Built Today

Six pieces shipped against the round-2 spec, all local, none pushed live.

**Clinician Brief.** Every completed intake now generates a structured brief for the prescribing clinician - concerns, history, severity-rated risk flags, suggested actions from a closed set, a screening note in clinical register that never uses diagnostic language. Commercially this is the feature that makes doctors advocates rather than sceptics: it saves their time on every single consult, it replaces a £65/user/month third-party licence, and it is the artefact a partner's medical director will actually read when deciding whether to sign.

**Luxury concierge experience.** A full "concierge" visual preset - ivory and charcoal with gold accents, serif editorial typography, calm motion, and a summary reframed as "Your Health Report" with a print stylesheet that produces a genuinely beautiful branded PDF. This is the sales weapon. White-label deals are won on how the partner's brand looks wearing the product, and no competitor's intake looks like this. It is also the register that justifies premium clinic pricing rather than commodity platform fees.

**Clinical surfacing in staff and clinician portals.** Quiz results were write-only JSON - zero clinical utility, the platform map's own words. Now there is a Health Quiz tab in every per-patient view and a read-only pre-brief in the consultation screen. Commercially, this converts HealthPilot from a marketing gadget into clinical infrastructure: the data doctors and staff actually use daily, which is what makes it sticky and what makes it defensible in a CQC inspection.

**Escalations.** Urgent risk flags in an intake now automatically create an escalation record in the existing staff workflow, deduplicated per intake. This is the safety and DD story made operational - red flags stop being silent JSON and become tracked events with an owner. It is one paragraph in this note and the first slide in any conversation with a regulator.

**Revenue routing cards.** Suggested actions from the intake map to a clinic-branded "Recommended for you" card on the patient dashboard, deep-linking to the exact purchasable next step - testosterone panel, Wellman panel, consult, TRT or ED pathway - respecting each clinic's feature toggles, with invitational rather than diagnostic copy. This is the attributable-revenue seam: the click path from "answered a quiz" to "bought a £150 panel" now exists end to end.

**Public lead capture with claim-on-signup.** The quiz was locked behind login; now a partner clinic can run it on its public site with no account required, and completion becomes a claimable lead - "create your account to unlock your Health Report." This is the Evaro-pattern move: the validated script-tag-simple acquisition mechanic, attached to a platform that can actually do blood testing and prescribing behind it.

## 5. Where the Data Should Sit

Patient and data ownership is the industry fault line. Evaro's model is explicit: Evaro owns the patient, the partner rents a revenue share. That is exactly the fear a white-label prospect brings into the room. Meanwhile the WL Service Agreement's Data Processing Schedule (clause 10.9) is with Ignition Law now - so this decision is live and cheap to make today, expensive to reverse later.

**Recommendation: quiz data is processed by Ted's Health as data processor for the partner clinic, who is the controller of its own patients - and the DPA grants Ted's aggregated, anonymised learning rights across the network.** The partner owns its patients outright; Ted's keeps the right to learn from de-identified intake-to-outcome data to improve routing and publish conversion benchmarks.

Why this way round: at this stage, partner attractiveness beats data hoarding. The pitch that wins against Evaro is "you keep your brand and your patients" - claiming ownership of quiz data would hand Evaro's biggest weakness straight back to prospects and hand a lawyer on the other side a reason to stall a deal Ted's needs signed. The flywheel does not require owning patients; it requires learning rights, and aggregated anonymised learning inside the DPA delivers the benchmark data and matching improvements with none of the ownership friction. It also keeps HealthPilot cleanly on the right side of GDPR special-category rules already covered in the business plan. Get the aggregation clause into the schedule while it is being lawyered anyway - one clause now versus a renegotiation across every signed partner later.

## 7. Risks Worth Naming

Four, all manageable, none optional. First, the screening line: the quiz screens and never diagnoses - that boundary is engineered into the Clinician Brief's language rules, but it must be policed on every copy and prompt change, because drift into diagnostic language is a CQC/MHRA problem, not a copywriting one. Second, LegitScript: HealthPilot content embedded on partner sites is advertising on their domains - the compliance pass has to cover partner deployments, not just Ted's own. Third, insurance: the claims-made policy with no run-off cover is still unresolved, and putting an AI screening layer in front of more patients raises the cost of leaving that gap open - Raemy should close it before the first external clinic goes live. Fourth, ops: Filmer resigned 23 June with roughly two months left and still drives pricing and updates; HealthPilot's rollout needs a named owner on the Ted's side before he goes, or it becomes another orphan of the handover.
