**16 July 2026 · Built by Junior for Jesse · CONFIDENTIAL**

*Analysis of Tyler Ball's 6 July offer. Every Nero figure below is founder-stated and unverified — that is itself one of the findings. Sources: Tyler's emails of 27 May, 29 Jun and 6 Jul 2026, and Raemy's due diligence queries of 29 Jun.*

---

## The verdict in one paragraph

**Do the deal. Don't do this deal.** The brand fit is genuinely excellent, Tyler executes, and the business is real. But the structure he's proposed underprices Bear on every axis that matters and gives away the single most valuable thing Bear owns — his handle, running paid ads — for free. The headline "5% at a $12M valuation" is designed to be read as $600k of value; in practice it's $300k of illiquid paper on signing, in a fifteen-month-old single-product company with no filed accounts. Meanwhile the royalty, which is where the actual money is, is attached to a carve-out range that doesn't exist yet and rests on an undefined phrase ("net revenue") that can swing Bear's cheque by 8x. **Counter, don't decline.**

## Finding 1 — The valuation is a decoy. Don't argue about it.

This is the most important thing in this document.

Tyler wants a debate about whether Nero is worth $12M. **That debate is irrelevant, and engaging in it helps him.**

Here's why. Bear is not writing a cheque. The vesting language ("tied to creative deliverables") makes clear this is **equity granted for services**, not equity purchased. If Bear pays nothing, then the valuation does not set Bear's *price* — it only frames what his 5% is nominally "worth."

And that means **the valuation runs against Bear's interest.** The higher the agreed valuation, the more impressive 5% *sounds*, while the amount of the company Bear actually receives stays fixed at 5%. Tyler is arguing simultaneously that:

- Nero is worth $12M today, so your 5% is worth a handsome $600k; **and**
- $12M is a bargain because we're about to triple.

Both can't be leverage for him. If Nero really is on its way to the Year 3 forecast of £14.1m revenue, then 5% for Bear's entire brand weight is *cheaper* for Tyler, not more generous.

**The only number that matters to Bear is the percentage.** Valuation only becomes relevant if BGV puts cash in — which, on the current DD picture, it should not.

> **Do not negotiate the valuation. Negotiate the percentage, the royalty base, and the whitelisting.** If Tyler insists on defending $12M, agree with him enthusiastically and then ask for 9%.

*One caveat that must be closed first: the email says "5% of Nero at a current $12M valuation" without saying whether this is granted or purchased. Everything above assumes granted. **If Tyler intends Bear to invest $600k of cash for the 5%, this is a completely different conversation** and my answer changes to "no, not on these numbers." Nail this down in the first sentence of the reply.*

## Finding 2 — The royalty is the deal. The equity is the lottery ticket.

Modelled over three years, base case:

| Component | 3-yr value to Bear | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Royalty** (BG range ramping £1m → £2m → £3m, correct definition) | **~£600k** | Cash. Near-term. Low variance. |
| **Equity** (Nero hits Y2 forecast, exits at 1.5x revenue, 25% diluted) | **~£377k** | Paper. Illiquid. Requires a buyer to ever appear. |
| **Ad whitelisting of Bear's handle** | **£0** | The giveaway. |

Tyler's own email tells you this: the royalty gives "immediate, recurring cash flow… rather than value that's locked up until some future exit." He's right. Which is exactly why the royalty terms deserve the negotiating energy, and why it's telling that the royalty is the *least* precisely drafted item in his offer.

Equity at exit, 5%, for reference:

| Scenario | Enterprise value | Bear's 5% (undiluted) | After a realistic 25% dilution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stalls (~£3m rev, 1.0x) | £3.0m | $203k | $152k |

Note the base case: after dilution, Bear's 5% is worth roughly what it's nominally "worth" today — *if* there's an exit at all, years from now. The equity is an option, and it should be priced like one. It should not be the reason to sign.

## Finding 3 — "Net revenue" is undefined, and it swings the cheque 8x

**This is the same trap as the Water2 / Craig Robinson "net profit" clause I flagged on 13 July.** It is now a pattern in BGV's inbound deals and it should have a standing answer.

On a £2m Bear-range year, here is Bear's "10% royalty" under each plausible reading of "net revenue":

| Definition of the royalty base | Base | **Bear's 10%** |
|---|---|---|
| Gross sales less returns & discounts only ← **the correct one** | £1.70m | **£170k** |
| …also less shipping & payment processing fees | £1.50m | £150k |
| …also less COGS (i.e. "net" quietly means gross profit) | £0.80m | £80k |
| …also less marketing / ad spend | £0.44m | £44k |
| "Net profit" — their P&L bottom line | £0.20m | **£20k** |

Same headline. **£170k or £20k.** The definition *is* the deal.

**Required drafting:** *"Net Revenue means gross invoiced sales of Bear Grylls Range products, less only (a) documented customer returns and refunds, and (b) discounts actually granted at point of sale. No other deduction of any kind — including cost of goods, freight, duties, payment processing, platform fees, marketing, advertising, staff, overhead or allocated cost — shall reduce Net Revenue."* Plus: quarterly statements, payment within 30 days of quarter end, and an audit right at Nero's cost if an audit finds a >5% under-report.

## Finding 4 — The royalty base leaks Bear's own traffic

The royalty only touches "a dedicated Bear Grylls product range."

But the whitelisting means **Nero will be running paid ads as Bear across the entire catalogue.** A customer who sees Bear's face, clicks, lands on Nero and buys the core shorts — Nero's actual hero product — generates **zero** royalty. Bear drives the traffic; Nero banks the conversion.

This is the quiet structural flaw in the offer. Bear's audience will lift *everything* Nero sells, and Bear is paid on a carve-out that doesn't exist yet and whose size Tyler controls. He also controls how much of the range gets designated "Bear Grylls Range" versus "core."

**Three ways to fix it, in order of preference:**

1. **Attribution royalty.** 10% on the BG range *plus* 3-5% on all Nero revenue attributable to Bear-driven traffic (whitelisted ad clicks, Bear's link/code, UTM-tagged). Entirely trackable — this is standard affiliate infrastructure, not an exotic ask.
2. **Broader base.** A lower rate (say 3%) across *all* Nero net revenue for the term, in place of or alongside the range royalty.
3. **Minimum annual guarantee.** Accept the carve-out, but with a floor — e.g. £150k/year from the range's launch — so Bear isn't exposed to Tyler's execution on a product line that doesn't exist yet. If Nero believes its own forecast, a floor costs it nothing.

Whichever route: **the whitelisting and the royalty must be tied together.** If Nero runs ads as Bear, Bear gets paid on what those ads produce. That's the principle.

## Finding 5 — "Full whitelisting" is the biggest concession in the deal and it's priced at zero

Nothing in the offer prices this. It's presented as a fourth bullet, as if it were a detail.

Whitelisting means Nero can run paid advertising **from Bear's own handle** — his name, his face, his credibility, at whatever spend and frequency they choose, to whatever audience they choose, for the whole term. Bear's handle is arguably the single most valuable asset in the entire BGV portfolio. It underwrites Modern Savage, Ted's, BMF, the entertainment slate, and every future licensing deal.

**Risks of granting it "fully":**

- **Volume/fatigue** — Nero's incentive is to spend to the edge of profitability. Bear's audience sees Bear selling shorts, relentlessly.
- **Creative control** — ads run *as Bear* that Bear never approved.
- **Brand-safety** — any Nero misstep (product failure, supply-chain story, Tyler's other ventures) lands on Bear's handle, not Nero's.
- **Category conflict** — unrestricted whitelisting could cut across future BGV apparel/outdoor deals.
- **Precedent** — this is the template every future partner will ask for.

**Required restrictions:** written creative approval on every whitelisted asset (48-hour deemed approval so it isn't a bottleneck); a monthly spend ceiling or notification threshold; territory and category limits; term-limited and co-terminous with the partnership; immediate revocation on termination or brand-safety breach, with a hard takedown SLA (24 hours); and **no** use of Bear's likeness on anything outside the agreed range without separate sign-off.

And it should be **paid for** — either folded into the equity percentage or as a separate annual licence fee. "Full whitelisting, free, for the term" should never leave the building.

## Finding 6 — The numbers are unverified, and one of them wants a closer look

Raemy asked the right questions on 29 June. She has not had complete answers.

**What's still open:**

- **No Nero accounts exist.** Tyler, 29 June: *"I don't have any provisional accounts yet, however I can request them."* The Year 1 figures — £2.6m revenue, ~20% net margin — are **founder-stated and unaudited**. Nero's year end was 31 March 2026. Nothing has been produced.
- **The 20% net margin is the number to probe.** Tyler's other business, Built Different (50/50 with Matthew Edge), did **£9.1m revenue on £378k net profit — a 4.2% net margin**. Nero is claimed to be nearly 5x more profitable at a third of the scale. Possible for a premium single-product DTC brand with strong terms; but it's the assumption the whole valuation rests on, and DTC margins compress rather than expand as paid acquisition scales. **Get the P&L.**
- **The £440k "biggest month yet" is unevidenced.** One Shopify/Stripe export settles it.
- **The track record cuts both ways.** Built Different's growth has *slowed* (Tyler: "£3.2 million in sales from March 1, 2026 to now" — roughly flat annualised), and it ran a **£2m Uncapped credit line** with £714k outstanding; bank loans grew from £235k to £901k in the FY25 accounts. It's been repaid, and Tyler has stepped back with a new MD in place — fine, and he was upfront about all of it. But this is the evidence being offered *for* the $12M, and it's a thin-margin, debt-financed, now-flat business.
- **Concentration.** "$4m in net sales on a single product line" is offered as proof of upside. Read it the other way: ~$4m ≈ £3.0m against roughly £4.1m of total company revenue since launch. **The shorts are ~70%+ of everything Nero has ever sold.** That's not a portfolio with a promising line in it — it's a single-product company. Which is fine at fifteen months old, but it's a risk disclosure, not a selling point.
- **Cap table.** "100% owned" needs confirming in writing — no options pool, no SAFEs, no promises outstanding. And the unnamed "founder of a successful brand and podcast" at the same $12M: **get it in writing, with the name and the terms.** An unverifiable comparable used to anchor a price is not a comparable.

**Nothing gets signed until Raemy has (a) Nero management accounts to 31/3/26, (b) the run-rate evidenced, (c) a written cap table, and (d) the comparable partner's terms.**

## Finding 7 — The vesting is asymmetric

50% on signing, 25% at Y1, 25% at Y2, tied to "creative deliverables **and continued partnership** each year."

**The problem:** Bear's value is delivered up front and cannot be clawed back. Once Nero has run two years of Bear-fronted advertising, the brand lift is banked permanently — the awareness, the customer base, the credibility. But 50% of Bear's equity remains hostage to a subjective annual judgement about whether the "partnership continued." If the relationship sours in month 14, Nero keeps everything it gained and Bear loses half his stake.

**Required:**
- **Objective deliverables only**, in a schedule: *X* in-feed posts, *Y* stories, *Z* shoot days per year. If Bear delivers the schedule, the tranche vests. Full stop.
- **Delete "continued partnership"** as a vesting condition. It's a unilateral option for Nero dressed as a mutual commitment.
- **Acceleration in full on change of control or exit.** Non-negotiable. Otherwise a Year-1 sale wipes 50% of Bear's stake exactly when it's finally worth something.
- **Acceleration on termination without cause**, and on Nero's material breach.
- **Anti-dilution or at minimum pre-emption rights.** Without this, Tyler raises at $30m and Bear's 5% quietly becomes 3.5%.
- **Information rights** (quarterly management accounts), **tag-along** on Tyler's sale, and a **mutual** morality/termination clause — not a one-way one aimed at Bear.

## The recommended counter

Send this week. The shape:

1. **Clarify first:** granted for services, not purchased. Bear puts in no cash.
2. **Equity: 8-10%, not 5%.** Justify on reach, not valuation. Bear is not an unnamed podcaster; the asset being contributed is not comparable, so the terms shouldn't be. If Tyler holds firm at 5%, that's acceptable **only** if the royalty base widens materially (Finding 4) — one or the other, not neither.
3. **Royalty: keep 10%, fix the base and the definition.** Tight "Net Revenue" wording (Finding 3), plus attribution on Bear-driven traffic or a £150k/yr floor (Finding 4). Quarterly statements, 30-day payment, audit right.
4. **Whitelisting: not "full", not free.** Approval rights, spend ceiling, territory/category limits, co-terminous, 24-hour revocation SLA. Priced into the equity or as a separate licence fee.
5. **Vesting: objective schedule, no "continued partnership", full acceleration on change of control.** Plus pre-emption, information rights, tag-along.
6. **DD gate:** signing conditional on management accounts, evidenced run-rate, written cap table, and the comparable partner's actual terms.

**Negotiating posture:** warm and fast. Tyler has been chasing since June and is offering, in his words, "equity partnership, paid content, product collaboration" — he wants Bear badly and has already moved from a vaguer ask to a structured offer. That's leverage. But he's also building fast with fifteen products queued, and a founder who feels strung along starts talking to someone else. **The single highest-value action is to stop being slow.** A reply this week that says "we're in, here's our structure" is worth more than another month of refinement.

**What I'd actually accept if it came back:** 6-7% granted, objective vesting with change-of-control acceleration, 10% royalty on a tightly-defined base plus 3% attribution on Bear-driven traffic, whitelisting with approval rights and a spend cap, and a clean DD file. That's a genuinely good deal on a genuinely good brand fit.

## Why this is worth doing at all

Setting the structure aside: the brand fit is the best of any inbound BGV has had this year. Natural fibre, no synthetics, endurance, outdoors — that is Bear's actual territory, not a stretch. Tyler is a real operator with a product team out of Icebreaker, deep supplier relationships in Portugal and China, supplier terms most brands can't get (0% deposit on fabric, 30% on production, balance Net 30/60), and a monthly product cadence. He built to £2.6m in year one, bootstrapped, and he was straight with Raemy about the debt on his other business when he didn't have to be. Bear is already keen.

The problem isn't Nero. The problem is that the offer asks Bear to hand over his handle, his content and his name for $300k of paper that vests on signing, and to accept a royalty on a range that doesn't exist, defined by a word that isn't defined.

Fix those four things and sign it.
