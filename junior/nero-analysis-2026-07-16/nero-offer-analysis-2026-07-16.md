## The verdict

**The brand fit is the opposite of what it looks like — and that is both the risk and the opportunity.**

On paper Nero is the best inbound BGV has had this year: natural fibre, no synthetics, endurance, outdoors. That is Bear's actual territory.

In practice, Nero's growth engine is testosterone-anxiety advertising. Its top paid landing page — live as I write this — is titled **"7 Reasons Your Gym Shorts Are Killing Your Testosterone."** Business of Fashion, in a February profile in which Tyler is the named subject, wrote that Nero's ads *"wouldn't feel out of place within the manosphere"*, described a TikTok of *"young, shirtless men wielding spears"* with an AI voice-over, and quoted the brand boasting its products *"keep [their] balls cool."* **Tyler conceded the mechanism on the record: hyper-masculine posts "tend to fare well algorithmically."**

That is the register that produced $3m in fifteen months. It is also the exact opposite of Bear.

So the real question is not "is 5% enough." It is: **does Tyler intend to keep running that advertising once Bear's face is on the brand — and would he accept a contractual bar on it?** Everything else follows from his answer. If he says yes to the bar, this is a bigger deal than he's pricing and BGV should push hard. If he won't, no equity percentage compensates, and BGV should walk warmly.

**Recommendation: don't counter on terms yet. Ask that one question first.** Then counter.

- *"That plastic contains chemicals that tell your body to produce less testosterone."*
- *"Tiny plastic particles from your shorts are being found inside men's testicles."*

And from [Business of Fashion, 16 Feb 2026](https://www.businessoffashion.com/articles/sustainability/wool-natural-fibre-activewear-nero-mover-huha-mate-the-label/) (I fetched and confirmed every quote):

> *"Nero's advertisements wouldn't feel out of place within the manosphere, the growing corner of the internet dedicated to men's social issues and to the preservation of an idealised form of masculinity."*

> *"In a November video posted to the activewear brand Nero's TikTok account, a rapid montage of stock footage clips featuring young, shirtless men wielding spears accompanies an AI-generated voice-over narration."*

> Nero *"boasts that its products will improve men's virility and 'keep [their] balls cool.'"*

> *"Hyper-masculine social media posts — including ones comparing Nero's offerings to the attire worn by Alexander the Great, Japanese samurai and bodybuilders like Arnold Schwarzenegger — **tend to fare well algorithmically, according to Ball**."*

**This is already published, in tier-1 trade press, searchable, with Tyler named.** Any Bear Grylls association inherits it on day one. The first journalist who covers a Bear x Nero deal will find that article in one search — it is the top editorial result for the brand.

**Three consequences:**

**1. Bear personally carries the regulatory exposure.** Under CAP Code 1.7, both the brand *and* the influencer are named in ASA rulings, and the ASA has ruled against influencers even for content the brand didn't know about. "I never saw the ad" is not a defence. Testosterone and hormone-disruption claims on a clothing product are squarely the kind of thing the ASA rules on. *[Evidence: research-sourced, CAP Code cited — worth Raemy confirming with counsel.]*

**2. It contaminates Modern Savage.** This is the cross-portfolio point nobody else will make. Modern Savage launches mid-September into a category whose defining liability is Liver King. My positioning work last night concluded MS's edge is precisely that it is mainstream and family-framed with *no* manosphere baggage. Bear fronting "your shorts are killing your testosterone" in the same quarter as the MS launch hands a journalist a free narrative: *Bear Grylls is selling men vitality anxiety across two brands at once.* That risk is not priced anywhere in this offer, and it is BGV's risk, not Tyler's.

**3. There is verifiable false urgency on the same page.** Also live: *"Nero prices increase every Monday as wool costs rise. Today's price won't be here next week."* **This is false on arithmetic alone.** The hero short has been listed since 9 January 2025 — roughly 78 Mondays. At even £1 a week it would now be ~£148. It is £70. Under the Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act, false urgency is a banned practice with direct CMA enforcement. *[Price and publish date verified by me from Nero's Shopify catalogue; the DMCCA characterisation is my read, confirm with counsel.]*

> **The uncomfortable core:** the marketing BGV would need to clean up is the marketing that made the company work. Tyler is unlikely to abandon the engine that took him to $3m. That tension is the deal, and it has to be resolved before terms, not after.

## Finding 2 — But the bull case is bigger than Tyler is pricing

Read Finding 1 the other way and it becomes the reason to do this.

Nero has grown to **$3m in fifteen months, bootstrapped, no dilution, no secured debt, sole owner** — while renting essentially all of its demand from Meta and having, on the public evidence, close to no organic community. **The one asset Nero conspicuously lacks is the one Bear has: trust, mainstream credibility, and a reason to buy that isn't fear.**

That makes Bear not an endorser but a **repositioning** — the bridge that takes Nero from a fear-based performance-marketing niche to a real brand. If that's what Bear is, 5% is not in the right postcode. Beckham got ~2% of a company about to IPO at multiple billions for two appearances a year; the percentage scales inversely with what the celebrity is being asked to carry. Bear would be carrying the entire brand transition.

**So the deal is genuinely interesting — and both the upside and the danger come from the same fact.** It only works if Nero changes, and Nero changing means giving up what currently works.

Worth saying plainly: **Tyler is a serious operator.** Serial e-commerce founder, product team out of Icebreaker, supplier terms most brands can't get (0% deposit on fabric and yarn, 30% on production, balance Net 30/60), 80% of revenue into the US from a standing start in Manchester. He was also upfront with Raemy about the debt on his other business when he didn't have to be. The problem here is not the operator.

## Finding 3 — The valuation is a decoy, and a high one actively costs Bear money

Tyler wants a debate about whether Nero is worth $12m. **Don't have it.**

Bear is not writing a cheque. The vesting language ("tied to creative deliverables") makes this **equity granted for services**. If Bear pays nothing, the valuation doesn't set Bear's price — it only frames what 5% nominally "sounds" worth. Tyler is arguing both that Nero is worth $12m today *and* that $12m is cheap because it's about to triple. Both cannot be leverage for him. If the Year 3 forecast of £14.1m is real, then 5% for Bear's entire brand weight is *cheaper* for Tyler, not more generous.

**And there's a sting most people miss.** Shares issued to a UK individual by reason of services fall to be considered under the **employment-related securities rules (ITEPA Part 7)** — potentially an income tax charge on market value at acquisition. That is a **dry tax charge on an illiquid minority share Bear cannot sell to pay it.** The higher the agreed valuation, the bigger Bear's tax bill, for the same 5%.

> **So Tyler's "generous" $12m framing is, on a services grant, closer to a liability transfer than a gift.** Agree with him enthusiastically about the valuation, then ask for a bigger percentage.

**Two actions, both for Raemy:**
1. **Clarify in the first line of the reply: granted or purchased?** If Tyler expects Bear to invest $600k cash for the 5%, this is a different conversation and my answer changes to no on these numbers. *(For reference if it does become a cash question: $12m implies ~3.4x trailing revenue and ~17x trailing profit. Lululemon trades around 1.25x sales and 9.5x earnings today. [Research-sourced; verify before deploying.])*
2. **Get specialist tax advice before signing anything.** The **s.431 election has a 14-day deadline that cannot be extended.** Structure the grant to a corporate vehicle (Bear Grylls Ventures / an image-rights company), not to Bear personally — that is what Beckham (DB Ventures) and Shaq (ABG-Shaq LLC) both did. Route via LSR Partners.

## Finding 4 — The royalty is the deal. The equity is the lottery ticket.

Modelled over three years, base case:

| Component | 3-yr value to Bear | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Royalty** (BG range £1m → £2m → £3m, correct definition) | **~£600k** | Cash. Near-term. Low variance. |
| **Equity** (Nero hits Y2 forecast, exits 1.5x revenue, 25% diluted) | **~£377k** | Paper. Illiquid. Needs a buyer to appear. |
| **Whitelisting of Bear's handle** | **£0** | The giveaway. |

Tyler's own email concedes this: the royalty gives "immediate, recurring cash flow… rather than value that's locked up until some future exit." He's right — which is why it's telling that the royalty is the *least* precisely drafted item in the offer.

**The precedent is uncomfortably exact.** Beckham put £250k into Guild Esports for ~5% alongside an image-rights deal worth £15.25m over five years. Guild's valuation went from ~£41m to £4.11m; his stake diluted 5% → 3.4%; his shares ended up worth under £50,000. **The cash line paid roughly 30x what the equity did — in a listed company with public filings.** Dilution isn't a fraud scenario; it's the default outcome of a company that keeps needing money. *[Research-sourced; figures cited to public reporting.]*

**And one wipeout mechanism nobody mentions:** Bear would hold **ordinary** shares. Any future investor will hold **preferred with a liquidation preference**. In any exit below the preference stack, ordinary returns **zero**. Nero is undiluted today — which is exactly why this must be handled now, before there's a stack to sit under.

## Finding 5 — "Net revenue" is undefined, and it swings the cheque 8x

**This is the same trap as the Water2 / Craig Robinson "net profit" clause I flagged on 13 July. It is now a pattern in BGV's inbound deals and deserves a standing answer.**

On a £2m Bear-range year, Bear's "10% royalty" under each plausible reading:

| Definition of the royalty base | Base | **Bear's 10%** |
|---|---|---|
| Gross sales less returns & discounts only ← **the correct one** | £1.70m | **£170k** |
| …also less shipping & payment processing | £1.50m | £150k |
| …also less COGS ("net" quietly means gross profit) | £0.80m | £80k |
| …also less marketing / ad spend | £0.44m | £44k |
| "Net profit" — their P&L bottom line | £0.20m | **£20k** |

Same headline. **£170k or £20k.**

"Net revenue" is **not a term of art** — it has no settled market definition, which means the drafter wins the ambiguity. Flo Rida's $82.6m case against Celsius turned entirely on the undefined word **"unit"**: a 14x swing on one noun.

**Required drafting:**
- *"Net Revenue means gross invoiced sales of Bear Grylls Range products, less only (a) documented customer returns and refunds actually credited, and (b) discounts actually granted at point of sale. No other deduction of any kind — including cost of goods, freight, duties, payment processing, platform fees, marketing, advertising, staff, overhead or allocated cost — shall reduce Net Revenue."*
- **Cap aggregate deductions at 10% of gross.** This single line defeats most of the attack surface even if the deduction list is imperfect. No cap is the tell.
- Define gross off **list price**, never "invoice selling price" (the standard loophole).
- **Anti-avoidance:** intercompany/affiliate transfers not royalty-bearing; retail computed at retail; sublicence receipts shared at 50%, not the headline rate; binds affiliates and successors.
- **Audit right:** 5 years from each statement, licensee pays if underpayment ≥5%, free choice of auditor, access to management not just documents. Quarterly statements, payment within 30 days.
- **A minimum annual guarantee** — the floor no accounting treatment can reach. *A counterparty who resists both a deduction cap and a meaningful MG is telling you what they intend.*

## Finding 6 — The royalty base leaks Bear's own traffic

The royalty touches only "a dedicated Bear Grylls product range."

But the whitelisting means **Nero runs paid ads as Bear across the entire catalogue.** A customer who sees Bear, clicks, lands on Nero and buys the core shorts — Nero's actual hero product, and until ten weeks ago its *only* product — generates **zero royalty**. Bear drives the traffic; Nero banks the conversion. Tyler also controls how much of the range gets designated "Bear Grylls Range" versus "core."

**Fixes, in order of preference:**
1. **Attribution royalty** — 10% on the BG range *plus* 3-5% on all Nero revenue attributable to Bear-driven traffic (whitelisted clicks, Bear's code/link, UTM-tagged). Standard affiliate infrastructure, not an exotic ask.
2. **Broader base** — a lower rate (~3%) across all Nero net revenue.
3. **Minimum guarantee** — accept the carve-out but with a floor (~£150k/yr from launch). If Nero believes its own forecast, a floor costs it nothing.

## Finding 7 — Refuse account-level whitelisting outright

**This is the one term I'd treat as non-negotiable, and the argument is better than "we're uncomfortable."**

There are three different things routinely conflated. A **Meta Partnership Ad code** covers *one named post*, 60 days, revocable. **Account-level whitelisting** hands over the handle itself, open-ended, and lets the brand **upload new creative that Bear has never seen** and run it from his identity. Only the second is what Tyler asked for.

**The killer argument: Nero doesn't need it, on Meta's own data.** Meta's 300-brand study found labelled per-post Partnership Ads **beat** account-level allowlisting on purchase outcomes — ~3.9% lower cost per purchase. **The performance case for account-level access is negative.** The only thing it buys is the right to originate claims in Bear's name. *[Research-sourced — verify before quoting at Tyler.]*

**And look at what the claims are.** Whitelisting + "your gym shorts are killing your testosterone" means: **ads about testicles, running from Bear's handle, that Bear never sees** (whitelisted dark posts don't appear on his profile), on a brand already publicly labelled manosphere-adjacent — with Bear named in the ASA ruling when it comes.

**Position:** per-post codes only, each one approved. If Nero wants account-level, the answer is no, and Meta's own numbers say it costs them nothing.

## Finding 9 — Before arguing Bear is worth more, find out what Bear is worth

**This is the most uncomfortable section and the most useful.**

The counter I'd otherwise recommend rests on "Bear's reach dwarfs an unnamed podcaster." **The public data doesn't obviously support that**, and BGV should know its own asset before negotiating.

- Bear's Instagram is ~8m followers, but two independent trackers put engagement at **0.22-0.44%** — roughly 18-35k likes and comment counts in the low hundreds. Healthy mega-influencer engagement is 1-2%. Posting frequency is under one a month, which also limits campaign inventory.
- The nominal ~23m "total reach" is a vanity number — over half is a legacy Facebook page where organic reach is typically 1-3% of likes.
- **Audience geography is the gap that matters, and it is not available free.** There's circumstantial reason to think Bear's audience skews heavily to India and SEA (Man vs Wild's enormous Indian audience, the Modi episode, the *Ranveer vs Wild* Netflix India spin-off).

**Now put that next to the fact I verified: 80% of Nero's revenue is US.**

**If Bear's audience is 30-50% India/SEA, he may not move Nero's actual customer base at all** — and Tyler, who has been buying US traffic for fifteen months and watching the data, may know it. That would make 5% a considered number rather than a lowball.

**Action: buy one HypeAuditor or Modash report on Bear's audience (~$50-300) before agreeing anything.** It is the highest-return spend available on this deal — it either arms the counter or saves BGV from overplaying a hand it doesn't hold. *[Engagement figures are aggregator-sourced and should be treated as indicative; the geography question is genuinely open. That's the point — resolve it.]*

## Finding 10 — The vesting is asymmetric

50/25/25 tied to "creative deliverables **and continued partnership** each year."

Bear's value is delivered up front and can't be clawed back. Once Nero has run two years of Bear-fronted advertising, the brand lift is banked permanently. But 50% of Bear's equity stays hostage to a subjective annual judgement about whether the partnership "continued." If it sours in month 14, Nero keeps everything it gained and Bear loses half his stake.

**Required:**
- **Objective deliverables only**, scheduled. Copy Shaq's specificity: named service days, capped hours, itemised assets. And watch the counting rule — Beckham's "12 posts per year" counted the same post on two platforms as two posts, so 12 can be discharged as three assets cross-posted. **Separate grid posts from stories; they aren't the same product.**
- **Delete "continued partnership."** It's a unilateral option for Nero dressed as a mutual commitment.
- **Single-trigger acceleration in full on change of control.** Non-negotiable. Note the argument: double-trigger exists to retain an *employee's* future labour and needs a termination to fire — Bear isn't an employee, so the trigger may be literally unreachable. Single-trigger is the principled ask, not a greedy one.
- **Copy the Beckham formula verbatim** — *"the higher of (i) 1.0% of the enlarged total number of shares, and (ii) $5,000,000 divided by the Share Price."* **That is anti-dilution and a valuation floor in one line.**
- **Anti-restructuring clause** — benefits must follow value up to any new parent. Without it a Newco hive-up strands Bear in a shell.
- **Put share rights in the ARTICLES, not the shareholders' agreement.** Articles bind all members including future investors; an SHA right is worthless against someone who never signed it.
- **On pre-emption:** UK s.561 has two holes that bite exactly here — it covers only shares issued **for cash** (so the next ambassador's services grant sits outside it entirely) and it can be **disapplied by 75% special resolution**. **Getting Bear named into the "Investor"/"Major Investor" definition is worth more than arguing the percentage.**
- **Information rights** (quarterly management accounts), **tag-along** on Tyler's sale, and a **mutual** morality clause — plus pay-or-play, so Nero failing to book Bear isn't Bear's non-performance.

> **And a warning worth holding onto:** Beckham won his argument with F45 and still lost the money — shares transferred eight months late, the stock collapsed meanwhile, ~$9.3m gone, settled after 20 months. **A protection you have to litigate has already partly failed.** Get it right in the drafting.

## The recommended sequence

**Step 1 — Ask the one question, this week.** Warm, direct, founder-to-founder from Jesse, not a lawyer's letter:

> *We're genuinely interested, and Bear likes the product and what you've built. Before we talk structure, one thing we have to line up. Your paid funnel currently runs on testosterone and hormone claims — the "gym shorts are killing your testosterone" page, the manosphere framing BoF wrote about in February. That's the engine that got you to $3m, and we respect it, but it's the one register Bear can't be anywhere near. So: are you willing to build the Bear side on a different footing, and to write that into the deal? If yes, we're excited and we'll come back with a structure. If it's the core of the business, we should say so now and stay friends.*

His answer is the decision. **It also tells you what he's like to be in business with**, which is worth more than any diligence file.

**Step 2 — If yes, then counter:**
1. **Confirm granted, not purchased.** Bear puts in no cash. Grant to a corporate vehicle, never to Bear personally. Tax advice before signing (s.431, 14 days).
2. **Equity 8-10%, justified on the repositioning, not the valuation.** Concede the $12m cheerfully; argue the percentage. Beckham formula for anti-dilution and floor. If Tyler holds at 5%, that's acceptable **only** if the royalty base widens materially — one or the other, never neither.
3. **Royalty: keep 10%, fix the base and definition.** Closed deduction list, 10% cap, list-price gross, anti-avoidance, audit right, quarterly statements, plus attribution on Bear-driven traffic or a £150k/yr floor.
4. **Whitelisting: per-post codes only.** Account-level is refused. Meta's own data says it costs them nothing.
5. **Vesting: objective schedule, no "continued partnership," single-trigger acceleration.** Rights in the articles. Named as a Major Investor.
6. **Marketing covenant as a condition precedent** — no hormone/testosterone claims, no false urgency, no inflated review counts, on any Bear-associated surface. With a termination right and equity acceleration if breached.
7. **DD gate:** management accounts, margin definition in writing, repeat-rate and cohort data, US trademark secured, written cap table, the comparable partner's actual terms.

**Step 3 — In parallel, buy the audience report.** ~$300, before any number is agreed.

**Posture: warm and fast.** Tyler has been chasing since June and moved from a vague ask to a structured offer unprompted. That's leverage — but a founder who feels strung along starts talking to someone else, and he's building quickly. **The single highest-value action is to stop being slow.** A reply this week that says "we're in, here's the one thing we need to solve" is worth more than another month of refinement.

**What I'd accept if it came back:** 6-7% granted, marketing covenant with teeth, objective vesting with change-of-control acceleration, 10% royalty on a tight base plus 3% attribution, per-post whitelisting only, clean DD. That's a genuinely good deal.

**What makes me walk:** Tyler won't bar the hormone claims, or the 20% margin turns out to be contribution margin *and* he won't move on price and percentage.
