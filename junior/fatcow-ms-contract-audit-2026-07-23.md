# Contract audit — Fat Cow x Modern Savage

23 July 2026 | Internal, for Jesse only | Not for Ryan

I went through the draft line by line against the five emails. Ten issues. Three would have cost money or credibility. All fixed in the clean version now sitting in your drafts.

---

## The three that mattered

### 1. The royalty and margin definitions could be read to deduct discount twice

Both "Net Receipts" and "Net Sales" said gross receipts **less VAT, less refunds, less any discount actually given**. But gross receipts already are the amount after discount. Read strictly, a £45 tin sold at 20% off would take £36, then deduct the £9 discount again, leaving £27 as the base.

On the royalty that is Ryan's own wording from 22 July, which you accepted, so it is not a trap he set deliberately. But it runs against us: it shrinks both your 45% and your 10%. On a 20%-off sale it would have taken your royalty from £3.00 to £2.25.

**Fixed.** Both definitions now read "the amount actually received from the customer, after any discount actually given, less VAT and less refunds." Identical economics to what everybody meant, no second bite.

### 2. Two clauses were both protecting you against the same refund, which does not survive a careful read

Your 23 July email proposed that Ryan is not paid until the cancellation window has passed. My first draft had that **and** a separate clause crediting you back when a customer changes their mind. Those are two mechanisms for one problem. Together they take more than was agreed, and Ryan would have spotted it and reasonably asked what else had been slipped in.

**Fixed.** One mechanism only, and it is the one you proposed and he accepted: settlement waits for the cancellation window, so a cancelled order simply drops out of the invoice. No separate credit clause.

If he comes back on the payment timing, which I still think he will, the fallback is to give him ordinary 14-day terms and take the change-of-mind credit instead, at his unit price less his actual cost of goods. Do not give up both. They are worth roughly the same and you only need one.

### 3. Nobody has said who keeps the shipping the customer pays

Not mentioned once in five emails. Ryan pays the postage on every order including yours. If a customer pays £4.95 delivery to Modern Savage, the draft did not say whether that sits inside the split, gets passed to him, or stays with you.

At any real volume this is the difference between the deal working for him and not. Left silent it becomes an argument in month two.

**Fixed** as: delivery charges collected from the customer sit outside the split and are passed through to Fat Cow, who bears the actual postage. That is the answer his numbers assume, since his £20.63 was calculated on the product price alone. Flagged to him openly in the covering note rather than buried.

## The other seven, all fixed

**Chargebacks were not mentioned.** Now treated the same as refunds.

**RRP contradicted itself.** One clause fixed £45 as changeable only by agreement, another gave you complete freedom to price and promote. Now: £45 is the reference price for the launch, you price your own channel as you like, and the reference price only moves by agreement.

**Approval timing was vague in one place and specific in another.** "Promptly" in the clause, five business days in the schedule. Now consistent, and silence is still not approval.

**The sell-off on exit applied even to a brand-safety termination.** If you ever terminate because the association has become damaging, the last thing you want is ninety days of him selling through stock with Bear's credit on it. Now excluded.

**No longstop on launch.** The term ran from first sale, but nothing forced a first sale, so the agreement could have sat live and dormant indefinitely. Now either side can walk if it is not on sale within six months of signature.

**He could have changed the formula without asking.** The pack carries Bear's credit line, so the formulation behind it cannot be a moving target. Now needs your written approval.

**No change-of-control right.** He could have sold Top Dog Now and you would have woken up co-branded with whoever bought it. You can now terminate on a change of control.

Also added, minor: minimum twelve months of shelf life remaining on despatch, and it now states plainly that each side is the seller of record on its own channel.

## Two things I could not fix by drafting

### The VAT position is a live commercial hole, not a technicality

Modern Savage Inc is a Delaware company. These are UK domestic sales, in sterling, of goods that never leave the country. Fat Cow will invoice £20.63 **plus £4.13 VAT** on every unit.

If Modern Savage is not UK VAT registered it cannot recover that £4.13. That is a quarter of your margin per unit, gone, on a deal whose entire value to you is that margin. And as a non-established taxable person selling to UK consumers there is no registration threshold to hide behind, so the obligation starts at the first sale, not at £90,000.

This is Raemy's call and it should be made before signature, not after launch. Either a UK entity signs, or Modern Savage Inc registers for UK VAT. The contract has a placeholder for the signing entity so nothing is prejudiced either way, but do not let this one drift.

### Confirm we actually hold the right we are warranting

The agreement has Modern Savage warranting it has the right to grant what it grants, which includes permission to put Bear's credit line on a pack. Worth being certain there is something underlying that, whether that is Bear's say-so in writing or an existing arrangement through BGV, because it is Modern Savage giving the warranty and Modern Savage carrying it if anyone ever questions it.

Almost certainly a formality given who is involved. Better checked now than assumed.
