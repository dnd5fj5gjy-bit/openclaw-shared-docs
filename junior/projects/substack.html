I'm sailing to Greenland this summer and I'm not entirely sure why
Adventure / Personal — Sets up the expedition as an ongoing story

There's a 40ft steel boat in a Norwegian boatyard that I'm about to spend a large amount of money on.

This summer I'm sailing it from Norway to Iceland to Greenland. No support vessel. Small crew, possibly just me for stretches. I've been planning it for about a year and the closer it gets, the harder it is to explain to people why.

The honest answer is I don't fully know.

Part of it is that I've spent most of my working life building things that have someone else's name on them. My dad's brand, other people's businesses. That's not a complaint — I genuinely love the work, and I'm proud of what we've built. But there's a small, persistent voice that has always wanted to go and do something where no one can take credit except me. The sea tends to sort that out pretty quickly.

A sailor called Robin Knox-Johnston — he's one of the most experienced ocean sailors alive — told my dad when he found out I was doing this: "Buy the Cruising Association Guide to Greenland. And make sure your AIS works, because it's not well surveyed." That was basically his blessing. I'll take it.

I don't really know what I'll find out there, or what state I'll be in when I get back. But I think that's the point. I'm going to write about it as it happens — the preparation, the parts that go wrong, the bits in between when it's just you and the water and too much time to think.

The boat doesn't have a name yet. I'm open to suggestions.

The marketing advice I keep seeing that doesn't work
Brand / Business — The mission vs incentives thesis. Written for founders and anyone building something.

I had lunch with a founder a few months ago. Smart guy, building something genuinely useful in fintech. He walked me through his go-to-market plan and it was basically: cashback, referral bonuses, switching incentives. His whole theory was that he just needed to out-incentivise the incumbents.

I thought he was going to lose. I probably said it too bluntly.

Not because his product wasn't good — it was. But he was setting up a fight on exactly the terms the big banks have already mastered. They have bigger budgets, more data, and have already run every test he was thinking about. You can't beat someone at their own game when they've been playing it for 30 years.

I've spent most of my career working on my dad's brand. One thing I've watched up close for a long time is why people follow him — and it's genuinely never the incentive. Nobody follows Bear Grylls because they're getting something for it. They follow because they believe in something. That distinction sounds simple but it changes everything about how you think about building an audience or a business.

The brands that have stuck around aren't the ones that had the best offer. They're the ones that made you feel like you were part of something. I don't think you can manufacture that, but I do think you can choose to orient your whole strategy around it instead of around acquisition mechanics.

The founder I had lunch with is rethinking things. I hope it helps. I also might have been wrong — I often am about this stuff. But it's the frame I keep coming back to.
