Why I'm sailing to Greenland this summer
Adventure / Personal — Sets up the expedition as an ongoing story

There's a 40ft steel boat sitting in a boatyard right now with my name on it — or it will have, once I finish refitting it.

This summer, I'm sailing it from Norway to Iceland to Greenland. Solo, or close to it. No support vessel. No safety net beyond what we carry.

People keep asking me why.

The honest answer is that I've spent the last five years building things for other people. My dad's brand. Other people's social media. Other people's businesses. All of it genuinely meaningful — I'm proud of it. But there's a version of me that has always needed to go and do something that no one can take credit for except me.

The sea doesn't care who your father is.

Greenland is one of the last genuinely underexplored sailing routes in the world. The charts are patchy. The weather is unforgiving. Robin Knox-Johnston, who has sailed more miles than almost any living human, told me through my dad: "Buy the Cruising Association Guide to Greenland. And make sure your AIS works, because it's not well surveyed."

That felt like enough reason.

I'm not doing this for content. I'm doing this because the version of me who doesn't take these risks is a version I don't want to become.

But I'll be writing about it as I go. The preparation. The fear. The moments out there when things go wrong — and they will. If you've ever felt the pull toward something that seems unreasonable, I think you'll recognise something in what I'm going to share.

The boat doesn't have a name yet. Suggestions welcome.

Jesse Grylls is based between London and Verbier. He runs Bear Witness Ltd and works on his father Bear Grylls' brand globally.

The worst marketing advice I keep seeing
Brand / Business — Your Alfred thesis as a public piece. Speaks to founders, marketers, anyone building a brand.

I had lunch with a fintech founder recently. Smart guy. Good product. Real problem being solved.

He laid out his go-to-market plan. Cashback. Referral bonuses. Switching incentives. "We just need to out-incentivise the incumbents."

I told him he was going to lose.

Not because the product wasn't good — it was. But because he was about to play the exact game the big banks have already won. They have ten times his budget. They've run every A/B test he's thinking about. He would compete on their terms and lose on their terms.

The only way to beat an entrenched incumbent is to refuse to play their game.

I've spent years managing one of the most recognised brands in the world. My dad's name opens doors. But what keeps those doors open — what turns followers into believers — is never the incentive. It's the mission.

People don't follow Bear Grylls because they get something for it. They follow because they believe in something. Because he stands for something real. That's not a marketing strategy — it's a worldview. And it's impossible to out-spend.

The brands that win aren't the ones with the biggest budgets. They're the ones that make you feel like you'd be less of yourself if you didn't join.

That's a completely different brief to write. But it's the only brief worth writing.

A few principles I keep coming back to:

  1. Your enemy is indifference, not your competitor.
  2. Incentives attract the wrong customers — people who leave the moment a better offer appears.
  3. Mission attracts the right customers — people who sell on your behalf because they believe in what you're doing.
  4. The best brands don't ask "how do we get people to switch?" They ask "how do we make people feel stupid for not switching?"

The founder I had lunch with is rethinking his approach. I think he'll get there.

Jesse Grylls manages global brand strategy for Bear Grylls and runs Bear Witness Ltd.
