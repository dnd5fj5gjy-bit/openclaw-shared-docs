The Series A Problem

Neil Smith said in late 2023 they'd raise a Series A "next year." It's now mid-2026 — two and a half years later — and no Series A has been announced. This is the single most important signal. Sophisticated investors have looked at Mettle's growth metrics and passed. That matters more than any amount of positive press coverage.

For context: if a mental health app has compelling user growth and retention, it gets funded. Calm raised $75M Series C. Headspace raised $215M. The market rewards growth. The absence of funding means the growth isn't there.

What We Don't Know (And Should Ask For)

Before making any final decision, these metrics matter and aren't publicly disclosed:

  • Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) — at £6.49/month, how many paying subscribers do they actually have?
  • DAU/MAU ratio — are users actually opening the app daily?
  • 30-day retention — meditation apps live or die on this number
  • CAC vs LTV — what does it cost to acquire a subscriber, and how long do they stay?
  • B2B revenue — how much are Thames Valley Police, Deutsche Bank, Bank of Ireland actually paying?
State of the Market

The Opportunity Is Real

Men's mental health is a genuine gap. 1 in 8 men in the UK experience mental health problems. Men account for 75% of suicides. But 40% of men have never spoken to anyone about their mental health. The reluctance to seek help is structural — most existing mental health tools feel clinical, feminine-coded, or stigmatising for men who grew up being told to "man up."

The mental health apps market is $7.5B in 2024 growing to $17.5B by 2030 (14.6% CAGR). The B2B corporate wellness market is worth £500M+ in the UK alone and growing. These are real numbers.

The Brutal Truth About Consumer Subscriptions

Calm and Headspace have spent a combined £300M+ building content libraries, running paid acquisition, and building brand awareness over 10+ years. Mettle is trying to compete with £2.5M total funding. This is not a fight Mettle can win on consumer subscriptions alone — not at any realistic funding level that's achievable right now.

The 21K Instagram followers on @be.mettle — after 2.5 years with Bear Grylls as co-founder — tells you everything. If the organic growth from the world's most famous adventurer co-founding the app hasn't moved the needle, paid acquisition certainly won't at this stage.

Where the Market Is Actually Open

B2B Corporate Wellness. UK corporate wellness is a £500M+ market where buyers are HR directors with budgets, not individuals choosing between apps. The pitch is completely different: "Reduce mental health sick days, improve resilience, demonstrate duty of care." Mettle already has Thames Valley Police, Deutsche Bank, and Bank of Ireland. That's meaningful proof for seed stage.

Corporate wellness procurement moves slowly, but contracts are large (£30K-£200K+ per enterprise client), retention is multi-year, and they don't need to win an App Store algorithm fight. Bear's credibility and network are actually more valuable for this buyer than for consumer Instagram followers.

Strategic Options

Option 1 — Exit Now

Worth Considering

Approach Neil Smith about buying out BGV's equity stake — either back to the company, or to a secondary buyer. Take the financial loss, free up Bear's brand equity and Jesse's mental bandwidth.

For

  • Clean break while equity still has some notional value
  • Frees Bear's name from a company that may fail
  • Jesse's time goes to Modern Savage, Wildernests, EBG
  • Avoids throwing good money after bad

Against

  • Secondary buyers for seed-stage apps are rare
  • Likely to get pennies on the pound
  • Bear's name is tied in regardless — reputationally harder to fully exit
  • Might feel like giving up too early

Option 2 — Pivot to Pure B2B, Stop Consumer Spend

Highest Potential

Tell Neil to stop spending on consumer acquisition entirely. Strip the overhead. Focus 100% on enterprise clients using Bear as a sales asset — not a social media asset. Use Bear's credibility with police forces, banks, corporates to sign 5-10 enterprise contracts. Each one at £30-100K/year changes the revenue profile overnight.

  • Bear attends 2-3 enterprise sales events per year (not social posts — actual boardroom meetings)
  • Target: UK police forces, military, banks, construction (high male workforce, high mental health risk)
  • 5 enterprise contracts at £50K/year = £250K MRR — transforms the business
  • This model could actually get them to Series A

For

  • Bear's network is genuinely useful for enterprise sales
  • Already have 3 enterprise clients as proof
  • Removes the unwinnable consumer fight
  • Much higher contract values, better retention
  • Could reach profitability faster

Against

  • Requires Neil to fully embrace the pivot
  • Slower sales cycles (6-12 months per enterprise deal)
  • May need bridge capital to survive until contracts close
  • Bear's time commitment goes up, not down

Option 3 — Bear Goes All-In on Consumer, Properly

Only If Bear Commits

Bear runs a dedicated, sustained 30-60 day campaign across all his platforms — personal stories, daily content, direct calls to download. Not one story. Not a post. An actual campaign. If 1% of Bear's 10M Instagram followers download and 10% convert to paid, that's 10,000 subscribers = £65K/month MRR. That would change the company's trajectory overnight.

For

  • Could genuinely move the needle immediately
  • No additional capital required
  • Creates the growth story needed for Series A
  • Bear's personal mental health story is authentic

Against

  • Bear hasn't done this in 2.5 years — why now?
  • Requires 30-60 days of Bear's social content focus
  • Competes with Running Wild, Adventure Garage, Modern Savage for Bear's posts
  • Even if it works, consumer churn is the next problem

Option 4 — Passive Hold, Do Nothing

Not Recommended

Continue as is. Let Neil run it. Don't inject capital or time. Watch what happens by summer 2026.

This is the default path and it leads to one outcome: Mettle runs out of money, can't raise, and either folds or sells for almost nothing. Not a strategy — a slow-motion exit with worse optics.

My Recommendation

The consumer app isn't working and the market evidence suggests it won't without a step-change in Bear's platform commitment — which hasn't happened in 2.5 years despite him being co-founder.

Before making a final call, get the MRR and enterprise revenue numbers from Neil. Those two figures change everything. If enterprise revenue is growing (even slowly), the B2B pivot is the play. If it's negligible, this is probably heading toward wind-down.

Your time is finite and you have better-returning options competing for it right now. Modern Savage and Wildernests are both earlier stage with cleaner upside paths and more direct control.

If enterprise revenue is real: Push Neil to pivot fully to B2B and stop consumer burn. Bear does 2-3 enterprise sales appearances per year — not social posts.

If enterprise revenue is negligible: Explore exit options for BGV's equity while there's still something to exit, or accept the write-down and move on. Don't put more capital or time in.
