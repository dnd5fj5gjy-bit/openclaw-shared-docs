# Liberty / EBG internal arrangement — what Sovereign's has actually proposed

23 July 2026 | Internal, for Jesse | Not for Sovereign's

CJ forwarded John Coleman's note and Jonathan's model this morning. CJ's position is that none of it is negotiable until there is a signed Liberty deal, and he has not been through the model in detail. I have.

The headline claim is that the new structure "over five years yields the same as the prior proposal to Bear/NGU." Bear does land on $10.0M in both. That is the only sense in which it is the same.

---

## What the model is actually built on

Two assumption lines and a lot of allocation arithmetic. Both assumption lines are placeholders, and it is worth knowing that before anyone argues about percentages of them.

**Revenue** is a straight line: $4,002,000 in Year 1, rising by exactly $667,000 a year to $6,670,000 in Year 5. $26.68M over five years. Every figure is $667,000 times a whole number. That is not a forecast of a Liberty deal, it is a unit multiplied out.

**Direct costs** are $1,150,000 in Year 1 growing at exactly 5% a year, $6.35M over five years, 23.8% of gross. Also a placeholder.

Neither number is disclosed anywhere in the document. I derived both from the EBG fee line and the residual. Everything else in the model, including the "Fund receives 23.7% of cumulative net revenue" style statements, is arithmetic performed on those two guesses.

CJ's instinct is right, and the numbers prove it rather than just supporting it.

## The three real changes, stated plainly

| | EBG proposal (Ben) | SC proposal (new) |
|---|---|---|
| Bear salary | $2,000,000 p.a., flat | $750,000 p.a., flat |
| EBG fee | 20% | 15% |
| Everest/NGU split of residual | Everest takes 100% | Scaled: Everest ~75% in Y1 stepping to ~40% by Y5 |
| Bear, 5-year total | $10,000,000 | $10,000,000 |
| NGU Ventures, 5-year total | $15,336,000 | $14,002,000 |
| Everest Fund, 5-year total | $4,989,524 | $6,323,524 |

Bear is flat. **NGU is $1,334,000 worse off and the Fund is $1,334,000 better off.** That is the EBG fee cut from 20% to 15%, and it is a straight transfer that the "same for Bear" framing walks straight past. Bear only stays whole because his cut of the split makes it up.

## The four things I would push on

### 1. The split is scaled the wrong way round, and their own document says it does not need to be

Bear's cut of the split: $376k in Year 1, $630k in Year 2, then $1.38M, $1.74M, $2.11M. **Two thirds of Bear's $6.25M residual falls in Years 4 and 5.** Everest's take runs the other way, front-loaded, and it recovers its $1.7M pref inside two years.

So on a deal that is not signed, with a counterparty that has not committed, the Fund is paid early and Bear is paid late.

The document says the split "is 50/50 across years on a dollar-weighted basis." Then make it 50/50 in each year. I ran it: a flat 50/50 gives Bear **$6,286,762** against the $6,250,000 he gets under their scaling. He is $37k better off in total, and Year 1 goes from $376k to $751k, Year 2 from $630k to $1.01M. Cumulative through Year 3 improves by roughly $630k.

Everest ends five years $37k worse off. On their own numbers this costs them nothing.

This is the ask I would lead with, because their own justification hands it to you. If the point of the structure is genuinely shared risk rather than deferred payment, symmetric timing is the test of it.

### 2. Nobody has defined "costs", and $6.35M of them sit between Bear and his money

This is the same hole as Water2 and Nero, in a bigger deal. Bear's $6.25M is a residual after "EBG's fees and any direct costs." The costs are 23.8% of gross, they are not itemised, not capped, not audited, and not controlled by Bear. Curriculum development, ongoing support, coordination with productions — all managed on the other side of the table.

Whoever books cost to this deal sets Bear's pay. And note the incentive: the 15% EBG fee comes off the top, before costs, so nothing in the structure rewards anyone for keeping costs down.

Under the EBG proposal a cost overrun hits the Fund. Under this one it hits Bear. John says so, in terms: the old structure "would risk the deal going negative if costs are different than expected." He is not hiding it. He is asking Bear to take it.

What I would require before this is agreed in principle:

- An itemised cost budget for Years 1-5, in the side letter, not in an email
- An annual cap at, say, 110% of budget, with anything above borne by the Fund
- No allocated overhead, management fees, or salaries of people who are not working on Liberty
- Affiliate and related-party charges at cost, no mark-up
- Audit right, five years, counterparty pays on a 5% or greater discrepancy, access to people as well as documents

If they resist a cap, that answers the question of what the cost line is for.

### 3. Footnote 1 quietly cancels the headline

At the bottom of page one: *"Assumes Bear receives NGU's full cut of the Everest/NGU split for this deal; a smaller NGU ownership share would reduce his total proportionally."*

The entire "$10M, $2M p.a. average" claim depends on Bear taking 100% of NGU's share of this deal. If his economic interest in NGU is 70%, his residual falls from $6.25M to $4.38M and his five-year total is **$8.1M, not $10M**.

The $2M salary proposal has no such exposure. $10M is $10M.

So the equivalence claim is not established until someone states, in writing, what share of NGU's cut Bear receives on this deal. Resolve that before comparing the two structures at all, because right now they are not comparable.

### 4. The RWS9 paragraph should be struck, and it is useful that they wrote it down

*"Recoupment of RWS9 loss... the Fund was ~$4M short of reaching its 1.15x pref... SC's Proposal captures $4.6M of incremental value above the pref on its EBG investment, which is near the midpoint of the missed return opportunity in RWS9."*

That is a written statement that the Liberty economics have been sized to make the Fund whole on a different, earlier, losing deal. There is no cross-collateralisation between RWS9 and a Liberty NIL agreement — if there were, it would not need arguing in a memo.

Worth noting the arithmetic works against them too: under the current LLLP agreement the Fund clears $8.16M, which is $6.46M above the pref, more than the $4.62M the SC proposal delivers. So on their own RWS9 logic they should be arguing for the contract, not for this.

## Where I would be fair to them

Two things, because the negotiation goes better if you concede what is true.

**Against the actual contract, this is a real improvement for Bear.** Under the current LLLP agreement Bear takes $6.12M over five years with no salary. The SC proposal takes him to $10.0M. They have chosen a structure that pays Bear $3.9M more than the document they could hold you to. The live argument is SC against Ben's $2M proposal, not SC against the contract.

**The $1.7M catch-up waiver, though, is not a concession.** John offers it as something the team "is willing to consider on a one-off basis." Look at the model: the catch-up line reads zero in all three scenarios, including the LLLP baseline. It is a constant, not a variable. And the narrative says the Fund recoups the $1.7M anyway, out of the split, across Years 1 and 2 — it just does so without formal priority in the waterfall. The waiver costs them nothing and should not be paid for.

## The cost of "the same"

Even in the perfect case, where the straight-line revenue lands exactly and costs behave, Bear's money arrives later:

| Cumulative to Bear | Y1 | Y2 | Y3 | Y4 | Y5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| EBG proposal | $2.00M | $4.00M | $6.00M | $8.00M | $10.00M |
| SC proposal | $1.13M | $2.51M | $4.64M | $7.14M | $10.00M |

Discounted at 10%, that is **$7.25M against $7.58M** — about $330k of present value given away for nothing. At 15%, roughly $430k.

Then risk-adjust it. If Liberty comes in 20% under the modelled revenue and costs hold, Bear's total lands near **$7.8M instead of $10M**. Under the $2M salary he gets $10M and the Fund absorbs the miss — which is precisely why they want to change it. If the deal is renegotiated or ends after Year 3, which is ordinary for a university NIL arrangement, Bear has collected $4.64M of his $10M and the Fund has taken its pref and $3.64M.

"The same over five years" is true only at the end of five perfect years.

---

## What I would do about sequencing

CJ is right that there is nothing to negotiate until Liberty is signed. There is a risk running the other way, though: once Liberty is signed, everyone is committed and the only thing left to argue is the internal split. Bear's leverage is highest now, while the answer to "is there a deal at all" is still him.

The move that captures both: agree the **principles** now, non-binding and contingent on a Liberty deal existing — cost definition and cap, flat 50/50 split, the NGU share question answered, salary characterisation, RWS9 struck. Leave every number to be filled in from the real deal. That banks the structural protections without negotiating hypothetical figures, which is CJ's actual objection.

## One for Raemy, not for this table

The $750k is described as a "salary" to Bear. The model books it inside NGU Ventures as a "Bear Talent Fee", which is a different thing with a different tax outcome. Given where Bear is resident and how the rest of his engagements are structured, whether this is personal employment income or a services fee to a corporate vehicle wants deciding before the side letter is drafted, not after. Same category of issue as the equity-for-services point on the earlier deals.

---

## Summary

Not a bad-faith proposal, but a well-argued one that moves $1.33M from NGU to the Fund, moves 62.5% of Bear's economics from contractual to at-risk, defers his money by roughly $400k of present value, and leaves the number that determines most of it undefined.

Three asks, in order of how easily they should be granted:

1. **Flat 50/50 split each year.** Costs them $37k over five years on their own model. Their own document already calls the split 50/50 dollar-weighted.
2. **Define and cap the cost line, with an audit right.** If Bear is taking cost risk, Bear gets to see and limit the costs.
3. **State Bear's share of NGU's cut on this deal in writing.** Without it the $10M headline is unverifiable and the two proposals cannot be compared.

And strike the RWS9 justification. A prior fund shortfall is not a charge against this deal.
