# What Made the Best Supplement Brands Win in Year 1
**Prepared:** April 24, 2026
**Author:** Junior
**Purpose:** Pattern data from the closest comparables to Modern Savage. What actually worked, not theory.

---

## The Three Case Studies

### AG1 (Athletic Greens) — Founded 2010, now $1B+ valuation
Chris Ashenden, a NZ guy in LA, had chronic gut issues and couldn't find a product that worked. Built AG1 for himself. The launch growth driver was podcasts — specifically, Tim Ferriss was one of the first hosts to promote it, authentically, as something he was personally using. Joe Rogan and Andrew Huberman followed. The brand didn't buy reach — it found communities that already believed in the premise (people "taking ownership of their health, personal finances, relationships") and embedded there.

Critical discipline: AG1 stayed single-SKU for years. One product, perfected, brand equity built around it before expanding. That discipline is what made them a billion-dollar brand.

---

### Ancestral Supplements / Liver King — Built to $100M+/year
Brian Johnson (Liver King) built his supplement empire entirely on identity. The Liver King persona was the product. Raw organ meat as daily practice, documented in exhaustive content, became a movement. The business model: cultivate an extreme but coherent lifestyle identity, then sell the supplements as the physical proof of that commitment.

The scandal (steroid use exposed 2022) didn't destroy the supplement business — because the community's identity was real even if the founder's wasn't. People who had built their self-concept around the ancestral lifestyle kept buying.

Lesson: community identity is more durable than the founder's personal credibility. But it requires the identity to be genuine in the first place.

---

### Heart & Soil / Lineage Provisions — Dr. Paul Saladino
Paul Saladino (Carnivore MD) built a personal brand as a physician advocating animal-based nutrition. He then launched Heart & Soil (organ supplements) and later Lineage Provisions (food/snacks) directly into his audience. No grocery stores. No retail. Direct DTC to people who already trusted him as a doctor and thinker.

Deliberately small-batch and quality-obsessed. Growth was through podcast appearances as an expert — he appeared on other people's podcasts talking about carnivore nutrition science, then the audience found the product.

Key: the distribution channel (podcast guest slots) was the same as the trust channel. No separation between marketing and credibility.

## The Patterns That Show Up in All Three

### 1. The Founder IS the Proof of Concept
This is the non-negotiable. AG1 = Chris optimizing himself. Liver King = Brian living the lifestyle. Saladino = the doctor who actually eats this way. None of them had a celebrity face endorsing the product. The founder's lived experience WAS the product story.

For Modern Savage, this means: Bear has to be genuinely taking this. Not in an ad. Daily, visibly, authentically. "I've been taking organ supplements for six months and here's what I've noticed" is worth more than any campaign budget. If Bear doesn't actually use the product, that gap will show — and the ancestral health community, above all others, will call it out.

---

### 2. Community Before Customers
None of these brands launched into a cold market. They launched into existing communities that already believed the premise:
- AG1 launched into the biohacker/performance optimization community (Ferriss readers, Rogan listeners)
- Liver King launched into the carnivore/ancestral health community
- Saladino launched into his own direct audience (Carnivore MD newsletter, podcast)

Modern Savage's 170 waitlist is exactly this. These are people who already believe the premise (ancestral nutrition, organ supplements, Bear Grylls credibility). The first email doesn't have to convince them of anything. It just has to reward their belief with a product that's actually good.

---

### 3. Single SKU Discipline
Every successful supplement brand starts with one product done exceptionally well. AG1 resisted SKU expansion for years. That's what built the brand equity. Expanding too fast dilutes the message and splits attention.

The family pack, kids' blend, and other Modern Savage extensions should wait until the Adult Blend is proven and reordering at scale. Start with one thing. Make it the definitive product in its category.

---

### 4. Podcast Guest Slots, Not Podcast Ads
The AG1 breakthrough came from Tim Ferriss genuinely using the product and talking about it authentically — not an ad read. Saladino's growth came from being a credible guest expert on other people's podcasts. This is consistently more effective than paid placements because it's trust transfer, not interruption.

For Modern Savage: Bear is already getting podcast invitations constantly. One appearance where he talks about why he started taking organ supplements — as a genuine part of his nutrition philosophy, not a product pitch — will do more than 20 ad reads. The key: it has to feel like Bear talking about what he eats, not Bear selling a supplement.

---

### 5. The Identity Purchase Is Sticky
People don't cancel supplements that define who they are. AG1 subscribers don't quit because the monthly cost feels like an identity statement. Liver King customers didn't quit even after the scandal because they'd built their self-concept around the ancestral lifestyle.

The Modern Savage email sequence already does this correctly — "Built for the person who refuses to accept the baseline." That positioning creates the identity purchase, which is what drives low churn. The emails should reinforce this identity continuously post-purchase, not just at launch.

---

## The Liver King Warning

The single biggest risk of the ancestral supplement category: the Liver King got caught faking the lifestyle. His physique came from steroids he was denying. The community felt betrayed — not because they stopped believing in organ supplements, but because the founder identity was fiction.

Modern Savage's differentiator from the Liver King space: Bear's credibility is built over 30 years of documented, filmed wilderness survival. It's not possible to fake. That's a genuine moat. But it only works if Bear is actually using the product — not just putting his name on it.

The launch sequence and all subsequent marketing should make Bear's personal usage visible and specific. Not "Bear Grylls recommends" — "Bear takes two capsules of Modern Savage every morning before training, here's what he says about it." Specific is credible. General is an ad.

## The One Insight That Changes the Launch Plan

Every successful brand in this category grew through **trust transfer from a credible figure to a specific community, not broadcasting to a general audience.**

AG1 didn't advertise to "health-conscious people." They embedded with biohacker communities through people those communities already trusted. Saladino didn't advertise to "carnivore dieters." He appeared on podcasts that his future customers already listened to.

Modern Savage already has this structure: Bear has a community that trusts him. The 170 waitlist members are already in that community. The question is not "how do we find customers" — it's "how do we activate the trust that already exists."

The answer is: Bear uses the product, tells his story of why, and the community comes to him. Everything else is amplification.

## Practical Additions to the Launch Plan

From this research, two additions to the Modern Savage strategy doc:

**Addition 1 — Bear Podcast Cadence**
Identify 5 podcasts in the ancestral health / performance optimization space where Bear could appear as a guest and naturally discuss his nutrition. Not as a sales slot — as a conversation about how his approach to health has evolved. Target: one booking before launch (gives Bear content to reference in Email 1 or 2) and one in Month 2 (retention + new customer acquisition).

Candidates: Huberman Lab, Tim Ferriss Show, Paul Saladino's podcast (Fundamental Health), Rich Roll, Rhonda Patrick (FoundMyFitness).

**Addition 2 — Founding Member Identity**
The first 170 customers should receive something that reinforces their founding member status permanently — not a discount, but a designation. A "Founding Savage" status that shows up in their account, in email subject lines, in community spaces. The identity investment keeps churn low. AG1 never had to fight churn because customers' identities were wrapped up in the purchase.
