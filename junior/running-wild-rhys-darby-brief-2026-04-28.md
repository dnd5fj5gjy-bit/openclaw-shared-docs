## Who Rhys Darby Is

Fifty-two in 2026. Born in New Zealand. **Served in the New Zealand Army before becoming a comedian.** This is not a footnote.

He is known in the UK and US primarily for Flight of the Conchords (Murray, the perpetually hapless band manager for the New Zealand folk duo in HBO's cult comedy). Then: Yes Man with Jim Carrey. The Jumanji reboots (Nigel, the NPC guide). Our Flag Means Death (2022-2023, Stede Bonnet alongside Taika Waititi — beloved by the show's fans, canceled before its time).

He also hosts a podcast called **The Cryptid Factor** — dedicated to seriously investigating mysterious creatures: Bigfoot, the Loch Ness Monster, the Bunyip, things that shouldn't exist. He is not joking. He believes these things are worth investigating.

Married to Rosie Carnahan-Darby since 2004. Two children. Lives in Los Angeles but identifies fiercely as a New Zealander.

---

## What Connects Him to Bear's Audience

**Two things. Both are better than they appear on the surface.**

**One: He was in the army.** Not as a prop or a talking point — as a young man who signed up, served, and trained in real survival skills before anyone knew his name. There's an actual baseline of physical resilience in Rhys Darby that his comedy persona obscures. In the wilderness, that might surface.

**Two: The Cryptid Factor.** Bear has spent his career in extreme wilderness — the places where most people never go. Rhys Darby has spent years taking seriously the idea that in those same places, things exist that we haven't documented. Two men who approach the wild very differently but with the same underlying conviction: out there, something real is happening that most people ignore.

This is the episode that might actually be funny on purpose. Bear's audience skews masculine and appreciates both physical challenge and dry wit. Rhys Darby is warm, self-deprecating, not performatively tough — and that creates space for something more interesting than the typical Running Wild dynamic.

---

## What To Avoid

- **Our Flag Means Death cancellation** — the show was beloved by its fans and abruptly canceled. HBO Max's decision upset a lot of people. Don't reference the cancellation. The show is fine to mention; the ending isn't.
- **"Funny foreigner" framing** — Rhys Darby's comedy partly plays on being a cheerful New Zealander navigating an American world. Don't lean into that. He's a trained comedian and a military veteran. Treat him as both.
- **Cryptids as a joke** — If the episode touches on The Cryptid Factor, don't make it a punchline. He genuinely cares about this. Treating it as eccentricity misses the point.

## Why This Episode Has a Different Texture

Most Running Wild guests bring the wilderness into contact with a known persona: the survivor, the icon, the person who endured something. Rhys Darby brings something rarer — genuine curiosity about what's out there, mixed with actual military training that he's never traded on publicly.

Bear talks to a lot of people who respect the wild. Rhys Darby is one of the few who thinks the wild might contain things we haven't admitted yet. That's a different conversation.

Trust what actually happens in the episode. The Cryptid Factor angle only works if it surfaces naturally. If it doesn't, Angle 1 (the army) is clean and strong on its own.
