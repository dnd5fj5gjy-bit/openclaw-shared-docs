What Is Naturecan

Naturecan is a UK-based direct-to-consumer health and wellness company. They sell supplements, CBD products, and longevity/collagen products. They also operate what they describe as the 2nd largest medical cannabis pharmacy in Europe (75,000 patients). They have a wholly-owned manufacturing facility and a proprietary medtech clinic and pharmacy platform.

The pitch comes via Charles Vyner Brooks, a Knightsbridge broker who is personally close to Naturecan's CFO. He circulated the deck to his "family/friends" network — meaning this is a relationship-driven soft circle, not a competitive institutional round.

The Number That Matters Most

70% gross margin with 2.5% EBITDA means roughly 67.5% of revenue is consumed by sales, marketing, and operations. On £40m revenue that's ~£27m in overhead. This is a heavy-spend growth machine that needs constant capital to sustain its trajectory. The moment they stop investing, growth likely stalls.

The EIS Angle

Naturecan has HMRC Advanced Assurance on EIS — this is meaningful. It means:

EIS Benefits (confirmed via HMRC assurance)

30% income tax relief on the amount invested — applicable to FY25/26 or FY26/27. CGT exemption on exit if held 3 years. Loss relief at marginal rate if the company fails. For a higher-rate taxpayer investing £50k, effective cost after relief is £35k — with full upside participation.

The EIS framing is doing a lot of heavy lifting in this pitch. It makes even a marginal investment look more attractive on a risk-adjusted basis. But tax benefits don't rescue a bad business — they just reduce the cost of a loss.

Bear's Question — The Ted's Health Angle

Bear asked "Any interest for us for our Ted's plans here?" The key question is: what specifically does he see?

Possible reading 1 — Platform acquisition/partnership

Naturecan owns a "wholly owned proprietary medtech clinic and pharmacy platform adaptable for multiple markets and product sectors." If this is what Bear is pointing at, the strategic read is: instead of building Ted's Health's white-label clinic platform from scratch, could you invest in/partner with Naturecan and use their existing infrastructure? This is the most interesting angle — if the platform is good, it could accelerate Ted's by 12-18 months.

Possible reading 2 — Pure financial investment

Bear sees a EIS-qualifying health/wellness play and wants to recycle a capital gain tax-efficiently. This is a reasonable thing to do, separate from Ted's.

Critical context: Ted's Health may be closing this month

Bear privately wrote in BGV Financial on Apr 17: "Tom tbh hasn't delivered anything. Do I keep paying and try this innovation or do we close it this month?" If Bear is leaning toward closing Ted's, investing in a related business at the same time sends mixed signals — and compounds risk. The Naturecan question may have been written before that private message, or Bear may be thinking of Naturecan as a replacement for Ted's rather than a companion.

Key Concerns

1. Valuation not disclosed

The deck doesn't state the Series B valuation or price per share. You cannot assess the investment without knowing what you're paying for £40m revenue and £1m EBITDA. Comparable private health/D2C companies trade at 1-3x revenue. If Naturecan is at 3x, that's £120m pre-money — making this round's shares expensive against thin profitability.

2. CBD regulatory headwinds

CBD in the UK and Europe is in a difficult regulatory period. The FSA Novel Foods authorization process is ongoing and contested. MHRA has tightened advertising standards for CBD health claims. Any adverse regulatory shift hits their core product range and their medical cannabis pharmacy simultaneously.

3. "Family/friends" distribution

CVB is circulating this to his personal network — not via a lead VC with institutional conviction. This can mean the deal is early-stage and growing genuinely, or it can mean institutional money has passed and they're filling with softer capital. Without knowing which institutional funds have seen and declined, you can't assess this risk properly.

4. 470 SKUs signals sprawl

470 products across 65 countries is complex to operate profitably. It often signals a company that has grown by saying yes to everything rather than building a defensible core. The 2.5% EBITDA margin supports this reading.

5. Timing for Jesse/BGV

Modern Savage is in its critical 12-week pre-launch window. Ted's Health is under existential review. The sailing trip is May 7. This is not the moment to add a new investment relationship requiring attention unless the strategic case is compelling and the management lift is minimal (which EIS generally is).

THE KILLER
Assumes failure. Finds what kills it.

Three things kill this investment:

1. The margin problem is structural. £40m revenue, £1m EBITDA. That's not a scale problem — that's a business model problem. 70% gross margin means the underlying products are fine. But ~£27m in operating costs means they are running a marketing-dependent acquisition engine that requires constant reinvestment. If the Series B capital runs out before they hit operating leverage, they're back raising — or not.

2. CBD is a regulatory time bomb. The UK FSA is mid-process on Novel Foods authorization. If that process produces restrictions or blanket bans on certain CBD health claims, a significant portion of their £40m revenue base is threatened simultaneously. They can't easily pivot 470 SKUs.

3. This is the wrong moment for BGV. Bear is considering closing Ted's Health. Modern Savage is pre-launch. Adding a speculative investment relationship, even a small one, dilutes decision-making bandwidth at the worst time. The EIS tax benefit is real but it's a sweetener on a risk you don't need right now.

The single most dangerous assumption Jesse is making: that Bear's question means Bear has done the analysis. He hasn't — he's asking you to do it. Don't inherit his enthusiasm uncritically.

THE STRANGER
Zero context. Sees what insiders miss.

Three things an outsider notices immediately:

Naturecan's most interesting claim — "2nd largest medical cannabis pharmacy in Europe, dispensing to 75k patients" — is listed sixth out of seven bullet points, almost as an afterthought. When someone leads with the tax benefits before the business fundamentals, they know the business fundamentals alone won't close you.

Uncomfortable question: if the platform genuinely accelerates Ted's Health, why isn't a larger health company already in this round? What do the people who know this space better than BGV think of it?

THE OPERATOR
What breaks first when you say yes?

Three execution risks if BGV invests:

That means a technical review, a legal review of the platform licensing terms, and calls with their team. That's 4-6 weeks minimum. Modern Savage won't wait.

2. The Ted's Health decision needs to come first. If Bear is genuinely close to closing Ted's this month, investing in Naturecan with the rationale "it helps Ted's plans" is a bet on a decision that hasn't been made. Sequence matters: decide on Ted's Health first, then decide if Naturecan's platform is the right way to execute that decision.

3. Minority investor with no board seat has no leverage. A small EIS investment in a £40m+ revenue company gives you essentially no influence over how the company is run. You are along for the ride. If the management team makes poor decisions, you watch.

Lock down the Ted's Health decision before evaluating any investment that depends on Ted's being alive. The investment thesis collapses if Ted's closes.

THE CFO
Follows the money. Finds hidden costs.

The EIS math at small ticket sizes:

£25k invested → effective cost £17.5k after 30% relief. If Naturecan fails: loss relief at 45% brings effective loss to ~£9.6k on a £25k investment. If they exit at 3x (optimistic): ~£75k gross gain, CGT exempt under EIS. Risk/reward at small ticket is acceptable for a high earner with capital gains to shelter.

But the valuation is unknown. Without knowing the round price, you cannot calculate the implied exit multiple needed to generate meaningful return. A £120m pre-money valuation on £1m EBITDA requires either a massive profitability improvement or a sector re-rating for any return.

The platform angle changes the maths entirely. If Naturecan's medtech platform would save Ted's Health 18 months of build time, and Ted's Health is worth £Xm at exit, the strategic value of early access via investment could dwarf the financial return on the shares. But only if Ted's Health is alive.

The one number Jesse needs: the Series B valuation. Do not commit capital, even EIS-wrapped, without knowing what you're paying per share and what exit multiple that implies.
