Bear Witness · Internal Strategy Note

Diagnose then build - is it attainable?

Olly's question: can "we review a business and build them a tailored agent that frees up their people's time" become a real dual offering, not just a slide. Short answer: yes, but only in phases, and only if the build side stays capped until the hosting problem is actually solved. This is the phased path, the pricing, and the audit method that gets us there.

See the plan
01 · Where this sits today

Three decks already exist. This one asks whether the combination is buildable, not just sellable.

The Stream 2 deck sells strategy audits using our own team as proof, no hosting commitment. The pivot deck sells three depths of custom-built agent, priced like headcount, with the marketing team as the demo. The pricing deck gives us real cost-to-serve numbers instead of guesses. What none of the three answers directly is Olly's actual question: if the audit and the build are sold together, as one offering, what has to be true before that is safe to do at more than one client at a time. That is what follows.

What we can sell today

The audit, on its own

Using Ted, Nexus, Junior and the BGV Agent as the proof case costs nothing extra to run - they already exist and already work. Selling a review of a prospective client's business against that evidence needs no new infrastructure and no new hire.

What we cannot safely sell today

A build for a stranger's business

Every build in the pivot deck currently means Felix's own hours, on Felix's own Mac Studio pattern, with no documented, repeatable process and no proven way to isolate a second client's agent from the first. One client is a favour. Ten clients is a queue Felix cannot clear.

What already exists to close the gap

A scoped, unbuilt bridge

The Cloud Hosting deck already validated rented multi-tenant VMs (Hetzner, DigitalOcean, AWS Lightsail) as a cheap, capex-free way to host many clients' agents on one small box, the same pattern Ted already uses to serve several Telegram users off one Claude Code instance. It has not been built yet.

The gap is not the idea. It is sequencing. Everything below assumes the audit is sellable now, at volume, and the build stays deliberately capped to one or two proof clients until the hosting bridge and a documented build process both exist. Sell the two together as a marketing story before then, and Felix becomes the single point of failure the pivot deck already flags as its sharpest risk.

This is not a Bear Witness invention. The audit fee gets credited, not wasted

Nearly every firm running this shape credits some or all of the diagnostic fee against the build if the client proceeds. It de-risks the client's first yes and turns the audit into a genuine sales step, not a separate product competing with the build for the same budget.

03 · The phased path to a real dual offering

Three phases. The second does not start until specific, checkable conditions are met - not a date on a calendar.

1
Phase 1 · This quarter · Audit only

Sell the review. Do not sell a build promise yet.

Run the Stream 2 offer as designed: a paid review of a prospective client's business, using Ted, Nexus, Junior and the BGV Agent as the live proof that this way of working actually saves time. Output is a written audit (see the methodology below) that names the specific roles and tasks worth automating and what it would take to build each one - not a built agent. Price: £1,500-3,500 per audit (section 05). Target: 2-3 paid audits this quarter through warm channels only - Hodder Faith's network, Ted's Health's white-label partners, Modern Savage's supplier and ambassador contacts. No cold outreach yet; the proof needs a few real logos before it travels further.

2
Phase 1.5 · Gate conditions · Must be true before Phase 2 opens

Three specific things, not a vague "once we're ready"

One - a test multi-tenant VM is standing up and running at least one non-production agent workspace, proving the isolation pattern the Cloud Hosting deck scoped actually holds under a second tenant, not just in theory. Two - Felix has documented the build process from at least one real build (ideally the first paid audit that converts) into a repeatable checklist, per the pivot deck's own highest-priority risk item. Three - at least one audit client has said yes to a build, proving the audit-to-build handoff actually converts before we build a sales motion around it. None of these are big-bang projects. All three can run in parallel with Phase 1's audit sales.

3
Phase 2 · Once gated · The real dual offering

Audit and build sold together, at more than one client at a time

With the hosting bridge live and a documented build checklist in hand, the audit becomes the front door to the pivot deck's three build tiers (Single Agent, Team Agents, Full Workforce) rather than a standalone product. Capacity is still Felix-gated - realistically 1-2 new builds running concurrently at this stage, not a queue - so the sales motion should stay deliberately slow and selective, not volume outbound. This is also the point at which "building agents for businesses" can honestly be described internally as a second line of the business, not an experiment.

4
Phase 3 · Beyond this plan's scope · Scale

More builds running concurrently, a second builder, or both

Scaling past 2-3 concurrent builds needs either the build checklist proven robust enough that someone other than Felix can run parts of it, or a second technical hire. Both are real decisions with cost attached and neither is needed to answer Olly's actual question - they are the honest next question once Phase 2 is running, not before.

04 · The audit methodology - what we actually check

Four filters, applied to every task a prospective client's people spend time on.

Automation-opportunity frameworks in the wider market (the IMPACT model - Importance, Manual effort, Predictability, Accuracy, Complexity, Timeline - and process/task-mining approaches used by automation consultancies) all converge on the same underlying question: is this task repetitive, rule-based, high-volume and error-prone. We are adapting that into four checks that map directly onto what Bear Witness can actually build, rather than a generic automation questionnaire a client would need a consultant to interpret.

Filter one · Volume

How often does this happen

A task done twice a week is a candidate. A task done twice a year is not, no matter how tedious it is - the build cost never pays back. We ask for a rough weekly or monthly count for every task named in the interview, not an estimate of "a lot" or "not much."

Filter two · Rules

Can the decision be written down

If the person doing the task could explain their decision as a checklist to someone new on their first day, it is rule-based enough for an agent. If the answer depends on reading the room, a relationship, or judgment that changes case by case, it stays human - the agent can still draft, but a person signs off.

Filter three · Data access

Can an agent actually reach what it needs

An agent needs the same inputs a person uses - an inbox, a CRM, a shared drive, a calendar. If the task lives entirely in someone's head or in a system with no API and no export, it is not buildable yet regardless of how repetitive it is. This filter is where most audits find the real cost is not the agent, it is getting the client's own systems talking to each other first.

Filter four · Risk if wrong

What happens on a bad output

A draft social post that is slightly off costs five minutes of editing. A client invoice sent to the wrong company, or a compliance-sensitive email sent without review, costs a relationship or a fine. High-risk tasks are not excluded, but they are scoped with a mandatory human approval step built into the agent's workflow, not automated end to end on day one.

A task only qualifies for a build recommendation if it clears Volume and Rules, and has a workable answer on Data access. Risk determines how much human oversight the build includes, not whether it happens at all. Every audit deliverable scores each named task against all four, in plain language, so the client can see exactly why one task is recommended and another is not - no black box.

Audit input

A structured interview, not a form

60-90 minutes with the business owner or the manager whose team is being reviewed, walking through a typical week task by task. Supplemented by a look at whatever systems they already use - email, CRM, project management, socials - to check the Data access filter against reality, not assumption.

Audit output

A scored task list and a recommendation

Every task named, scored against the four filters, sorted into three groups: build now, build later once a data or systems gap is closed, and leave alone. Each "build now" task is costed against the pivot deck's three tiers, so the client sees a real next-step price, not just a diagnosis.

Audit proof

Shown against our own team

The audit walks the client through how Ted, Nexus, Junior and the BGV Agent were scoped using this exact same four-filter method inside BGV itself - the proof is not a case study from someone else's client, it is the audit method applied to us, live, and still running.

06 · "Free employee time is the most valuable currency" - made concrete

Translate the phrase into a number the client can check against their own payroll.

The phrase itself is not sellable on its own - it is a framing, not a figure. What makes it land in a sales conversation is turning it into the client's own numbers, worked out during the audit itself, not asserted by us.

Step one

Count the hours, not the vibe

The audit's Volume filter already produces a weekly hour count for every task reviewed. Sum the hours across the "build now" tasks for one role - this is the number the client can verify against their own week, because they gave it to us.

Step two

Price the hour at their own rate

Multiply the weekly hours freed by that role's fully loaded hourly cost (salary plus on-costs, divided by working hours in the year) - not a generic UK average, the client's own number, which they usually know better than they expect.

Step three

Show what the freed time buys

State it as a choice, not a saving: those hours can go into the parts of the role that were being crowded out - selling, planning, relationships, the work an agent genuinely cannot do. The pitch is capacity returned to a person, not a headcount removed.

07 · Honest risks - what could stop this being attainable short-term

Three real risks, stated plainly, with what would need to change.

Risk one

Felix stays the only builder

This plan deliberately keeps Phase 2 capped at 1-2 concurrent builds for exactly this reason. If the build checklist never gets documented into something a second person could run, the dual offering caps out permanently at a small number of clients regardless of how well the audit side sells. What changes it: the checklist gets written from the first real build, not deferred to "when there's time."

Risk two

The hosting bridge does not get built

The Cloud Hosting deck scoped this, it has not been stood up. Without it, every build genuinely does need its own dedicated hardware pattern, which reintroduces the exact capex-per-client ceiling the whole plan is meant to avoid. What changes it: one test VM, one non-production tenant, proven before the first paying build client is onboarded - a days-long task, not a project.

Risk three

The audit does not actually convert to builds

Every number in section 02 assumes clients who pay for a diagnostic go on to pay for a build often enough to justify running the audit as a funnel rather than a standalone product. If the first 2-3 paid audits produce recommendations but no build sales, that is real evidence the dual offering does not hold in Bear Witness's actual market yet - not a reason to build hosting infrastructure faster, a reason to pause and find out why before Phase 2 opens at all.

The honest bottom line: this is attainable this year, not this quarter, as a real dual offering at more than a handful of clients. This quarter, the audit alone is genuinely sellable with zero new infrastructure. The build side becoming a second real revenue line, sold at volume rather than as a one-off favour to a warm contact, depends on two specific pieces of work - the hosting bridge and Felix's documented build process - that are scoped but not started. Selling "we review your business and build you a tailored agent" as the full pitch before those two exist would put every early client's build on Felix's spare hours, which is the same single point of failure the pivot deck already flagged as its sharpest risk.

The ask

Greenlight Phase 1 now. Treat the gate conditions as the real Phase 2 start date, not a quarter number on a slide.

Concretely: sign off the £1,500-3,500 audit price and run 2-3 paid audits through warm channels this quarter. In parallel, ask Felix to scope the one-VM hosting proof and start the build checklist from whichever audit converts first. Phase 2 - the real dual offering - opens when those three things are true, not on a calendar date.
