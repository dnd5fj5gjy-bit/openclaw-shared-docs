Principle

Why personal, and why founder-to-founder

White-label is a strategic, brand-linked decision — a business choosing to put TH's clinical engine behind its own brand. It isn't bought by procurement; it's decided founder-to-founder. So we skip the contact form and lead with peer respect.

01

Speed

One conversation with the right person beats weeks of being routed through a partnerships inbox.

02

Signal

A principal-to-principal approach reads as a strategic partnership, not a vendor pitch.

03

Control

You set the frame in the first message, before anyone files TH into a category that doesn't fit.

Defensible to a sceptic. "You're emailing pharmacies at random" — no: we start only with partners who've passed the same compliance bar we hold, segmented by how their audience overlaps with men's-health demand. "Why not just buy ads?" — WL isn't a D2C product, it's infrastructure sold into businesses that already own the audience. "Personal doesn't scale" — the top tier is single digits; personal is exactly right at this volume. Scale comes after the model is proven.

Targeting Logic

Prioritise on compatibility, not size

The constraint on onboarding isn't audience size — it's regulatory and operational compatibility. We start where we can win quickly and cleanly, then go up-market on brand later.

Tier 1 · go first

LegitScript-certified

Already passed the same regulatory bar TH operates to: lowest onboarding friction, they value compliant prescribing, minimal education needed. 9 unique certified entities.

Tier 2 · after

Strong fit, not certified

Real WL fits (men in their ecosystem, plausible appetite) but no cert evidence. Higher friction — some may need to certify. Approach after Tier 1, or in parallel only where there's a genuine warm relationship.

Excluded

Out of scope

Insurers / cash-plan providers, private hospital groups, and defunct or unidentifiable entities — removed from the list entirely.

Why LegitScript and not brand size? A certified partner can be live faster and is far less likely to stall in compliance review. Audience size doesn't help if onboarding jams in governance — so warmth-to-close, not reach, sets the order.

The Sequence

Four touches over ~16 days, then park

Touch 1 · Day 0

Initial

Who you are, the one-line fit specific to their business, the 30-minute call ask. Warm email for Tier 1; compressed LinkedIn note for Tier 2.

Touch 2 · +4 days

Follow-up

The demand-leak angle: their men already search for TRT/ED answers; without a pathway that demand leaves for a D2C competitor. Offer a one-pager as an alternative to the call.

Touch 3 · +5 days

Value / proof

What actually sits behind the WL: CQC registration, GMC doctors, branded intake-to-prescribing, MHRA-compliant pharmacy. "You bring the audience; we bring the operating system."

Touch 4 · +7 days

Breakup

Graceful close: leave the door open, no clutter. After this with no reply, mark dormant and revisit in ~3 months or on a fresh hook.

Cadence: ~16-day window per target, then stop — don't chase past four touches. Cap 8–10 active conversations at once so every reply gets a genuinely personal, same-day response. On a positive reply, stop the sequence immediately and book the call — the sequence exists only to get the call.

Expansion

Beyond the pharmacy pool

The Tier 1/2 list is the online-pharmacy & clinic pool. The same logic — "your brand on the front, our clinical engine underneath" — works for any business that owns a relevant audience but has no men's-health prescribing pathway.

The model is already proven — by competitors. Morrisons Clinic (powered by Phlo, Jan 2025) and Asda Online Doctor (powered by ZAVA) both run live grocery clinics: questionnaire → clinician → delivery. Phlo and ZAVA are not targets — they're competitors who each sell the WL operating system and have already won a supermarket. The opportunity is the white space they leave — and the wedge is depth: sell TH as the specialist men's-health / TRT module, not another generalist clinic.

The model that works here

Affinity, not resale

These bodies don't resell clinics — they offer benefits to members. So pitch a co-branded member benefit: "[Body] Men's Health, powered by Ted's Health," offered to members on a rev-share (or discounted member-rate) basis. Their brand + duty-of-care on the front, our clinical engine underneath. A standard resale-WL is the wrong frame here.

How to actually run the expansion these change the hit rate
  1. Target networks/franchisors, not single sites. One deal with a buying group or franchise = dozens of fronts live at once. Far better ROI on Olly's time.
  2. De-risk the first yes. Lead with a free pilot + revenue-share, no upfront cost. Removes the biggest objection and gets you live to prove it.
  3. Build a clickable "YourBrand Clinic" demo. A one-page mock storefront with the partner's logo as the leave-behind — they see it instantly instead of imagining it.
  4. Lead every pitch with the proof point. "Morrisons + Asda already moved — here's the same model, men's-health-specialist." Social proof + mild FOMO.
  5. Tiered ladder, easy first step. Referral link → co-brand → full white-label. A small first yes beats asking for the whole integration.
  6. Mine the BGV/Bear network for warm intros first. A warm path beats any cold note — map LinkedIn + portfolio relationships before cold-outreaching.

Prep

Three things you'll be asked on the first call

What is white-label?

Their brand, your front-end. Ted's Health clinical infrastructure, supply and fulfilment behind it. They keep the patient relationship and the margin on top.

Commercials?

One line on revenue share or fee structure. Don't wing it — get the number from Calvin before Week 1.

Time to live?

From signature to partner running on TH-WL. Be honest — Calvin and engineering can give a real number.
