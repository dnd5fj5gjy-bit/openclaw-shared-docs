01 — Where we are

A UK success compounding. A US market wide open.

The Greatest Story Ever Told sold 300,000 copies in the UK in its first eight months. That is already half of The Shack's entire UK lifetime, done in a fraction of the time, for a book whose author was not previously a faith publisher's lead voice.

The book debuted at #1 on the Sunday Times. Reader reactions consistently compare it to The Chosen. Bear has called it the proudest thing he has ever done.

That is a UK story compounding under its own momentum. The strategic gap is the United States.

The US Christian book market is roughly fifty times the size of the UK's. It is up 40% since COVID. The appetite is at a generational high. And Bear, universally known in the US, is not yet known there as a person of faith in the way he is in the UK. The bridge between Bear-as-explorer and Bear-as-faith-author has been built here. It has not been built there.

That is the work to do.

The strategic question for Hodder Faith is no longer "how do we promote this book." It is "how do we operate the brand of the biggest Christian title we've published in a generation, across two geographies and the start of an international rollout, without losing the momentum we have."

To do that, you need an operating engine. An engine that produces content, distributes it, holds the voice, builds owned audience, and proves attribution back to retail, every week, against a calendar that already exists.

This proposal puts BGV behind the wheel of that engine.

04 — How the engine works

A loop, not a funnel.

The engine is built as a single integrated system. Six channels, each with a specific job, each feeding the others. No channel works on its own. None of them is the centre. The whole loop is the deliverable.

Discover

Catch people who weren't looking. Short-form on TikTok, Reels, Shorts. YouTube search.

→

Engage

Give them a reason to stay. YouTube long-form. Instagram daily presence.

→

Relate

Turn interest into relationship. Substack subscriptions. Facebook reading community.

→

Act

Convert with no friction. Website with geo-routed retail tree. Tracker layer throughout.

→

Give

Readers become the campaign. Gifting, reader testimony, church group adoption.

Readers who give a copy create the next reader's moment of discovery. The loop feeds itself.

Most publisher marketing treats platforms as separate megaphones: post the same message everywhere, hope it reaches someone. That fails because it asks every platform to do the same job. In this engine, each platform has one job. Content travels between them in a specific direction. The platforms are not silos. They form a journey.

05 — The channels

Six channels. One engine.

Search and permanence

YouTube

The library that compounds for years

+

YouTube is a search engine that happens to play video. Five years from now, someone searching "who was Jesus," "Gospel retelling," "books like The Chosen," or "Bear Grylls faith" should find this channel. A video uploaded in June 2026 still earns watch-time and book sales in June 2030. Of every platform in the stack, this is the one whose work compounds longest.

Two episodes a week, every week.

  • Episode A — The Reading. Eight to twelve minutes. The book itself, read from the audiobook master, with editor-added soundscape and ambient audio. The door creaks open. The storm rises. The crowd noise builds. A modern audio-driven reading that does what reviewers keep saying the book does: makes the familiar feel new.
  • Episode B — The Unpacking. Eight to twelve minutes. A theologian sits with the same passage. Where it sits in the Hebrew scriptures. What the original audience would have heard. The historical setting. The same scholars who fact-checked GSET have a permanent role here.
  • The math. 52 weeks. 2 episodes. 104 episodes per year as the base layer.
  • Batch production. The full theologian cycle filmed in a three-day batch shoot, refreshed every six months.
  • Self-funding. 8-minute minimum hits the YouTube ad threshold. The channel pays for itself over time, with revenue treatment open: pooled production budget, retainer credit, or P&L line.
  • The source layer for everything else. Every YouTube episode is the raw material for short-form across TikTok, Reels, and Shorts. Re-cut, never cross-posted. One shoot, infinite outputs.
Discovery

Short-form — TikTok, Reels, Shorts

The hook that catches people who weren't looking

+

Where the audience meets the book without having gone looking for it. Three to five clips per week, per platform, each re-cut for the platform's grammar. The crucifixion scenes from the audiobook. Shimon's failure. Maryam of Magdala at the tomb. Theologian moments that stop a scroll.

  • Source one: the YouTube engine itself. Every long-form episode is sliced into multiple short-form cuts, each engineered for one platform.
  • Source two: BG-activated content. Existing footage of Bear reading the book, podcast clips (Eldredge, Brierley, Metaxas, Will Cain), ChosenCon, press interviews. Hours of usable material already in the bank.
  • Source three: licensed visual atmosphere. Passion of the Christ, RRR archival, and The Chosen footage where permission can be cleared, set against the audiobook's emotional beats.
  • Every clip points one stage deeper into the engine. Discovery is wasted if there is nowhere to go after it.
  • Geo-locked variants on TikTok and Reels to isolate UK and US activity for clean measurement.
Depth and ownership

Substack

The only owned email list in the stack

+

Substack is the most strategically important channel after YouTube, because it is the only one that gives Hodder an owned email list. Algorithms can take TikTok and Instagram away tomorrow. They cannot take Substack away. Twelve months in, this subscriber list is the single most valuable owned asset GSET has.

  • A standalone GSET Substack. Separate from Bear's personal one. Weekly cadence.
  • Content recipe. Theologian YouTube transcripts edited and tightened into proper essays. Andy's offer of an editorial pass is gratefully accepted.
  • Reader letters and spotlights. Curated reader stories appearing twice a month, with permission, in the editorial.
  • Seasonal series. Lenten reading companion, Advent calendar, Pentecost sequence.
  • Discussion guides for church groups, downloadable, free, gated only by email signup.
  • The reader-not-follower distinction. A Substack subscriber has given an email address and opted in to weekly contact. That is a different commercial asset to a social follower.
Daily presence

Instagram

The heartbeat between peaks

+

The book does not need to be selling on Instagram. It needs to be familiar, so when a calendar peak arrives the audience already knows it. Steady, consistent, always there.

  • Five feed posts a week, plus stories. Designed quote cards, audiograms, reader-testimony spotlights, theologian-of-the-week, behind-the-scenes from the YouTube shoots.
  • Reels share the short-form stack but get their own platform-native treatment.
  • Saves and shares over likes as the success metric. Content that travels DM-to-friend matters more than reach.
  • Bio always points one stage deeper into Substack or the website.
Community

Facebook

The reading community and the structured cohorts

+

Facebook is undervalued by book marketers and over-served by faith audiences. It is where the book's natural readers actually are. Two distinct mechanics:

  • The GSET reading community group. Moderated by us, self-sustaining within ninety days. Discussion prompts twice a week, reader-led from there. Church group leaders find each other and exchange materials. A permanent home that members invite their friends into.
  • Read-along reading groups. Structured five-week reads, one per eyewitness perspective, fully facilitated. People sign up, get a weekly prompt, a passage, a discussion question, and access to a private cohort. By year-end, several thousand readers have moved through one.
  • Geo-locked posts split UK and US activity for clean measurement. This is also the cleanest way to isolate the US sales-correlation signal Andy asked for.
  • Page activity mirrors Instagram, with Facebook-native formats and community tone.
Conversion and attribution

The website

The only piece Hodder fully owns

+

The existing thegreateststoryevertoldbook.com serves a purpose. It is not the home this brand needs. We rebuild it as the permanent central hub. The only piece of infrastructure with no algorithm in the way, and the only piece that can prove the marketing is working.

  • Geo-routed retail link tree. UK visitors see Amazon UK, Waterstones, Bookshop.org, St Andrews. US visitors see Amazon US, Barnes & Noble, Bookshop.org US. Foreign editions appear as they roll out.
  • Geo-detected cover swap. US visitors see the US cover. UK visitors see the UK cover. Future foreign editions served in their respective markets.
  • Auto-pulled Substack journal. Every new article appears in the site's journal section without manual republishing.
  • Embedded social feeds, events page, tracker links throughout. Every outbound click attributed by source, geography, and campaign.
  • Build phase month one. Live by end of month two.

One note: no LinkedIn. LinkedIn audiences are not the audiences for this book. They are professional, transactional, and behaviourally cold to faith content. Time spent there is time taken from the channels that work.

06 — Measurement

How we'll prove this works.

Andy asked the right question on the call: how does this lead to book sales? The honest answer is that book sales are a lagging indicator with retail noise on top, and no marketing operator can give a perfectly clean attribution chain. What we can give you is a signal layer that, taken together, tells you the engine is working and tells Hodder where to lean.

01

Direct attribution

Every outbound retail click from the website is tagged. Every link in every Substack post is tagged. Every social bio link is tagged. We will tell you, within a week, how many readers reached a retail page through which channel. We won't see what they bought once they got there. Retail doesn't share that. We see volume and geography, and that is enough.

02

Geo-isolated velocity

Geo-locked Facebook content, US-targeted Substack campaigns, and US-cover web routing produce a measurable US-specific traffic line. We compare its movement to Hodder's US retail velocity reports month over month. Correlation is not proof. But if US web sessions and US Substack subscribers climb while US retail velocity climbs, the marketing is moving the needle.

03

Engine analytics

YouTube watch time. Substack subscriber growth. Facebook Group active membership. Instagram saves and shares. Short-form view-through rates. These tell us whether the engine itself is healthy: whether content is finding audience, and whether audience is converting deeper into the loop. Reported monthly in a single dashboard.

04

Qualitative signal

Reader testimonies. Comment quality. The kind of DMs that come in. The church groups starting up. The readers who write to say they have given the book to someone. The data that does not appear on a spreadsheet but matters more than any of it. Tracked, surfaced, and reported.

Reporting cadence. One monthly summary document. A weekly check-in covers the operational layer. A quarterly strategy review covers the three-month horizon.

07 — Voice, audiences, Bear's role

The things that do not change because the operator changed.

The handover strategy document settled these answers already. They hold whether the engine is operated in-house at Hodder or by BGV. They are listed here for completeness.

Audiences

Five problems remain the five content pillars: "I don't know the story," "I want to share my faith," "the Bible feels impenetrable," "I've lost the feeling," "I want my kids to engage." Each pillar gets its share of weekly output, weighted toward US-relevant framings where the channels are US-targeted.

Voice

Warm, direct, invitational, never preachy, never salesy. Bear's own published words supply the strongest tone: the audiobook, the book itself, the existing video interviews. Our voice is the friend who genuinely loved the book and wants to talk about it.

Bear's role on GSET

Unchanged from the handover document. Roughly three calendar-anchor posts per year on his personal channels: Easter, Christmas, anniversary. Written by his team and approved by him. Occasional story reposts when content earns them. No filming briefs. No requests for new content. No Bear-dependency in the operating model.

A hard line on AI

Hachette policy. No AI-generated voice. No AI-generated imagery. No AI in covers or marketing creative. Where AI tools are used for backend processes (transcription, scheduling, analytics), they sit outside the creative output and are never used to generate published content.

08 — Operating model

One owner. One rhythm.

Single point of contact

One name at BGV, one name at Hodder. All requests, queries, approvals route through that person on both sides.

Weekly check-in

A standing thirty-minute call, agenda sent ahead. Status across the seven workstreams, last week's analytics, the coming week's plan, anything that needs Hodder input.

Monthly report

One document. Dashboard, channel summaries, qualitative highlights, the next month's priorities.

Quarterly strategy review

A longer session. The calendar three months out, anything that needs to shift, new collab opportunities, the international roadmap.

Approval flow

Operational content (daily posts, clips, scheduled assets) published on our judgement; a sample sent for Hodder's review weekly but not gated on approval.

Calendar-anchor content (Easter, Christmas, anniversary, major US push moments) approved by Hodder in advance via a content brief.

Sensitive content (theological framing, contested topics, anything that crosses a Hodder brand line) escalated for approval before publication.

Service-level commitments

Urgent fixes live within 48 hours. Standard pipeline 7 days. Calendar-anchor content prepared 2 weeks ahead. Theologian batch filming scheduled 1 month in advance. Substack at weekly cadence. Website changes live within 7 working days unless structural.
