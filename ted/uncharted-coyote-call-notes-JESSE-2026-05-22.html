Your explanation · read it as one flow

"So on promoting the show itself — this is the part I've spent my career on. With Running Wild, we had to launch and sustain a show at the highest level — big names, a major network, real scrutiny — and a lot of what made that work is exactly what we'd bring to Uncharted Treasures.

The first principle is that you treat the launch as an event, not just an airing. The promotion starts weeks before anything goes out — building anticipation, seeding the story, making sure that by premiere night it already feels like something people can't miss. That's how we ran Running Wild, and it's the difference between a show that drops quietly and one that genuinely lands.

Then every episode is its own moment. Not just 'watch the show this week' — each one has a hook, a reveal, a discovery that becomes its own story. We plan the whole season as a series of events, so there's always a fresh reason to come back.

To get the reach, we run every channel together. Paid to drive the volume and put it in front of the right audiences. Our owned channels and the wider portfolio to hold and grow the audience. Earned media and press for the credibility you can't buy — and with real finds and real archaeology, this show generates genuine news stories, not just TV moments. And then the talent reach — your audience and Bear's — to launch it fast and at scale.

And it doesn't stop at premiere night. The job is to sustain it across the whole run — keeping the energy up, turning every episode into more content, so the audience builds through the season rather than spiking once and fading.

The reason I'm confident in all of this is that we've done it. Running Wild showed the level we operate at — launching and promoting a show that had to compete right at the top. We've got the experience, the relationships and the machine to do exactly that here."
