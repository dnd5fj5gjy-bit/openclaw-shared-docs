Ted's Health — White-Label

Discovery Call
Script

Olly Westmacott · Partnerships Lead  ·  Target length: 20–30 min
Opening · 30 sec
Set the Frame
"Thanks for jumping on — really appreciate you taking the time out of your day. Just thought I'd share a quick bit of context before we get into it: this call isn't a pitch, it's more of a discovery conversation. I just want to understand your business first and then work out together whether there's actually a fit here.

You'll have seen Dr Andrews in the videos — he's our Medical Director and leads on the clinical side. My role is more on the commercial and partnerships side, so if anything clinical comes up that needs his input, we can always loop him in on a follow-up. Does that sound good?"
Hold the frame: If they push for a pitch immediately, redirect warmly — "Absolutely, and I want to make sure what I share is relevant to your situation — can I ask you a couple of questions first?" The more they talk before you pitch, the better.
Discovery · 5–8 min
Get Them to Confess the Need
Goal: hear them articulate the gap themselves, unprompted.
Opening question:
"So I just wanted to start and ask — what actually made you want to have this conversation today?"
Let them talk. Don't interrupt. You're listening for them to say they have men who need this, demand they're not currently meeting, or revenue they're losing.
"And do you feel you're already seeing your customers or community members asking about testosterone, ED, energy levels — anything in that space? Even quietly?"
"If you were to open up a pathway tomorrow — even informally — do you think men in your world would actually use it?"
"And if this worked — do you see this as a serious revenue line for the business, or more of an added-value thing for your audience?"
Use 1–3 of these, not all. Follow the conversation.
Reconfirmation before moving on:
"Okay great — so just to make sure I've got this right: you've got [X type of audience], you're already seeing [signal of demand], and you do believe there's a real commercial case here. Is that a fair summary?"
Don't proceed until they say yes. This is their verbal commitment. If they hesitate, address it before going to the three pillars.
The Platform · 5–7 min
The Three Pillars
What Ted's Health actually does. Keep it to three things.
"So I'm sure you've seen the videos but let me explain what we actually provide — and I'll just keep it to three things."
Pillar 1
We build and run the clinical platform
"We set up the full white-label infrastructure under your brand. That's intake, triage, eligibility screening, clinical review by GMC-registered doctors, and prescribing coordination where appropriate. You don't build any of that. We've already built it."
Pillar 2
We get your product to market
"We configure the pathway, embed URLs for your specific audience and business type — whether you're a clinic, gym, diagnostics provider, or community. The model adapts to how your customers already interact with you. Your brand is front and centre. Ted's Health is the engine behind it."
Pillar 3
We intake and manage the patients
"Once men are in the pathway, we handle the clinical journey — consultation, prescribing, repeat prescriptions, fulfilment coordination, retention. You don't manage patients. We do. You just maintain the trusted front-end relationship you already have."
"Does that make sense as a model? And do you have any questions on how it actually works before we keep going?"
Pause here. Give them space. Their first question tells you where their head is — price, regulatory, fit, or competition. That tells you which objection to handle first.
Objections · as needed
Handling Pushback
Let them raise objections naturally. If they don't: "Is there anything about the model that feels like it might not work for your business?"
I don't want to become a clinic / I don't want the regulatory burden
Say: "You're not becoming a clinic. You're not prescribing anything. You're not making clinical decisions. Your role is exactly what you already do — hold the relationship with your audience. Ted's Health holds the CQC registration, employs the doctors, and manages the clinical pathway. You bring the men. We bring the infrastructure."
Could this damage my brand?
Say: "That's the right question to ask — and it's exactly why we're selective about who we work with. The risk of doing this badly is real. The risk of doing it properly, through a CQC-registered, doctor-led service, is actually the opposite — it builds trust. Men are already searching for this. The question is whether they find a responsible pathway through your brand, or they end up on a random forum."
I'm not sure my audience actually wants this
Say: "Men don't tend to talk about this openly — that's the nature of it. But privately, they're searching. The demand is already there. What most businesses find is that once there's a credible pathway, the uptake is faster than expected — because you're not creating demand, you're just giving existing demand somewhere to go."
We already refer patients to someone else / have a clinical partner
Say: "That makes sense — most businesses in your position do. The question I'd ask is: what happens to that man once he leaves your ecosystem? Right now you're building trust, identifying the need, and then sending him somewhere else. That other provider gets the recurring revenue and potentially the deeper relationship. What we're offering is a way to keep him in your world — under your brand — so the value you've built stays with you."
I need to think about it / speak to my business partner
Say: "Totally fair. Who else needs to be in the room for a decision like this? If it makes sense to get them on a second call, let's do that — I'd rather take an extra week and have the right people involved than move forward with half the picture."
We'd need to speak to our legal or compliance team
Say: "Completely understand — and I'd expect that. We have a full compliance pack ready: CQC registration, clinical governance documentation, prescribing protocols, and the partnership agreement template. Everything your team would need to review is already prepared. We can also schedule a short follow-up with Dr Andrews on the line to address anything clinical directly. What would make it easiest for your legal team?"
We're already building something similar ourselves
Say: "Respect that — and if you're well down the road, it may not make sense. But I'd ask one question: where are you with CQC compliance and GMC prescribing sign-off? Because what most businesses find is that the build is longer and more expensive than expected, and the regulatory pathway is the biggest blocker. If Ted's Health can cut 18 months and several hundred thousand pounds off that journey, that's worth understanding — even if you ultimately decide to continue building."
What does this cost me?
Before you answer — flush remaining objections first: "Good — let's get into that. Before I do, can I just confirm: if the numbers made sense, is there anything else that would stop you moving forward?"

(Handle anything that comes up, then move to the negotiation section below.)
Commercial · 3–5 min
Negotiation — Commission & Pricing
Only enter this section once all objections are handled and you have a verbal yes to moving forward in principle.
"Okay — so let's talk about the commercial structure."
Frame it correctly first:
"The first thing to know is there's no setup fee and no monthly platform cost. You're not paying anything upfront. The model is purely performance-based — you only pay when patients convert onto treatment. That means there's no financial risk in getting started."
"The way it works is simple. There's a cost price per treatment that Ted's Health charges you. You then set your own retail price to your patients — you control that number completely. Whatever margin you make on top of the cost price, you keep 63% and we take 37%. The more patients you convert and the stronger your retail price, the more we both make. We're completely aligned.

The exact cost price per treatment is laid out in the commercial proposal — I'll get that over to you shortly so you've got the full breakdown in writing."
Note: Confirm the split direction with Tom before going live — you noted it may be 37% partner / 63% TH. Use the right number.
"If you think about what one retained patient on a 12-month TRT programme is worth to your business — even a small percentage of your existing audience converted would still create a meaningful recurring revenue stream, without you having built any clinical infrastructure to get there."
If they push back on the commercial terms: "What's your concern — is it the cost price itself, the margin split, or the retail price you think you can charge your audience?" Isolate the specific objection and address it directly. Don't defend the whole structure if only one part is the issue.
Close · 2 min
The Close
Always end with a specific next step. Never leave it as "let me know."
"Thanks so much for taking the call today — based on everything we've talked about, does this feel like something worth exploring properly?"
If yes
"Great. The next step is getting a commercial proposal over to you so you've got everything in writing. I'll send that over shortly — and I'll book in a follow-up for [specific date] once you've had a chance to review it."
If not sure
"What's the one thing that would need to be true for this to make sense for you?"
Listen. Address it. Then propose the next step.
If no
"Totally fine — I'd rather you're direct. Is it a timing thing, or is it more fundamental than that?"
Understand the real reason. It may be rescuable. If not, thank them and leave clean.
Follow-up email within the hour, every time. One paragraph: what you understood about their situation, what you're sending over, proposed next step with a specific date. This separates you from 95% of people they speak to.
