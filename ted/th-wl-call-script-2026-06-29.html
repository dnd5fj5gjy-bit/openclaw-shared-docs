Discovery Call
Script

Olly Westmacott · Partnerships Lead  |  Target call length: 20–30 min
Opening · 30 sec
Set the Frame
"Thanks for jumping on — really appreciate you taking the time. Just to give you a bit of context before we get into it: this call isn't a pitch, it's more of a discovery conversation. I just want to understand your business first and then work out together whether there's actually a fit here.

You'll have seen Dr Andrews in the videos — he's our Medical Director, leads on the clinical side. My role is commercial and partnerships, so if anything clinical comes up, we can always loop him in on a follow-up. Does that sound good?"
Hold the frame: If they push for a pitch immediately, stay warm but redirect — "Absolutely, and I want to make sure what I share is actually relevant to your situation — can I ask you a couple of questions first?" The more they talk before you pitch, the better your conversion rate.
Discovery · 5–8 min
Get Them to Confess the Need
Goal: hear them say, unprompted, that they have men who need this.
Opening question:
"So — what actually made you want to have this conversation today?"
Let them talk. Don't interrupt. The goal here is to hear them articulate the gap themselves. If they say "we have clients asking about testosterone" or "we keep referring men out and losing them" — that's gold. Reflect it back later.
"And are you already seeing customers or community members asking about testosterone, energy levels, ED — anything in that space? Even quietly?"
"If you were to open up a pathway tomorrow — even informally — do you think men in your world would actually use it?"
"And if this worked — do you see this as a serious revenue line for the business, or more of an added-value thing for your members?"
Use 1–3 of these, not all. Follow the conversation. The questions are prompts, not a checklist.
Reconfirmation before moving on:
"Okay — so just to make sure I've got this right: you've got [X type of audience], you're already seeing [signal of demand], and you do see a real commercial case here. Is that a fair summary?"
Don't proceed until they say yes. This is their verbal commitment that the conversation is worth having. If they hesitate, that's useful signal — address it before going into the three pillars.
The Platform · 5–7 min
The Three Pillars
What Ted's Health actually does. Keep it simple.
"So I'm sure you've seen the videos — let me just explain what we actually provide. I'll keep it to three things."
Pillar 1
We build and run the clinical platform
"We set up the full white-label infrastructure under your brand. That's intake, triage, eligibility screening, clinical review by GMC-registered doctors, and prescribing coordination where appropriate. You don't build any of that. We've already built it."
Pillar 2
We get your product to market
"We configure the pathway and embed URLs for your specific audience and business type. Whether you're a clinic, gym, diagnostics provider, or community — the model adapts to how your customers already interact with you. Your brand is front and centre. Ted's Health is the engine behind it."
Pillar 3
We intake and manage the patients
"Once men are in the pathway, we handle the clinical journey — consultation, prescribing, repeat prescriptions, fulfilment coordination, retention. You don't manage patients. We do. You just maintain the trusted front-end relationship you already have."
"Does that make sense as a model? And do you have any questions on how it actually works before we keep going?"
Pause after this. Give them space to react. Their first question tells you where their head is — price, regulatory, fit, or competition. That tells you which objection to handle first.
Objections · as needed
Handling Pushback
Don't rush to this. Let them raise objections naturally. If they don't, prompt: "Is there anything about the model that feels like it might not work for your business?"
I don't want to become a clinic / I don't want the regulatory burden
Respond with: "That's exactly why the model is built the way it is. You don't become a clinic. You're not prescribing, you're not managing clinical risk, and you're not CQC-registered through this. We hold all of that. Your role is the same as it is today — you own the client relationship. We're the regulated clinical engine sitting behind it. You're not taking on any liability you don't already have."
We already refer patients to someone else / we have a clinical partner
Respond with: "That makes sense — most businesses in your position do. The question I'd ask is: what happens to that man once he leaves your ecosystem? Because right now you're building trust, identifying the need, and then sending him somewhere else. That other provider gets the recurring revenue and potentially the deeper relationship. What we're offering is a way to keep him in your world — under your brand — so the value you've built stays with you."
How much does it cost? What's the commercial model?
Respond with: "I want to come to that — and I will — but I'd rather make sure this is actually a fit before we get into numbers, because the model is structured around your volume and setup. Can I ask: roughly how many men do you think you'd realistically put through something like this in the first three months? Once I know that, I can give you a number that's actually meaningful rather than a generic figure."
We're not sure our audience is ready for this / men won't talk about it
Respond with: "That's the most common thing we hear — and it's also the thing that most of our partners say they were wrong about. The data on this is clear: men are searching for testosterone support in huge numbers, they just need a trusted face to approach it through. They won't go to a clinic they found on Google, but they will act if their gym, their GP, or a brand they already trust makes it easy. That trusted face is you. The barrier isn't demand — it's access."
We'd need to speak to our legal or compliance team first
Respond with: "Completely understand — and I'd expect that. We have a full compliance pack ready: our CQC registration, clinical governance documentation, prescribing protocols, and the partnership agreement template. Everything your team would need to review is already prepared. If it helps, we can schedule a short follow-up with Dr Andrews on the line to address anything clinical directly. What would make it easiest for your legal team?"
What happens if a patient has a serious adverse reaction?
Respond with: "Good question — and this is where the clinical model matters. Every patient goes through our eligibility screening before anything is prescribed. Our GMC-registered doctors hold clinical responsibility for all prescribing decisions. We have clinical indemnity in place. From your side, you're not the prescriber and you're not the treating clinician — you're the referral and relationship layer. The clinical risk sits with us, not with you."
Can we have exclusivity in our area?
Respond with: "It's something we can absolutely discuss — and it depends on the scale and scope of the partnership. We don't offer blanket exclusivity, but for partners bringing significant volume or a defined geographic footprint, there's a conversation to be had. Let's get to the point where we're both confident this is a fit, and then we can structure the commercial terms properly."
We're already doing something similar ourselves / building our own
Respond with: "Respect that — and if you're well down the road, it may not make sense for us to talk further. But I'd ask one question: what's the build costing you in time and money, and where are you with CQC compliance and GMC prescribing sign-off? Because what most businesses find is that the build is longer and more expensive than expected, and the regulatory pathway is the biggest blocker. If Ted's Health can cut 18 months and several hundred thousand pounds off that journey, that's worth understanding — even if you ultimately decide to continue building."
Commercial · 3–5 min
The Commercial Conversation
Only get here if they've said yes to the model in principle.
"So if the model makes sense — let me give you a sense of how the commercial side works. The structure is simple: [insert your confirmed model here — rev share, fee, etc.]. But before I talk numbers, I want to understand your volume. Realistically, how many men do you think you could put through a pathway like this in the first six months?"
Get their number first. If they say 10, the economics look different to if they say 100. Frame the commercial offer around their estimate — it anchors the value to their world, not yours. Don't lead with a price sheet.
"The reason I ask is that we want this to be commercially meaningful for you from the start. We don't want partners who are doing 2 patients a month — we want partners who see this as a proper revenue line. Based on what you've told me, I think you could be one of those."

Close · 2–3 min
The Close
Always end with a concrete next step. Never leave it as "let me know."
"Look — based on what you've told me today, I think there's a genuine fit here. The next step I'd suggest is [choose one below]. Does that work for you?"
If warm / interested

Book a technical call with Dr Andrews

"I'll set up a 30-minute call with our Medical Director so he can walk you through the clinical model in detail and answer anything your team has on the regulatory side."

If very interested / ready to move

Send the partner proposal

"Let me put together a one-page proposal tailored to your business — covers the commercial model, onboarding timeline, and what the first 90 days looks like. I'll have it with you by [day]."

If needs internal sign-off

Send the compliance pack

"I'll send over our full compliance documentation — CQC registration, clinical governance, partnership agreement template — so your legal team has everything they need. And let's schedule a follow-up for [specific date] once they've reviewed it."

If cautious / exploring

Offer a pilot conversation

"We do offer a limited pilot structure for the right partners — lower commitment, defined duration, see how the model performs in your context. Worth exploring if you want to test before committing fully."

"Whatever the next step is — I'll send you a follow-up email within the hour with a summary of what we've discussed and what I'm sending over. Thanks again for your time today — genuinely enjoyed the conversation."
Do this within the hour, every time. Send a brief email: "Great to speak. As discussed, I'm attaching [X]. Here's what I understand about your situation: [one line]. Proposed next step: [specific ask with date]. Let me know if I've got anything wrong." This separates you from 95% of salespeople they speak to.
