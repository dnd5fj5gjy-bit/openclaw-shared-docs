The Digital

The TV series plants the flag. The digital series keeps it flying — 365 days a year, between every season, forever. This is how Uncharted Treasures builds an audience that belongs to the franchise, not to any single talent or broadcaster.

Scroll
Series Format

Fast, deep, always live.

The digital series is not a shorter version of the TV show. It has its own format, its own pace, and its own reason to exist. Where the TV series goes wide and cinematic, the digital series goes narrow and obsessive — the side stories, the live digs, the historical threads the broadcast format cannot contain.

Episode Length
15–25 min
Long enough to go deep. Short enough to watch on a commute. Self-contained — no cliffhangers requiring the previous episode.
Release Cadence
1 per month
Consistent rhythm. Not binge-release, not sporadic. The audience learns to expect it on the first of every month.
Primary Platform
YouTube
The franchise's owned channel. Every view builds the subscriber base. Every subscriber is a permanent franchise asset — not rented from a broadcaster.
Production Style
Embedded / Real
Crew of 2-3. Embedded with the dig team or historical researchers. The camera is a witness, not a director. Authenticity is the product.
Talent Required
Zero guaranteed
No episodes require Bear or Coyote. Expert guests (archaeologists, historians, divers) are the talent. The franchise carries the episode — not any individual name.
Connection to TV
Companion, not recap
Digital episodes expand on TV episodes — what was cut, what was found next, what the discovery means in broader historical context. They reward TV viewers and work standalone for new audiences.
Series One — The Bahamas Strand

Eight episodes. Infinite threads.

The TV series covers the main dig across eight episodes. The digital series covers everything the broadcast format leaves behind — the side expeditions, the archival discoveries, the experts, the historical context that makes every find mean something. Below are eight digital episode concepts mapped to the TV run and extending beyond it.

"Every expedition produces ten times more story than any broadcast format can hold. The digital series is where the rest of the story lives."

Pre-Season

The Permission Nobody Got Before Us

The real story of how Bear Witness secured excavation permits that no previous production has held. Interviews with Bahamian heritage officials, the legal journey, what it means archaeologically. Released 6 weeks before TV premiere — the first piece of owned content that earns press coverage without a marketing budget.

Hook: "They said it couldn't be done. Here's how we did it." TV Episode Link: Premiere context
Companion — Ep 1–2

What the Maps Don't Show

The archival research behind the dig — the 18th century maps, the colonial records, the Bahamian oral histories that pointed us to Long Island. A historian-led deep dive into the evidence base that the TV series references but never has time to unpack. The audience that watched the first two episodes watches this and becomes obsessed.

Hook: "The treasure map existed. Just not in any museum." TV Episode Link: Episodes 1–2
Companion — Ep 3–4

The Archaeology of Piracy

What does it actually mean to excavate a pirate-era site? Marine archaeologist Bart Michelini walks through the methodology — what you look for, how you date a find, how you preserve it, what the law says you can keep. The academic context that makes the TV finds feel real rather than performed.

Hook: "This is what separates a real dig from a treasure hunt show." TV Episode Link: Episodes 3–4
Standalone

The Wreck That Nobody Claimed

A secondary site — a shipwreck too far from the main dig to feature in the TV series — explored in a standalone digital episode. One boat, two divers, one morning. A real find that becomes a franchise moment: the digital series discovered something the TV show didn't have room for.

Hook: "This wasn't in the plan. We found it anyway." TV Episode Link: None — digital-exclusive
Companion — Ep 5–6

The People Who Were Here First

The Lucayan Taíno people inhabited Long Island before European contact. Their history is almost entirely absent from the piracy narrative that surrounds these islands. A conversation with Bahamian cultural historians about what was lost, what survives, and what a responsible excavation owes to that legacy.

Hook: "Treasure hunters rarely ask whose land they're digging." TV Episode Link: Episodes 5–6
Standalone

If We Found the Main Cache

A speculative-documentary episode: what would actually happen if a major treasure find was confirmed? The legal framework, the Bahamian government's role, the international heritage law, the media storm. Told as a serious journalistic piece — not clickbait speculation but a genuine preparation document that the audience watches and realises this is actually possible.

Hook: "We've thought through what happens if we actually find it." TV Episode Link: Mid-season context
Companion — Ep 7–8

What Season Two Looks Like

The franchise-extension episode. Released alongside the TV finale, this episode documents the questions Season One raised but didn't answer — and maps where the next expedition could go. Not a teaser. A genuine strategic conversation about where Uncharted Treasures goes next, told as content rather than marketing.

Hook: "Season one found the thread. Season two pulls it." TV Episode Link: Finale companion
Post-Season

A Year After the Dig

Released 12 months after the TV premiere. What happened to every find? Where are the artefacts now? What did the Bahamian Heritage Authority decide? What changed for the communities around Long Island? A year-later check-in that proves this franchise cares about what it finds — and keeps the audience connected between seasons.

Hook: "We didn't just dig and leave." TV Episode Link: Season recap

Distribution Architecture

Owned first. Amplified second.

Every piece of digital series content is published to owned channels first. Third-party amplification (Coyote's channel, Bear's handle, partner newsletters) is bonus reach — not the distribution plan itself.

Owned Channels (Primary)
  • Uncharted Treasures YouTube — every digital episode, full-length. The franchise's permanent home. Subscriber growth is the key long-term metric.
  • Uncharted Treasures Instagram — Reels from every episode, social calendar content, community posts
  • Uncharted Treasures TikTok — short clips, franchise lore, discovery moments. Algorithm-native format.
  • Email newsletter — built during the TV campaign, sustained weekly. Highest-value owned audience. Not algorithm-dependent.
Amplification (Upside)
  • Coyote Peterson's YouTube — cross-post or feature in relevant episodes. Subject to talent agreement. Valuable if it happens, not planned as baseline.
  • Bear Witness portfolio — BGSA, Modern Savage, managed accounts. All add reach to the owned base.
  • Bahamas Tourism Board — co-promotion of location-specific content. Confirmed partnership adds geographic reach.
  • Press / earned media — digital-exclusive finds (like the wreck episode) create their own press moments that drive organic discovery at scale.
