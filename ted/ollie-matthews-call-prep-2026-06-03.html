The starting point

What he actually has.

OJay Health is a credible, established 1-1 functional health coaching practice. Ollie has the proper credentials, a strong founder story, sixteen named-client testimonials including recognisable names, and a working lead magnet (the Optimal Health Test). The product is solid. The brand is solid.

What he doesn't have is a repeatable marketing system. Right now everything depends on him personally being visible, posting, replying, taking calls. So when he gets busy or tired, the pipeline goes quiet. That's what's actually behind the message — not a lack of capital, a lack of structure.

The honest re-frame

Investor is the wrong frame.

Pressure-test what he'd actually get from an investor before he goes looking for one. The maths usually doesn't land for a service business that depends on the founder's time.

What "find an investor" really means

Giving up ownership to buy a marketer.

Investors want equity in exchange for capital, and they want a return. A 1-1 coaching practice doesn't scale without Ollie — so there's no obvious exit, which makes it unattractive to most investors and expensive to anyone who is willing. He'd dilute the business he built to fund something he can run for <£2k a month.

What he actually needs

A funded marketing function, paid from profit.

Productise the offer so it converts without him in every call. Run one clean funnel end-to-end. Hire one operator on a retainer or profit-share to run it. He keeps 100% of the business, and the system pays for itself within a few months. This is the "marketing engine" he's actually describing — he just labelled it "investor."

First steps · next 30 days

Where to start, in order.

Five concrete moves. Each one removes a specific blocker. The point is that he doesn't have to wait for capital or hire a whole team — these are all things he can start this week.

01

Publish pricing on the site.

The site sends every interested person to "Book a call" with no price. That filters out everyone who isn't ready to commit to an unknown number, and it makes him personally sell every single lead. Publish three tiers — even just a starting-from price — so high-intent people self-qualify before they get to him.

Why first: this single change halves his sales-call load and doubles call-show quality.
02

Build the funnel around the Optimal Health Test.

The lead magnet already exists and it's good. What's missing is what happens after someone takes it. Add a five-email nurture sequence that walks them from "I scored badly" to "book the call," plus an automatic call-booking link inside the result page. This is the spine of the whole system.

Why next: he already has the asset — he just needs to build the conveyor belt behind it.
03

Pick one channel. Run it properly.

Instagram is the right channel for his audience of high-performing men. Three posts a week, one format (reels works), one hook style, one CTA pointing to the Optimal Health Test. Stop scattering across LinkedIn, Facebook, blog. One channel done well beats four done badly every time.

Why: focus is the only thing that creates compounding. Spreading thin is what's making it feel overwhelming.
04

Hire one operator — not a whole team.

A part-time VA or content-led freelancer at £800–£1,500 / month who runs the IG calendar, replies to DMs, follows up unbooked leads, and reports weekly. Pay them retainer plus a small per-client kicker. He stays the face; they run the engine. This is what "an investor would let me hire" really looks like — he just funds it from cashflow.

Why: removes the founder bottleneck without ceding ownership. Reversible if it doesn't work.
05

Track one number weekly.

Leads → calls booked → clients signed, every Monday. That's it. No dashboards. The point is to give Ollie a single signal of whether the system is working, so he stops feeling reactive and starts feeling in control. Decisions about what to change come from this number, not from how busy he feels.

Why last: until 1–4 are in place, there's nothing to measure. Once they are, this is the steering wheel.
The bigger question

If he actually wants scale, he has to productise.

The above gets him a steady client flow inside 1-1 coaching. But if the real ambition is the kind of business an investor would back, that means moving beyond pure 1-1 — a group programme, a course, a productised diagnostic package — anything that doesn't trade his hours for revenue. That's a different conversation, and a much bigger one. Worth flagging but probably not the focus of this call.

Stay 1-1

System gets him steady clients.

What's in the first-steps section. Six months of focused execution and he has a calm, predictable practice without giving up ownership.

Add a tier above

Group programme.

A six-month cohort version of his coaching at a lower price point. Lets him serve 20+ people at once. Now the business has leverage.

Add a tier below

Self-serve diagnostic + course.

The Optimal Health Test plus an automated course as a £200–£500 entry product. Becomes a funnel into the higher tiers and a standalone revenue line.

Only then

Investor conversation makes sense.

Once there's a productised tier that scales without him, the business has an exit and an investor has something to back. Until then, profits fund growth.

Two paths to put on the table

Grow it yourself, plug into ours, or both.

Give him two clean options rather than one. Most people pick a hybrid, but they only get there if they see both laid out. Frame Path A as the foundation either way — Path B doesn't work without it.

Path A

Grow OJay Health on his own terms.

The five-step system. No investor, no dilution. He uses cashflow to fund growth and stays 100% owner.

  • Publish pricing — stop being the only filter
  • Build the funnel behind the Optimal Health Test
  • Pick one channel (IG short form) and run it weekly
  • Hire one part-time operator at ~£1k/m + kicker
  • Track leads → calls → clients every Monday
Verdict: this is the floor. Six months of focused execution and he has a calm, predictable practice — and a base from which any next move is cheap to fund.
Path B

White-label with Ted's Health.

He plugs OJay into BGV's regulated men's health infrastructure. We provide the clinical engine; he keeps his brand, his audience, his customer relationship.

  • Adds a productised, prescription-grade tier above 1-1 coaching
  • Captures clients he currently loses to "I'd need TRT for that"
  • No CQC build, no GMC hiring, no fulfilment headache
  • Recurring revenue share that scales without his hours
  • Compliance, doctors, intake, CRM — all handled
Verdict: this is the scale tier. The thing an investor would have funded — except he doesn't need an investor because it's already built.
The smart play

Path A first. Then Path B as the lever.

The honest answer is he probably wants both, in order. Path A is the foundation — he needs a working funnel, a clear offer and one operator running it before anything bolts on top. Once that's humming (call it 60–90 days), Path B becomes the productised scale tier: same funnel, same audience, but now an OJay × Ted's Health clinical pathway sits above the 1-1 coaching. That stack is what gives him a constant client stream, a scalable revenue line, and the freedom to either reinvest in growth or — if he still wants to — have a much more interesting conversation with an investor, because now there's something investable to back.

How to land it on the call

A simple way in.

Don't tell him "you don't need an investor" — that closes the conversation. Open it instead. The frame below keeps him in the driving seat and gets him to the same conclusion himself.

Open

"Before we talk investor, can we pressure-test what an investor would actually buy you? Because what I'm hearing you describe — someone to run your marketing, tell you what to post, run sales calls — that's all stuff you can hire directly. So the real question is: do you want capital, or do you want a system?"

If he says system

"Right — then let's design the system. The good news is you already have most of the pieces. You've got the credentials, the testimonials, the lead magnet. What you're missing is structure. Want me to walk you through what I'd do in the first 30 days?"

If he pushes back on price-on-site

"I know it feels counter-intuitive — but right now you're the filter for every lead, which is exactly the bottleneck you're describing. Publishing pricing isn't about converting more, it's about converting fewer, better."

If he asks "but how do I find the operator"

"There's a whole layer of part-time marketers and ops people who'll do this for £1k a month plus a kicker. Happy to send you a couple of templates for the role and how to brief it."

Bridge to white-label

"There's also a second route worth knowing about — separate question. We're building a white-label platform at BGV called Ted's Health. CQC-regulated, GMC doctors, full clinical engine. Partners like clinics, gyms, coaches plug their audience in and offer regulated men's health — TRT, ED, diagnostics — under their own brand. It's the productised tier above what you do now. Doesn't replace the coaching, it sits above it. The interesting bit for you: it's the thing you'd have used an investor to build, and it's already built."

How the two fit

"My honest take is do both, in order. Get the system from Path A working first — that's 60-ish days. Once you've got a funnel that converts and an operator running it, bolt the white-label tier on top. Same audience, same funnel, but now you've got a clinical revenue line your hours aren't capping. That's the stack that gives you a constant client stream and a scalable business without giving up ownership."

Close

"Pick one thing from Path A this week — publishing your pricing is the cheapest, highest-impact move. Do that, get the funnel started, and let's talk again in two weeks. By then we can have a proper conversation about the white-label tier with real numbers behind it."
