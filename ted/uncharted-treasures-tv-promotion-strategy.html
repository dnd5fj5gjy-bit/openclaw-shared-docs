The Mission

Not a campaign for a show. The first chapter of a franchise.

“Adventure television has spent two decades replacing real discovery with manufactured drama. Uncharted Treasures is the correction.”

This is not a marketing campaign for a TV show. It is the opening chapter of a mission: to prove that real discovery — real excavation permits, real archaeology, real finds — beats manufactured drama every time. When audiences watch Uncharted Treasures, they are not watching a format. They are watching history happen.

That mission shapes everything. The channels we use, the tone we take, the content we make, the communities we build. Every tactical decision in this document flows from one conviction: authentic access is the unfair advantage that no competitor can replicate.

The TV launch is how we plant the flag. The franchise is how we keep it flying. And the franchise must stand on its own — not borrowed from any single talent, channel or relationship. Uncharted Treasures is the IP. Everything else serves it.

Three content pillars power that franchise indefinitely: the TV series as the prestige anchor, a year-round digital series that keeps audiences engaged between seasons, and always-on social content that ensures the brand never goes quiet. That stack is what a franchise looks like — a single TV launch is what a show looks like.

Market Context

Why this lands now

The genre, the audience, and the talent are converging at the same moment. Treasure-hunt television has spent two decades on the periphery of cable. It is returning to the centre of the conversation, lifted by a real-archaeology renaissance, the AI-reconstruction trend in documentary filmmaking, and a chronic undersupply of family-safe adventure content across the streaming platforms. Uncharted Treasures sits precisely at that intersection — with a YouTube-native lead, a legitimate scientific team, real excavation permits no other production holds, and the most recognisable name in adventure television above the line.

01

Genre

Treasure-hunt television is having a quiet revival

Oak Island has held the bar for twelve seasons. Expedition Unknown, History's Greatest Mysteries and the AI-driven reconstruction documentaries coming out of Disney+ have all proven a real, durable appetite for slow-burn, evidence-led mystery. What the genre is missing is a flagship with a creator at the centre — a face the audience already follows into the unknown. Uncharted Treasures is built to be that flagship.

02

Supply Gap

Family-safe adventure is structurally undersupplied

Streamer commissioning has chased true-crime, dating formats and reality drama for the better part of a decade. Adventure-documentary with genuine co-viewing appeal — the kind a parent puts on with a nine-year-old on a Friday night — is an open lane. Networks need it. Family streamers need it. Uncharted Treasures is built specifically for that lane, with a tone and talent combination that works across the household rather than just for one part of it.

03

Talent

Coyote bridges household and feed in one move

Coyote Peterson is one of the very few creators whose audience is genuinely cross-generational and household co-viewed. Kids brought the parents in years ago through his wildlife content. He is the cleanest bridge between a YouTube-native younger viewer and the linear or streaming parent making the subscription decision — in a single talent, with twenty million subscribers already opted in to exactly this kind of real-world adventure content.

04

Distribution

Bear's EP credit opens rooms others cannot

Bear Grylls as executive producer is not a vanity line on a poster. It is a distribution unlock — the credit that gets the title into top-of-slate conversations at Disney, Nat Geo, Amazon, Apple and the global free-TV majors. It also brings Bear Witness's existing partner network into immediate reach, and adds Bear’s 8.5-million social following to the promotional infrastructure before a single paid pound is spent.

Strategic Objectives

Three stages. Each one unlocks the next.

The campaign is built as a sequence, not a set of parallel activities. Stage one acquires the audience. Stage two converts that audience into genuine believers in the show and builds the brand identity from zero. Stage three capitalises on the momentum of a credible, talked-about show to grow the owned ecosystem — the YouTube channel, the social following, the community — that the franchise is built on. You cannot skip a stage. Each one is the condition for the next.

I.

Acquire

Get the right audience in front of the show

Uncharted Treasures launches with no existing audience of its own. Every viewer has to be acquired from scratch, which means the combined reach of Bear Grylls, Coyote Peterson and the wider Bear Witness portfolio is not a supporting asset — it is the entire acquisition engine in the early phases. Paid media fills the gaps. The objective is simple: make premiere night unavoidable for the audiences most likely to become long-term fans of the franchise.

II.

Convert and credibilise

Turn attention into belief — and belief into a brand

Getting people to watch once is acquisition. Getting them to believe in the show, talk about it, share it and come back for the next episode is conversion. That requires credibility — and credibility is Uncharted Treasures' single biggest differentiator. Real excavation permits — genuine legal access no competitor holds. A genuine archaeological team. Real historical evidence. Tangible finds on camera. Expert voices validating it publicly. This is what separates the show from every other treasure-hunt format, and it has to be built consistently from T–12 so that by premiere night the brand already stands for something specific and trustworthy.

III.

Capitalise and build

Ride the momentum into a franchise that outlasts the broadcast run

A credible, talked-about show with a hooked audience is the foundation. Stage three rides that momentum — the repurposing engine turns every episode into weeks of content across every platform, feeding the YouTube channel and social following as they grow into the permanent franchise home. The audience delivered by the TV run is the starting point for something much larger: Uncharted Treasures as an umbrella brand and content hub, with established adventure specialists — treasure hunters, divers, historians, wilderness explorers — each keeping their own personal channel, identity and audience, connected under one community and one compounding content engine. The TV launch is what makes that possible — and the back half of this plan maps exactly how it gets built.

Audience Architecture

Three audiences. One show.

Because Uncharted Treasures has no existing audience of its own, the acquisition strategy is built entirely around borrowing from three pools that already exist and already trust the talent involved. Coyote Peterson's YouTube subscriber base alone represents over twenty million people who have already opted in to exactly this kind of content. Bear Grylls' social reach adds 8.5 million more across a slightly older adventure demographic. The family co-viewing audience is the broadest pool but the least pre-warmed — it requires paid media to reach efficiently.

The strategy treats these as three distinct campaigns running in parallel, each with its own creative tone, channel weighting and conversion path — because the message that converts a Coyote fan is different from the one that converts a parent choosing what to watch with their kids on a Friday night.

Channel Strategy

Four channel disciplines, working in concert

Each channel does different work. Paid drives the volume. Owned holds the audience we already have. Earned gives us the credibility we cannot buy. Partnerships extend reach into communities that paid would never efficiently touch.

Channel 01 · Paid

Bought reach, measured ruthlessly

Heavy Meta and YouTube investment from T–4. Three concurrent campaigns through to launch week: broad awareness for A1, retargeting for A2, expedition-led creative for A3. Paid social shifts toward retention and full-season binge messaging in Phase IV.

  • Meta Advantage+ for broad family targeting
  • YouTube TrueView pre-roll on adventure and family verticals
  • TikTok Spark Ads boosting top-performing organic moments
  • Connected TV buys in the two weeks pre-premiere
Channel 02 · Owned

The properties already in our hands

Bear Grylls' personal social, BGSA and Modern Savage — along with all other owned Bear Witness platforms — carry Uncharted assets in their own native voice, each platform contributing reach in proportion to audience fit. Every episode is repurposed into platform-native content — vertical Reels for Instagram, clips for TikTok, recap cuts for YouTube — driving tune-in during the broadcast run and building the owned audience that the franchise is built on after it.

  • Bear's personal channels lead with the EP-led cinematic teaser
  • BGSA frames the show as the spirit of expedition
  • Episode moment clips repurposed as native Reels and TikToks across all accounts
  • Portfolio email lists carry the premiere announcement and weekly episode drops
  • Coyote's twenty-million-plus subscriber base treated as primary owned reach alongside all Bear Witness managed accounts
Channel 03 · Earned

Expert validation, creator community, podcasts

The credibility of Uncharted Treasures is its biggest differentiator from every other treasure-hunt format. Earned strategy leads with that — getting the right experts, creators and podcast hosts talking about the show in environments the audience already trusts, long before and throughout the broadcast run.

  • Archaeologists, maritime historians and diving communities publicly validating the show's legitimacy — shared organically in the communities that matter
  • Adventure, history and exploration creators covering the show because it genuinely fits their world — not paid, earned through relevance
  • Coyote and Bear on the right podcasts in launch week — long-form, trusted, converts the kind of audience this show is built for
  • Expert community seeding at T–8 so credibility is established before the premiere, not after
Channel 04 · Partnerships

Communities we cannot buy our way into

School-network partnerships for educational tie-ins. Museum and aquarium co-promotion. Lego, treasure-themed toy and family-attraction brand crossovers. The Bahamas tourism board as a flagship cultural partner. Each partnership earns reach we could not pay for at the same authenticity.

  • Bahamas tourism board flagship partnership
  • School curriculum supplement around the show
  • Lego, treasure-themed toy crossovers
  • Museum and aquarium activation co-promotion
