This doc keeps the strategic intent (conviction / curiosity / invitation) but rebuilds the execution so each ad earns its first 1.5 seconds and says something a scrolling person actually stops for.

Section 01 · Diagnosis

Why the current ads feel flat

Five specific failure modes. None of these is a tone problem — the reverence is right. They're attention and craft problems, and each has a concrete fix.

1 · The hook is a windup, not a punch

If the first frame is a book cover, a title card, or a slow fade into "In a world where…", the viewer is already gone. A faith ad has roughly 1.5 seconds before the thumb moves.

Fix: open on the single sharpest spoken line or the most arresting image. Earn the cover by second three, never show it first.

2 · It tells you it's meaningful instead of being it

Abstract copy — "a story that has shaped the world", "the greatest story ever told" — asserts significance without delivering a single concrete moment. The viewer feels marketed to.

Fix: one specific, human, undeniable detail beats any superlative. Show the thing; don't label it.

3 · No tension, so no reason to watch on

The ads resolve too early. If the viewer knows where it's going by second two, there's nothing pulling them through. Reverent and predictable reads as wallpaper.

Fix: open a real question or a genuine tension (doubt, distance, a hard honest admission) and hold it. Let the resolve land late.

4 · Generic stock-feel visuals

Sunsets, open Bibles, hands in light — the visual grammar every faith ad uses. It signals "religious content" and the audience auto-scrolls because they've seen it a thousand times.

Fix: texture and specificity — a real face mid-thought, a worn page, a hand pausing. Editorial, not devotional-stock.

5 · The CTA is the loudest thing in the ad

If "Get the book now" arrives before the viewer cares, it kills the goodwill the rest built. We're not selling a gadget; the sell has to be quiet.

Fix: soft, single, late CTA — an invitation, not a command. The ad's job is to make them want the link, then offer it once.
Section 02 · Benchmark

What good looks like — the reference set

The bar to send to Ku, split honestly into two kinds: real Ku / BGV work we can point at directly, and recommended reference styles that describe the qualities to aim for. We are not fabricating specific Instagram posts we can't verify — where it's a style, we say so.

Real · Ku / BGV produced

The GSET "Narrative" cuts — "Find Your Way Back" (Task #194)

Of the current GSET batch, the narrative-led pieces have the most going for them — they have a story spine rather than just a claim. "Find Your Way Back" is the closest thing in the existing set to the right idea: a real human arc (drifting, returning) rather than a product pitch.

Why it's a useful reference: it leads with a human situation, not the book. Use it as the direction to push further — the instinct is right, it just needs a sharper hook and more honest, less polished texture to stop feeling like an ad.
Real · Ku / BGV produced

Ted's Health Podcast episode cuts (Task #192 and the series)

Ku's podcast clip work for Ted's Health is a good internal benchmark for craft discipline — clean captions, a tight spoken-line hook, restrained motion, sound-on design. That same craft standard is exactly what the GSET ads should inherit; the difference is tone, not technique.

Why it's a useful reference: proves the team can already cut a punchy, caption-led, hook-first short. GSET should borrow the craft and swap the energy for stillness and weight.
Reference style · aim for this quality

Bear's own archive when he speaks about faith plainly

Bear's most powerful faith moments on camera (existing interviews, the audiobook, podcast appearances) work because he is unguarded and specific — he names doubt, fear, failure. That register is the gold standard for GSET ad copy and any archive cut we use.

Why it's the bar: honesty over polish. A real admission in a real voice out-performs any scripted line. When we cut archive, choose the most human 6 seconds, not the most quotable.
Reference style · aim for this quality

Editorial publisher creative, not church-account creative

The look to chase is closer to how a serious literary publisher promotes a major book — restrained typography, generous whitespace, one strong line, real texture — than to typical faith-page design. Think the visual confidence of a Penguin or Sunday Times book launch, applied to a faith title.

Why it's the bar: it signals quality and respects the reader's intelligence. It also differentiates GSET from the sea of generic faith ads — the audience stops because it doesn't look like a faith ad at first glance.
Reference style · aim for this quality

The "one honest sentence on a plain frame" format

The single most reliable scroll-stopper for this kind of content is a stark, true sentence held on a clean frame, sound optional. No music build, no stock montage — just a line good enough to make someone pause. This is also the cheapest and fastest format for Ku to produce at volume.

Why it's the bar: it forces the work into the words, which is where faith content lives. If the line isn't strong enough to stop a scroll on its own, no amount of edit dressing will save it.
Section 03 · Creative Direction

Six concrete concepts for the next batch

Each keeps the strategic intent of the current variants but rebuilds the execution. Built for Meta (9:16 / 1:1), 10–20 seconds, sound-on but readable muted. Hook lands in frame one. CTA is soft and single. All buildable from archive Bear, audiobook VO and licensed footage — no new shoot, no AI.

Concept 01 · replaces Variant A — Conviction

The Admission

Conviction told honestly is more convincing than conviction asserted. Lead with Bear admitting something real, not declaring something grand.

Hook
"I've nearly died more times than I can count. None of it scared me like this question did."
Visual
Open tight on Bear's face mid-sentence from archive — unguarded, not performing. Hold it. No title card, no music swell. A single held frame of the worn book appears only at the end.
Copy
On-screen caption mirrors the spoken line, one phrase at a time. No second message competing. Let the admission breathe.
CTA
"His honest answer — The Greatest Story Ever Told." One line, late, soft. Link in the description.
Concept 02 · replaces Variant B — Curiosity

The Open Question

Real curiosity needs a real question held open — not a teaser that resolves in two seconds. Make the viewer want the answer enough to tap.

Hook
"Two billion people build their lives on this story. Most have never actually read it."
Visual
Plain frame, strong type, the line landing word-block by word-block. Cut to a single texture shot — a real hand pausing on a page mid-turn. Hold the pause. Editorial, not devotional-stock.
Copy
Follow with one line of genuine tension: "So what's actually in it?" — and stop there. Don't answer in the ad.
CTA
"Read it for yourself." The link is the answer to the question the ad refused to close.
Concept 03 · replaces Variant C — Invitation

The Five-Week Door

An invitation works when it's concrete and finite, not open-ended and vague. Offer a specific, low-commitment way in — the Matthew read-along — not "buy the book".

Hook
"Read the greatest story ever told — slowly, over five weeks, with a few thousand other people."
Visual
Warm, calm, human. Quiet morning light, a real reading setup — mug, page, no staging. Bear audiobook VO underneath, low. The opposite of urgent.
Copy
Caption frames the offer plainly: "A finite, shared read. Not a subscription. Not a sermon." Sets expectations honestly.
CTA
"Start with us." Drives to the Matthew cohort sign-up, not retail. The book sells itself once they're in.
Concept 04 · evolves Narrative 2 — Find Your Way Back

The Drift

Push the existing narrative further. Most of the audience isn't anti-faith; they've just quietly drifted. Name that exact feeling with no judgement and they'll see themselves.

Hook
"You didn't decide to stop believing. You just got busy, and one day it was gone."
Visual
First-person, mundane texture — commute, screen glow, ordinary days passing fast. No church imagery. The drift is everyday life, so the visuals are everyday life.
Copy
Turn gently at the end: "The story's still here. It hasn't gone anywhere." No guilt, no pressure. Recognition, then an open door.
CTA
"Find your way back to it." Soft. Links to the cohort or the book — test both.
Concept 05 · new · the cheap repeatable workhorse

One True Line

The "one honest sentence on a plain frame" format from the benchmark. Fast to produce at volume, and the strongest scroll-stopper when the line is good. Build a template, pour new lines weekly.

Hook
A single arresting line, frame one, strong serif on a clean ground. e.g. "It's survived 2,000 years of people trying to delete it." / "Bear Grylls read it when he had nothing left."
Visual
Locked brand template — one line, generous whitespace, one optional texture frame. No montage, no music build. Sound optional. This is the volume format.
Copy
The line is the whole ad. Caption below adds at most one supporting clause. Test 8–10 different lines against each other to find the winners.
CTA
"The Greatest Story Ever Told." Title + soft link. Same end card every time — the variable is only the line.
Concept 06 · new · social proof, done honestly

Someone Like You

A real reader's words, plainly presented, out-pull any brand claim. Once reader testimony is flowing (per Brief 05), turn the best lines into ads. Consent-gated, never composited.

Hook
A real reader's first line, verbatim. e.g. "I'm not religious. I read it because Bear did, and I didn't expect to be moved."
Visual
Text-on-screen over a quiet ambient frame, audiobook tag underneath. Attribution by first name + context ("James, 41, runner"). Real, unpolished, human.
Copy
Light edit only — preserve the reader's voice. Never embellish, never invent. The unpolished quality is the point and the proof.
CTA
"See what they found." Soft link to the book or cohort.

GSET ads — the bar, from now on

The problem we're fixing: recent GSET ads feel flat. They wind up before they punch, assert significance instead of showing it, resolve too early, lean on generic faith visuals (sunsets, open Bibles), and put the CTA before the viewer cares. Reverence is right — the energy and craft need to sharpen.

Six rules for every GSET ad:

  • Hook in frame one. Strongest spoken line or most arresting image first. Never open on the book cover or a title card — earn the cover by second three.
  • One specific human detail beats any superlative. Show it, don't label it. Cut the words "greatest", "transform", "life-changing".
  • Open a real tension and hold it. A genuine question, doubt or admission. Let the resolve land late so there's a reason to watch on.
  • Editorial, not devotional-stock. Real faces mid-thought, worn pages, real texture. Closer to a literary publisher's launch than a church account. No AI imagery.
  • Soft, single, late CTA. An invitation, not a command. Drive to the Matthew cohort sign-up where it fits, not always retail.
  • Craft standard = your Ted's Health podcast cuts. Clean captions, tight hook, restrained motion, readable muted. Borrow that craft; swap the energy for stillness and weight.

Tone: plain, sincere, direct. No cheesy metaphors, no hard-sell. We invite; we don't push. Guardrails: archive Bear only (no new filming), audiobook VO and licensed footage only, no AI voice / imagery / creative anywhere.
