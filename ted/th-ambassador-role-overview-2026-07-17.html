What the Ambassador Role Looks Like

A straight rundown of what the role actually involves and how it works day to day — so you can see whether it's a fit before we get into the specifics.

The idea

Why we work with ambassadors at all

Ted's Health grows through people whose audience already trusts them on health and performance — not through generic ads. An ambassador is someone who takes the test themselves, has a genuine result or experience worth talking about, and is comfortable sharing that with their audience in their own voice. We're not looking for a script read out loud — we're looking for real people who found it useful.

What it involves

What we're actually asking for

  • Take the test yourself and have a genuine experience worth talking about — good or middling, we're not asking you to oversell it.
  • One piece of content to start — a post, story or short video about your results/experience, in your own words.
  • Share a code with your audience in whatever way fits how you normally post.
  • Stay in touch periodically — a re-share or mention every so often, not a one-and-done. This is an ongoing relationship, not a single transaction.
  • Represent it accurately — your own experience only, no medical claims made on our behalf.
How it works

Day to day, what this actually looks like

1
You go through the test

Same process any client goes through — blood test, then a consultation to talk through your results.

2
You share your take

Once you've had the results and consultation, you post about it in your own voice — no script, no brand-speak.

3
Light-touch agreement

A short written agreement covering what's expected on both sides and how long it runs — nothing heavy, no exclusivity unless we specifically agree one.

4
Ongoing, not one-off

You keep it in rotation over time rather than a single post and done — and we're on hand for anything you need along the way.

What this isn't

It's not a paid influencer contract with fixed deliverables and a monthly invoice — it works differently to that.

It's not a medical endorsement — you're speaking to your own experience, not making claims on our behalf. Our clinical team owns everything clinical.

It's not exclusive by default — if that ever becomes relevant for either side, we'd talk about it separately.

Any questions
